🇸🇬 The Lion City: How Singapore Built a First-World Nation from Nothing

A Long-Form Report on the Most Remarkable Story in Modern Nation-Building

On August 9, 1965, Lee Kuan Yew sat before the cameras of Singapore’s television network and wept.

He was 41 years old. He had just read out a proclamation of independence. His country — if one could call it that — had not won independence. It had been expelled from Malaysia. Thrown out. Told it was too Chinese, too troublesome, too different. And so a city-state of fewer than two million people found itself, on a Tuesday morning, suddenly alone on the world’s map, sovereign over 728 square kilometres of swamp, harbour, and shoebox apartments, with no army, no water supply of its own, no natural resources, surrounded by neighbours who did not necessarily wish it well.

“For me,” Lee said later, “it was a moment of anguish.”

What happened next is one of the most extraordinary stories in the history of human civilization.

CHAPTER 1 — The Impossible Nation: Singapore Before Independence

A Dot on the Map

Look at a map of Southeast Asia. At the very tip of the Malay Peninsula — the finger of land that reaches down from mainland Asia into the Java Sea — there is a small island, barely larger than Greater London, separated from the Malaysian mainland by a strait so narrow you can see across it on a clear day. This was Singapore before modernity: a few fishing villages, some mangrove swamps, the occasional Malay orang laut (sea people) settlement, and absolutely nothing else that the world cared about.

Then, in January 1819, a 37-year-old British colonial administrator named Sir Thomas Stamford Raffles sailed into Singapore’s harbour and changed the course of Asian history.

Raffles was brilliant, ambitious, and possessed of a trader’s instinct for geography. He understood something that the Dutch — Britain’s main commercial rival in Southeast Asia — did not: that the future of Asian trade would flow through whichever power controlled the shipping lanes between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea. The Strait of Malacca, through which ships must pass to travel between Europe and East Asia, was the most strategically important waterway in the world. Singapore sat at its southern end like a gatekeeper.

The island’s sultan was in a succession dispute. Raffles exploited it with characteristic colonial aplomb, signed a treaty, raised the Union Jack, and declared Singapore a free port — meaning no duties, no tariffs, no barriers to trade. Within months, ships were arriving from China, India, Sumatra, and beyond. Within six years, Singapore’s trade volume exceeded that of Penang, the British East India Company’s existing regional hub. Within a generation, it had become one of the most important ports in the world.

That single decision — free port — became Singapore’s economic DNA. Two centuries later, that instinct has never left. Singapore today remains one of the most open economies on the planet, a shrine to the idea that a small place with no resources can grow fabulously wealthy by making itself indispensable to global commerce.

The Making of a Multiracial City

Colonial Singapore was a polyglot, chaotic, extraordinarily energetic place. The British needed labour for their port and plantation economy. So workers came — by the millions, across decades. Chinese coolies poured in from Fujian and Guangdong provinces, working the docks and building the roads. Indian labourers came from Tamil Nadu, laying railways and tapping rubber trees. Malay fishermen and farmers from the peninsula and nearby islands formed the indigenous community. And the British themselves — merchants, soldiers, administrators — perched at the top of a rigidly stratified racial hierarchy.

By the early 20th century, Singapore was a city of staggering ethnic complexity: Hokkien and Teochew and Cantonese-speaking Chinese communities, each fiercely distinct from the others; Tamil, Telugu, and Punjabi Indians; Javanese and Boyanese and Bugis Malays; Straits-born Chinese (Peranakans) who spoke Malay and considered themselves culturally unique; and a small but powerful European business elite. These communities lived side by side but largely separately — the British had little interest in national cohesion and every interest in keeping the communities divided and manageable.

The rubber and tin boom of the early 20th century made Singapore one of the wealthiest cities in Asia by the 1920s. It also made the British complacent.

The Fall: February 1942

On February 15, 1942, British Lieutenant-General Arthur Percival surrendered Singapore to Japanese General Tomoyuki Yamashita in what Winston Churchill called “the worst disaster and largest capitulation in British history.” Seventy thousand British, Australian, and Indian troops laid down their arms. The “impregnable fortress” — which had its great guns pointing seaward, toward a naval attack that never came, while the Japanese army simply bicycled down through the Malay Peninsula and attacked from the north — had fallen in 70 days.

For Singaporeans, especially the young men who witnessed it, the fall was an education more valuable than anything the colonial schools ever taught. It proved, beyond argument, that the Europeans were not invincible. That colonial protection was an illusion. That a small, dependent people had better learn to protect itself — because no one else would.

Among those young men watching was a 18-year-old Cambridge-bound Straits Chinese student named Lee Kuan Yew. He would never forget the lesson.

The Japanese occupation (1942–45) was brutal. The Chinese community suffered most — the Japanese army conducted systematic massacres known as Sook Ching (purging through cleansing), killing somewhere between 25,000 and 50,000 ethnic Chinese suspected of anti-Japanese activity. Food was scarce. Inflation was rampant. The social order dissolved. Lee Kuan Yew survived by selling tapioca and later working for the Japanese propaganda department, learning Japanese — and watching very carefully how power actually worked.

The Road to Independence

After British rule resumed in 1945, nothing was quite the same. The colonial spell was broken. Labour unions swelled. Communist movements, energized by their wartime resistance roles, attracted tens of thousands of working-class followers. A new generation of English-educated, Cambridge-returned politicians — including the young lawyer Lee Kuan Yew — was determined to achieve self-governance.

The story of the 1950s and early 1960s is one of extraordinary political complexity: LKY’s People’s Action Party (PAP), formed in 1954, was a radical left-leaning coalition that deliberately included pro-communist trade unionists as a mass base. LKY needed them to win elections. They needed him for his legal brain and his English-language legitimacy. It was an alliance of mutual necessity and mutual distrust, and everyone knew it would eventually break.

Singapore achieved self-governance in 1959. LKY became its first Prime Minister. Then came a fateful gamble: in 1963, Singapore merged with the Federation of Malaysia, which had just achieved independence from Britain. The calculation made sense on paper — Malaysia provided the hinterland, the water, the economic integration that tiny Singapore needed. But the merger was plagued from the start. Racial riots erupted between Chinese and Malay communities in 1964. Malaysian Prime Minister Tunku Abdul Rahman did not trust LKY’s political ambitions. Relations corroded.

On August 9, 1965, Tunku made an announcement. Singapore was out.

The Weight of That Morning

Lee Kuan Yew called an emergency press conference. He had been up all night. His voice, normally so controlled, cracked. He wept openly. “Every time we look back on this moment when we signed this document,” he said, “we will be sad… I have no regrets. My whole adult life I have sought merger and the unity of these two territories.”

What he did not say — but what he knew — was the magnitude of the crisis. Singapore had no army. The British bases, which accounted for roughly 30% of Singapore’s GDP, were scheduled to close. The city had no natural freshwater — its entire supply came through a pipeline from Malaysia, a neighbour that now had every reason to be hostile. There was no manufacturing, no industry to speak of, chronic unemployment, and a population of 1.9 million people of three major ethnic communities who had recently been rioting against each other.

Every development economist who looked at Singapore in 1965 would have made the same assessment: this is not going to work.

They were all wrong.

CHAPTER 2 — Lee Kuan Yew: The Man Who Built a Nation

A Portrait in Complexity

There is a famous photograph of Lee Kuan Yew, taken in the 1970s, at the height of his powers. He is lean, almost severe, in an open-collar shirt — Singapore’s Prime Ministers did not wear ties, a deliberate rejection of colonial formality. His eyes are sharp, watchful, analytical. He looks like a man who has never stopped calculating, even for a moment.

Lee Kuan Yew (1923–2015) is the most consequential city-state builder since Pericles of Athens and Augustus Caesar of Rome. That is not hyperbole. It is simply what the evidence shows. He took a city with every structural disadvantage imaginable and turned it into a nation with a per-capita GDP exceeding that of the United States. He did it in a single generation. And he did it his way — which was frequently not the world’s preferred way, but which worked.

Origins: A Peranakan Boy with a Cambridge Mind

Lee was born into a Peranakan (Straits Chinese) family — families who had been in the Malay archipelago for generations and had adopted local customs, language, and food while remaining ethnically Chinese. They were the colonial elite among the Chinese community. His grandfather began his British education, his father continued it, and Lee took it to its apex — he was one of the most brilliant students of his generation, studying law at Cambridge and the London School of Economics, where he graduated with a Double First.

The Japanese occupation interrupted his studies and changed his thinking permanently. Watching the British authorities crumble, watching the Japanese rule through fear and violence, watching how rapidly order collapsed and chaos filled the vacuum — these experiences convinced Lee of something he would carry for the rest of his life: the world is a Darwinian place, and only hard, unsentimental competence keeps a small nation alive.

Back in Singapore after the war, he became a trade union lawyer — not out of socialist conviction, he would later say, but out of strategic calculation. The unions were the mass movement. If he wanted power, he needed to be where the people were.

The Political Genius

Lee’s rise to power in the 1950s was a masterclass in political judo. The PAP he co-founded in 1954 was a coalition of English-educated moderates like himself and Chinese-educated pro-communist trade unionists. His strategy was to use the communists’ mass base to win elections, while maneuvering to ensure they never actually gained control. It was extraordinarily dangerous — one slip and the communists, who had the numbers, would have devoured him.

He played it perfectly. In 1963, working with the Malaysian government, he authorized the detention of his former pro-communist PAP colleagues under the Internal Security Act. Operation Cold Store rounded up more than 100 left-wing politicians and activists. They were held without trial. It was authoritarian. It was effective. And it showed, early, that Lee Kuan Yew was a man who would do what he believed necessary regardless of how it looked.

After independence, with the communist threat neutralized, Lee built the PAP into something unprecedented in post-colonial history: a governing party that was genuinely meritocratic, nearly incorruptible, and ruthlessly focused on results. In a region where post-colonial governments almost invariably descended into patronage politics, ethnic favoritism, and kleptocracy, the PAP was a genuine anomaly.

The Philosophy: Pragmatism as Religion

“We are ideology-free,” Lee Kuan Yew once said. “What works, we do.”

This sounds simple. It was revolutionary. In the 1960s and 1970s, ideological rigidity was the norm in developing-world politics. You were either a capitalist or a socialist. You embraced the American model or the Soviet model or the Maoist model. Lee rejected all of it. He took what worked from everywhere.

From the British colonial administration, he took the Common Law legal system, administrative structure, and the English language. From free-market economics, he took openness to foreign investment and global trade. From the German model, he took vocational training and technical education. From Israel, he took the idea of a small, professional national army built on universal conscription. From Wikileaks — well, Lee was running his own version of radical transparency decades before that: he published the salaries of civil servants and ministers, not to embarrass them, but to justify paying them competitive rates.

His three governing pillars were:

1. Meritocracy — advancement based purely on ability and performance, regardless of race, religion, or family connections. This was simultaneously a moral commitment and a practical necessity: in a tiny country with no natural resources, the only resource was human talent, and it had to be deployed as efficiently as possible.

2. Multiracialism — the radical insistence, against every historical trend in Southeast Asia, that Singapore’s Chinese majority would not and could not simply dominate the Malay and Indian minorities. Four official languages. Four official holidays. One national anthem sung by all.

3. Incorruptibility — the highest possible priority given to eliminating corruption, not through moral exhortation but through structure: high civil service salaries, severe penalties, independent anti-corruption agency with genuinely teeth-bearing powers.

The Authoritarian Question

This is where any honest account of Lee Kuan Yew must sit uncomfortably for a moment.

Lee used the Internal Security Act to detain political opponents without trial — sometimes for years, even decades. He sued political opponents and international newspapers for defamation, and he won, systematically bankrupting critics and driving opposition politicians into exile or poverty. He controlled Singapore’s press. He introduced the chewing gum ban (to keep the MRT stations clean). He penalized littering with fines steep enough to be genuinely punitive. He oversaw a state where homosexuality remained criminal. He told Singaporeans that their children must speak Mandarin rather than their ancestral Chinese dialects, and enforced it.

Was this authoritarianism or tough love? Was Singapore successful because of these controls, or in spite of them?

The honest answer is: both, probably, and the proportions are unknowable. What we can say is this: Lee operated from a coherent theory. He genuinely believed that a small, multiracial state at an early stage of development could not afford the luxury of Western liberal democracy’s full permission structures — that social cohesion, institutional trust, and economic development needed to be enforced before they could be genuinely internalized. He compared it to training a plant to grow straight: “You have to stake it. You can’t just let it grow wild and hope it turns out right.”

His critics — and they are serious, not merely sour — respond that this logic can justify almost any repression and that it conveniently converged with the interests of Lee Kuan Yew’s own continued power. The Chinese activist Chia Thye Poh spent 22 years in detention without trial. Journalist Said Zahari spent 17 years. These are not statistics. They were human beings.

Lee Kuan Yew’s Singapore was not a gentle place. It was a place that worked.

The Woman Behind the Man

Kwa Geok Choo, Lee’s wife of 60 years, was by every account his intellectual equal and his most trusted advisor. She was also a Cambridge-trained lawyer who had scored higher than her husband in their Bar exams — a fact Lee acknowledged publicly and with what seemed like genuine pride. She was quiet, fiercely private, and kept entirely out of political life in her public persona while remaining, those who knew the family said, entirely involved in private.

She suffered a series of strokes beginning in 2003. When she died in October 2010, Lee Kuan Yew was visibly diminished. He published a book about her two years later — Lee Kuan Yew: Hard Truths to Keep Singapore Going — in which he wrote that he recited a poem to her every night while she was ill. The hardest man in Asian politics admitted he had lost the one person who had made him fully human.

Lee Kuan Yew died on March 23, 2015, at age 91. Singapore declared a week of national mourning. About 450,000 people queued — some for ten hours — to pay their respects.

CHAPTER 3 — Building the Foundation: The First Decade of Survival (1965–1975)

Crisis All at Once

Imagine inheriting a company that is simultaneously facing bankruptcy, a hostile takeover, a factory fire, a labour strike, and a civil war — and imagine that the company has 1.9 million employees who are depending on you for their next meal. That was Singapore in August 1965.

The British had announced they would withdraw their military bases by 1971. These bases — HMS Terror, RAF Changi, the Sembawang Dockyard — employed 25,000 Singaporeans directly and supported perhaps 40,000 more through ancillary businesses. They accounted for an estimated 30% of Singapore’s GDP. Their departure was not a problem to be managed. It was an existential emergency.

Unemployment was at 14% and rising. Most Singaporeans lived in cramped shophouses or kampong (village) settlements without clean water or proper sanitation. The island had no manufacturing, barely any industry. The only legitimate economic activities were port services and entrepôt trade.

Ethnic tensions were simmering after the 1964 race riots. The Malay community felt economically marginalised. The Chinese community was politically divided between PAP moderates and pro-communist factions. The Indian community was small enough to feel forgotten. Race was a powder keg.

And Singapore had no army whatsoever.

The Survival Team

Lee Kuan Yew was lucky in one crucial respect: he surrounded himself with extraordinary people.

Goh Keng Swee, the Finance Minister and later Minister for Defence, was the economic architect — a man of complete intellectual honesty, steel nerve, and zero sentimentality. He had a Ph.D. in economics from the London School of Economics, but his real expertise was institutional: he understood how to design government agencies that actually worked in the real world.

S. Rajaratnam, the Foreign Minister, was the ideological visionary — a Tamil journalist from Sri Lanka who became one of the most eloquent articulators of Singapore’s national identity. It was Rajaratnam who developed the idea of Singapore as a “global city,” a state that would belong not to one ethnic group or region but to the world.

Hon Sui Sen ran the Economic Development Board and later the Finance Ministry — a steady, methodical administrator who made the machinery of economic development actually function.

Together, these men — along with a remarkable civil service built on rigorous meritocratic examination — constituted the most effective government team in post-colonial Asia.

The EDB: Singapore’s Secret Weapon

The Economic Development Board, established in 1961 and rebooted after independence, was Lee and Goh’s most important institutional creation. In essence, it was a one-stop shop for foreign investors — a government agency that could cut through bureaucratic red tape, offer tax incentives, allocate land, and provide infrastructure, all within one coordinated framework.

The EDB’s pitch to multinational corporations was radical for its time: Come to Singapore. We guarantee you a stable, strike-free labour force, zero corruption in government dealings, world-class port access, and a legal system based on English Common Law. In return, you will employ our people, transfer technology, and help us build an industrial base.

In the late 1960s, this pitch was received with deep skepticism in corporate boardrooms in New York and Geneva. Why would Texas Instruments build a semiconductor factory in a swampy former British colony with no industrial history?

Because Goh Keng Swee flew to their headquarters and made the case personally. Because Singapore had just passed the Industrial Relations Act, which restricted workers’ rights to strike in ways that made foreign executives nervous about labour relations across the region. Because the alternative — nationalist industrialization behind tariff walls, which was the standard post-colonial playbook — was already visibly failing across Asia, Africa, and Latin America.

By 1972, Texas Instruments was operating in Singapore. Hewlett-Packard followed. Then General Electric, Rollei, Philips, and dozens of others. Jurong Industrial Estate — literally reclaimed from swampland in the early 1960s, a massive infrastructure gamble that Goh insisted on when everyone else thought it was lunacy — began to hum with factories.

The HDB: Nation-Building Through Housing

If the EDB was Singapore’s economic machine, the Housing Development Board was its social machine — and arguably the more profound achievement.

In 1960, when the HDB was established, 70% of Singapore’s population lived in overcrowded slums and kampong settlements — conditions of genuine squalor, without clean water, proper sanitation, or fire safety. The HDB’s mandate was to change this. Within a decade, it had built more than 100,000 apartments. Within twenty years, it had housed more than 80% of the population in public housing that was affordable (subsidized, purchased on long mortgages), well-maintained, and integrated.

That last word — integrated — is the key. The HDB did not merely build apartments. It enforced a radical social experiment: ethnic quotas in every housing block. No block could be more than a certain percentage Chinese, Malay, or Indian. This broke apart the traditional ethnic enclaves that had characterized colonial Singapore — Chinatown, Little India, Kampong Gelam — and forced different communities to share lifts, staircases, and void decks.

The results, over time, were remarkable. Children of different ethnicities grew up in the same blocks, attended the same schools, played at the same playgrounds. Interracial friendships formed where the colonial city had produced interracial suspicion. The HDB became, slowly, the physical infrastructure of a multiracial national identity.

The world took note. Singapore’s public housing system is today studied as a masterclass in urban policy. Over 80% of Singaporeans own (technically, hold long-term leases on) their HDB apartments — giving the vast majority of citizens a tangible, financial stake in national stability. As LKY put it: “If every man has his home, he has a stake in the country.”

Building an Army from Nothing

With characteristic practicality, Singapore turned to an unlikely mentor for its national defence: Israel.

The Israeli military — battle-hardened, professionally excellent, and experienced at building a small-nation defence force against larger hostile neighbours — sent a secret advisory mission to Singapore in 1965 (secret because Singapore’s Malay and Muslim neighbours would have been outraged by an open Israeli presence). The Israelis helped design the Singapore Armed Forces’ structure, officer training programmes, and conscription system.

National Service — two years of compulsory military service for all male citizens — began in 1967. It was not merely a military programme. It was a social one. Men of all ethnicities — Chinese, Malay, Indian — trained together, sweated together, were commanded together. The SAF (Singapore Armed Forces) became, alongside the HDB, one of the two great institutions of racial integration.

Today, the SAF operates F-35 fighter jets, Leopard tanks, a submarine fleet, and one of the most technologically sophisticated militaries in the region — for a country of 5 million people. When LKY said he wanted Singapore to be a “poisonous shrimp” — too dangerous to eat even for larger predators — he meant this literally.

CHAPTER 4 — The Architects of Prosperity: Goh Keng Swee and the Economic Blueprint

The Man Who Designed the Engine

Goh Keng Swee (1918–2010) is perhaps the most underrated economic policymaker of the 20th century. While Lee Kuan Yew got the headlines, Goh designed the machinery. He was, in the words of economic historian Thomas Bellows, “the architect behind the architect.” Where Lee was charismatic and visionary, Goh was empirical and unsentimental. Where Lee gave the speeches, Goh wrote the memos that actually built the country.

His core strategic insight was breathtaking in its clarity: Singapore has no resources, no domestic market, and no hinterland. Therefore, it cannot afford a single inefficient institution. Everything must work. Everything.

This is easy to say. It is almost impossible to execute. What Goh understood, and what made him exceptional, was that the problem was not principally economic — it was institutional. Bad governance produced bad economics. If you fixed the governance — eliminated corruption, paid civil servants properly, created rational bureaucratic systems — the economic results would follow.

The Central Provident Fund: A Swiss Army Knife

Of all the policy instruments in Singapore’s arsenal, the Central Provident Fund (CPF) is perhaps the single most elegant. It is one of those rare institutional designs that solves multiple problems simultaneously without creating new ones.

The CPF is a compulsory savings scheme. Every working Singaporean contributes a percentage of their salary (currently around 37% total — employee and employer combined) to a personal CPF account. The contributions are split into three sub-accounts: the Ordinary Account (for housing, education, and investment), the Special Account (for retirement), and the MediSave Account (for healthcare).

Think about what this accomplishes in one stroke:

  • It creates a national retirement savings system without the government needing to run a pension fund
  • It provides housing finance — Singaporeans use their CPF Ordinary Account to pay HDB mortgage instalments, meaning they build home equity and retirement savings simultaneously
  • It funds national capital formation — the government can invest the CPF pool (placed with statutory boards) into national infrastructure
  • It creates individual stakes in economic stability — when the economy does well, your CPF does well

The CPF was not just a welfare policy. It was a macroeconomic tool. By forcing savings at very high rates, Singapore accumulated the domestic capital needed for infrastructure investment without relying on foreign borrowing. In a single elegant instrument, it solved the perennial developing-world problem of how to finance national development without accumulating crippling debt.

State Enterprises That Actually Competed

Across the developing world, state-owned enterprises became synonymous with inefficiency, corruption, and perpetual subsidy requirements. Singapore did something almost no other country managed: it created state-owned enterprises that were genuinely world-class competitors.

Singapore Airlines was formed in 1972 from the split of Malaysia-Singapore Airlines. From the beginning, it was run on strictly commercial principles. It received no ongoing government subsidies. It was expected to compete — and win — against the best airlines in the world. It did. Singapore Airlines has won the Skytrax Award for World’s Best Airline more times than any other carrier. Its brand, its service quality, and its management excellence are genuine achievements. The secret? Goh Keng Swee insisted from the start that “government-linked” would not mean “government-subsidized.” The company lived or died on its commercial performance.

The Port of Singapore Authority (PSA) applied the same logic to port operations. Singapore’s port had a geographic advantage — it sat astride the Malacca Strait — but geographic advantage is worthless if the port is slow, corrupt, or bureaucratic. Singapore invested relentlessly in efficiency: new cranes, automated systems, 24-hour operations, streamlined customs. By the 1990s, it had become the world’s busiest container port. It held that title for many years.

Singapore Telecommunications, the Development Bank of Singapore (DBS), Keppel Corporation: the pattern repeated. State ownership as a starting mechanism, then genuine commercial discipline as the operating principle.

Paying Civil Servants What They Are Worth

In 1972, the government of Singapore did something that caused international outrage: it dramatically raised the salaries of civil servants — including ministers — to match private-sector levels.

The logic was simple and iron-clad. If you pay your best people $30,000 a year to run the country when they could earn $300,000 in the private sector, one of two things happens: either you don’t attract the best people, or the best people find unofficial ways to supplement their income. Both outcomes are catastrophic.

Singapore chose a third path: pay them $300,000 (eventually more — the Prime Minister of Singapore earns more than the US President) and demand performance. The result was a civil service of genuinely exceptional quality — one that consistently ranks among the world’s most efficient, least corrupt, and most competent in international surveys.

Critics called it outrageous. LKY called it insurance. “Either you pay them right,” he said, “or you pay more to clean up the mess.”

CHAPTER 5 — The Financial Hub: How Singapore Became the Switzerland of Asia

The Pivot to Finance

By the late 1960s, Singapore’s manufacturing strategy was working. Factories were opening, employment was rising, GDP was growing. But Lee and Goh were already thinking ahead. Manufacturing was good — but it tied a country to labour costs, and Singapore’s labour costs would inevitably rise as the economy developed. The long-term competitive advantage had to be something more durable.

Finance was the answer.

In 1968, Singapore made a bold move: it created the Asian Dollar Market — an offshore market for US dollar deposits, modelled on the Eurodollar market that had developed in London in the 1960s. The timing was crucial. The post-war Bretton Woods financial system was straining under the pressures of American trade deficits and global capital seeking returns outside domestic banking systems. Singapore positioned itself to capture the overflow.

It worked with startling speed. By creating a tax-friendly, regulation-light environment for offshore dollar transactions, Singapore attracted banks seeking a financial hub in the Asian time zone — neither London nor New York was convenient for Asian business hours. By the 1970s, major global banks had regional headquarters in Singapore. By the 1980s, Singapore was Asia’s leading foreign exchange trading centre.

The MAS: A Central Bank Unlike Any Other

The Monetary Authority of Singapore, established in 1971, is one of the world’s most unusual central banks. It is extremely powerful — combining the functions of central bank, financial regulatory authority, and prudential supervisor in one institution — and it operates with a monetary policy logic found almost nowhere else in the world.

Most central banks manage their economies by setting interest rates. The MAS does not. It manages the exchange rate of the Singapore dollar. By allowing the Singapore dollar to gradually appreciate against the currencies of its trading partners, the MAS keeps import prices (and thus inflation) controlled, and forces Singapore’s domestic industry to continuously upgrade and move up the value chain — because you can’t compete on cheap currency if your currency is always rising.

It is a sophisticated and counter-intuitive policy that requires deep analytical capability and institutional confidence to execute correctly. The MAS has, over decades, managed it with near-flawless precision. Singapore’s inflation has remained low, its currency stable, and its financial system has survived every major global crisis — the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis, the 2008 Global Financial Crisis — without requiring a government bailout of its banking sector.

The Wealth Management Revolution

In the 1990s and 2000s, Singapore built a second element of its financial strategy: wealth management. As Asian economies boomed, a vast new class of wealthy individuals, family offices, and institutional investors emerged across the region. They needed somewhere to hold their wealth: somewhere safe, legally predictable, politically stable, and discreet.

Singapore offered all four. Its legal system, based on English Common Law, was taken seriously by international investors. Its courts were independent and efficient. Its tax regime was generous to foreign investors. Its banking secrecy laws, while less absolute than Switzerland’s, were strong enough to attract private capital.

Today, Singapore manages an estimated $5 trillion in assets — a figure that, for a country of 5 million people, is almost incomprehensible. Imagine the entire GDP of Germany and France combined, managed by a city roughly the size of Coventry. This is Singapore’s financial reality.

Temasek and GIC: Sovereign Wealth as Strategic Power

Singapore’s government earns money. A lot of it. The question of what to do with accumulated national savings led to the creation of two of the world’s most important institutional investors:

Temasek Holdings (established 1974) manages a portfolio of about $300 billion, primarily through direct equity investments in companies — originally Singapore’s state enterprises, now a global portfolio spanning technology, finance, life sciences, and consumer businesses.

GIC (Government of Singapore Investment Corporation, established 1981) manages Singapore’s foreign exchange reserves — officially stated to be “well above” $100 billion, with most estimates placing it between $650 billion and $800 billion. GIC invests globally, across asset classes, with a mandate of preservation and medium-term real returns.

Together, Temasek and GIC make Singapore one of the world’s most powerful sovereign investors. They are not just financial institutions. They are tools of national strategy — they give Singapore influence far beyond what its size would suggest, and they provide a cushion that allows Singapore to weather economic storms that devastate less prudent neighbours.

CHAPTER 6 — Education, Meritocracy, and Human Capital

The Greatest Natural Resource

In his first years as Prime Minister, Lee Kuan Yew used to say: “We have nothing. We produce nothing. The only thing we can sell to the world is ourselves — our intelligence, our reliability, our skills.” This was not merely rhetoric. It was an operational policy directive.

Singapore’s education system transformation is one of the most ambitious social engineering projects in modern history. In 1965, Singapore had a patchwork of colonial-era schools — Chinese-medium schools, Tamil-medium schools, Malay-medium schools, English-medium schools — operating under different curricula, different languages of instruction, different cultural assumptions. They produced graduates who could not communicate with each other.

The government systematically unified this system around English as the medium of instruction for all subjects (science, mathematics, social sciences), while mandating the teaching of each student’s “mother tongue” — Mandarin for Chinese, Tamil for Indians, Malay for Malays — as a second language. This was not pluralism; it was a managed policy that prioritised economic utility (English opened global opportunities) while preserving cultural identity (mother tongue maintained community roots).

The closure of Nanyang University — Singapore’s only Chinese-medium university, a cultural institution of deep emotional significance to the Chinese-educated community — in 1980 and its merger with the University of Singapore to form the National University of Singapore was the most painful moment in this process. Thousands of alumni mourned. Lee Kuan Yew pressed forward. The logic: in a globalised world, Chinese-medium graduates would be systematically disadvantaged in the labour market. The kindest thing was brutal clarity, not sentimental preservation.

The NUS and NTU Rise to the World Stage

The National University of Singapore today ranks in the top 10–15 universities globally in multiple independent rankings. This is extraordinary for a university that was unremarkable as recently as the 1980s, in a country that had no university tradition before independence.

The rise happened through relentless investment, aggressive recruitment of global academic talent, and a willingness to benchmark ruthlessly against the best in the world rather than against regional competitors. NUS partnered with Stanford, MIT, Duke, and other world-class institutions. NTU (Nanyang Technological University) built the most beautiful new university campus in Asia and became a genuine powerhouse in engineering, computer science, and materials science.

The result: Singaporean graduates in the 21st century are among the most sought-after in global engineering, finance, and technology firms. The country that had no universities in 1965 now exports intellectual capital to the world.

The Streaming Controversy

Singapore’s education system sorts students by academic performance — streams them — at multiple points in their schooling career. High performers go to elite schools and academically rigorous tracks. Lower performers enter vocational and technical streams.

The system is efficient. It maximises national output from the student population. High-ability students are pushed appropriately; vocational streams are genuinely respected and well-funded, not second-rate dumping grounds.

But it has a profound moral tension: it entrenches social destiny remarkably early. A child’s performance at age 12 can determine their life trajectory. Affluent families spend enormous sums on tutoring and enrichment to ensure their children perform well. The system rewards those whose parents can invest in their performance — which means it risks being less of a meritocracy and more of a well-disguised aristocracy of preparation.

Singapore has partially acknowledged this tension and reformed the system repeatedly, most recently abolishing mid-year examinations and standardised streaming names. The debate continues about whether Singapore’s education system is the world’s best — or the world’s most pressurised.

The Meritocracy Promise and Its Discontents

The meritocracy contract that Singapore offers its citizens is powerful: it does not matter who you are or who your parents are. If you perform, you rise. This promise has been largely kept. Singapore’s government is genuinely filled with people of non-elite backgrounds who rose through educational performance. Its military, civil service, and state enterprises are led by people chosen for competence, not family connection.

But the meritocracy promise contains a hidden sting: if everyone who fails genuinely had equal opportunity, then failure is purely one’s own fault. This can produce a society that is simultaneously dynamic and cold — high-achieving and high-anxiety, with little tolerance for those who fall behind.

Singapore’s suicide rate among the elderly is abnormally high. Mental health issues among students are a growing public concern. The drive to succeed is programmed deep.

CHAPTER 7 — Racial Harmony by Design: Engineering a Multiracial Society

The Bloody Backdrop

On July 21, 1964 — the Prophet Muhammad’s birthday — a Malay procession of 20,000 was passing through a Chinese neighbourhood in Singapore when violence broke out. By the time it was over, 23 people were dead and hundreds injured. A second round of riots followed in September. Images of burning streets and armed soldiers were broadcast around the region.

Then, in May 1969, race riots erupted in Kuala Lumpur and spread to Singapore. More deaths. More fires. These were not random outbreaks of mob violence. They were the consequence of specific historical resentments, economic inequalities, and political manipulation. They proved, viscerally, that ethnic harmony in a multiracial state did not happen naturally. It had to be actively constructed.

Lee Kuan Yew drew two lessons. First: ethnic enclaves, where single communities cluster and reinforce each other’s grievances, are dangerous. Therefore, the HDB ethnic quotas — enforced mixing in residential buildings. Second: political systems that allow ethnic majoritarianism — where the Chinese majority could vote as a bloc and dominate — were destabilizing. Therefore, structural solutions to ensure minority representation.

Engineering Harmony

Singapore’s approach to racial harmony is, in many ways, the most intellectually interesting aspect of its governance. Rather than hoping that diversity would naturally produce harmony — or that the majority’s goodwill would protect minorities — Singapore built structural guarantees.

The HDB ethnic integration policy has already been discussed: each residential block must maintain proportions roughly matching the national average. This is legally enforced. You cannot sell your HDB flat to someone of a different ethnicity if doing so would breach the quota of your block. Critics call this social engineering. Defenders call it the only thing that stands between Singapore and communal conflict.

The Group Representation Constituency (GRC) system is another structural device: rather than single-member parliamentary constituencies, Singapore has multi-member constituencies where each slate of candidates must include at least one from a minority ethnic group. This constitutionally guarantees minority representation in Parliament, even if minority communities are geographically dispersed.

The four official languages — English, Mandarin, Malay, and Tamil — and the explicit recognition of the four major communities’ cultural festivals as public holidays (Chinese New Year, Hari Raya, Deepavali, Christmas) created a symbolic architecture of inclusion that quietly trained Singaporeans to understand that the country belonged to all of them.

The Honest Limits

Is this harmony real? Yes and no. Surveys consistently show that Singaporeans of all ethnicities have genuine friendships across racial lines — far more so than comparable multiethnic societies elsewhere. The daily experience of shared schools, shared housing blocks, and shared National Service has produced a genuine sense of common identity.

But tensions persist beneath the surface. The Malay community, despite being the indigenous people of the region, remains statistically economically behind the Chinese community, and there is debate about structural barriers. Until very recently, Malays were excluded from certain sensitive roles in the military, though the official policy of non-discrimination existed on paper — a wound that many Malay Singaporeans have felt deeply.

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed another fault line: anti-Chinese sentiment when COVID was initially associated with China, and anti-South Asian sentiment when subsequent waves were traced to migrant worker dormitories. Singapore’s harmony is real and it is maintained — but it requires continuous maintenance, not complacent assumption.

CHAPTER 8 — From LKY to the Fourth Generation: Consolidation and Reinvention

Goh Chok Tong: The Liberalizer

When Lee Kuan Yew stepped down as Prime Minister in 1990, handing over to Goh Chok Tong, many expected the arrangement to be cosmetic — LKY would continue to govern through his successor. In fact, Goh was more independent than this narrative allows.

Goh’s Singapore was measurably more open than his predecessor’s. He tolerated a wider range of public discussion, pulled back (slightly) on some of the PAP’s more controlling social policies, and introduced the concept of Singapore as a “gracious society” — a place that valued not just economic performance but quality of life.

His most severe test came in 1997, when the Asian Financial Crisis swept through the region like a financial tsunami. Thailand, Indonesia, South Korea, and Malaysia all suffered devastating currency collapses and economic contractions. Indonesia’s crisis led to the fall of the Suharto government after 32 years. Singapore was hit — its economy contracted sharply — but it survived to stand largely unscathed compared to its neighbours, a consequence of its sound fundamentals, accumulated reserves, and the MAS’s exchange-rate policy that had kept the Singapore dollar from the speculative excess that devastated others.

Lee Hsien Loong: The Technocrat-Politician

Lee Hsien Loong, LKY’s eldest son, became Prime Minister in 2004 and held the office until 2024 — a 20-year tenure that he used to reinvent Singapore’s economic model for the knowledge economy.

His inheritance was a Singapore where the old formula — attract MNCs with cheap, skilled labour and excellent logistics — was becoming less tenable as wages rose and China offered the same proposition at lower cost. Lee Hsien Loong’s response was to push Singapore aggressively up the value chain:

Biomedical Sciences became a new pillar of the economy from the early 2000s. Singapore built Biopolis — a purpose-designed research campus — and recruited world-class pharmaceutical companies and research institutes.

The Integrated Resorts — Singapore’s euphemism for casinos — were, perhaps, the most politically courageous decision of his tenure. Casino gambling was long seen as socially harmful and culturally inappropriate (Singapore had banned casinos since 1966). Lee opened two in 2010. Marina Bay Sands (the building with the famous infinity pool balancing on top of three towers) and Resorts World Sentosa transformed Singapore’s tourism economy and generated revenues that exceeded expectations. This was not a betrayal of values — it was the values in action: pragmatism over ideology.

Smart Nation — launched in 2014 — was Singapore’s bid to become the world’s first fully integrated digital society. Digital identity for every citizen. Nationwide e-government services. AI-powered urban management (traffic lights, waste collection, public safety). A national digital payments infrastructure that made cash nearly obsolete. Singapore today is the most digitally governed nation in the world.

COVID-19 tested Lee Hsien Loong’s government severely. The initial response was praised globally. Then the pandemic swept through migrant worker dormitories — housing hundreds of thousands of low-wage workers in conditions that the pandemic exposed as inadequate — creating a moral reckoning about whose “Singapore” the Smart Nation project really served.

Lawrence Wong and the Fourth Generation

In May 2024, Lawrence Wong became Singapore’s fourth Prime Minister, representing a genuine generational transition. Wong is a technocrat who worked his way up through the civil service and politics — no dynastic connection, no military pedigree. He is economically sophisticated, personally approachable, and demonstrated his crisis management capabilities as co-chair of Singapore’s COVID-19 multi-ministry task force.

His challenge is the most interesting: to sustain Singapore’s success without any of the founding generation’s clarity of crisis purpose. When you have already built the first-world oasis, what do you build next?

CHAPTER 9 — The Singapore Model: What the World Can Learn

Eight Lessons That Transcend Geography

Singapore’s success was shaped by unique factors — its geography at the Malacca Strait, its particular historical moment, one extraordinary founding generation — that cannot be replicated wholesale. But embedded within the Singapore story are insights that are genuinely transferable:

1. Institutions before politics. Singapore invested in building incorruptible, competent institutions before it worried about electoral politics. The civil service, the judiciary, the regulatory agencies — these were designed and staffed for excellence before the country needed them to manage a complex economy. Most developing countries reverse this sequence.

2. Pay people what they are worth — especially in government. Corruption is not primarily a moral failure. It is an economic outcome of underpaying people in positions of power. Singapore’s solution was not austerity in government salaries but the opposite: competitive pay that eliminated the economic logic for corruption.

3. Housing stability is economic stability. The HDB shows that giving people a genuine stake — physical, financial — in their country’s stability is worth more than any amount of nationalist rhetoric.

4. Ethnicity managed honestly beats ethnicity ignored. Singapore did not pretend race didn’t exist. It built explicit structural solutions for managing racial tension. The result was far more successful than societies that assumed liberal values would naturally produce cohesion.

5. Small countries must choose their advantages very carefully. Singapore could not do everything, so it chose specific areas — finance, logistics, advanced manufacturing, law — and made itself genuinely world-class in each. Spreading limited resources too thin produces mediocrity across the board.

6. Long-term planning is a competitive advantage. Singapore’s government plans in decades, not electoral cycles. The CPF, the HDB, the Temasek and GIC reserve strategy — all were designed for 20-50 year horizons. Democratic governments that govern for the next election are structurally unable to replicate this.

7. Openness is not a risk — isolation is. Every time Singapore faced a choice between protectionism and openness, it chose openness. This was not naive — it was premised on a ruthless assessment that Singapore had no domestic market worth protecting, and its only path was to be better than everyone else in a globally competitive market.

8. The rule of law is an economic asset. Singapore’s legal system — predictable, efficient, enforced — is worth billions of dollars in foreign direct investment per year. Investors who are uncertain whether contracts will be honoured invest less. Certainty compounds.

The India Question

If there is one country that has studied the Singapore model most intensely and implemented it least effectively, it is India. The reasons are instructive.

Scale is real but overstated as an excuse. Singapore is small, yes — but the lessons of meritocratic civil service recruitment, anti-corruption enforcement, and infrastructure investment don’t require smallness. They require political will.

India’s civil service produces brilliant individuals through the IAS examination system but buries them in hierarchical, under-resourced, politically interfered departments. The Singapore model suggests that the problem isn’t the quality of the people — it’s the institutional design around them.

Infrastructure as philosophy. Singapore’s infrastructure investment philosophy — build ahead of demand, maintain relentlessly, make everything actually work — is one that Indian states like Telangana and Gujarat have partially adopted with visible results.

The SEZ experiment — India’s attempt to create Singapore-like industrial zones — largely failed because it couldn’t replicate the surrounding environment: predictable regulation, uncorrupt administration, reliable utilities. The zone needs the whole ecosystem, not just the fences.

The most direct Singapore-influenced urban experiment in India is Amaravati (conceived as Andhra Pradesh’s new capital) and to some extent GIFT City in Gujarat. Whether these succeed will depend on whether the surrounding governance reforms match the physical ambition.

CHAPTER 10 — Paradise Under Pressure: Challenges and the Singapore Future

The Cost of Excellence

Singapore is, as of 2025, the most expensive city in the world by multiple international measures. A monthly rental for a modest apartment can exceed $4,000. A car costs three to four times its market price in the US or UK, because the government artificially restricts vehicle ownership through a Certificate of Entitlement system (to manage congestion) that can cost more than the car itself. A plate of chicken rice at a hawker centre costs perhaps $4; a dinner for two at a mid-range restaurant costs $150.

For Singaporean residents who are high-earners — and there are many — this is manageable. For young Singaporeans entering the workforce, particularly those without elite credentials, the arithmetic is increasingly brutal. The pressures of the meritocratic system — the relentless examination culture, the parental investment in tuition, the anxiety of streaming — have created what sociologists call a society of kiasu (a Hokkien word meaning “afraid to lose”) — fearful, competitive, and exhausted.

Surveys show that Singaporean millennials are markedly less optimistic about their futures than their parents’ generation, and markedly more anxious. This is the price of building a high-performance society: the performance requirement never relaxes.

Democracy and Its Discontents

The People’s Action Party has governed Singapore continuously since 1959 — nearly 70 years. In no election has the PAP received less than 60% of the popular vote. In most, it has won all or nearly all parliamentary seats.

The 2020 general election, regarded as Singapore’s most competitive in a generation, saw the opposition Workers’ Party win 10 seats — still a tiny fraction of Parliament, but celebrated by many Singaporeans as a sign of healthy democratic development.

This raises the central political question about Singapore’s future: can a country that has been governed by one party for seven decades sustain the institutional quality and self-correcting mechanisms that its success has been built on? The PAP’s meritocracy has attracted genuinely able leaders — but institutional quality also requires genuine accountability, and accountability requires genuine competition.

Lee Kuan Yew’s most honest argument for one-party dominance was that Singapore was too fragile, too small, too exposed to the consequences of policy mistakes to tolerate the delays and inefficiencies of competitive democracy. This argument, plausible in 1965, is less convincing in 2025, when Singapore is wealthy, stable, and institutionally mature.

Squeezed Between Giants

Singapore’s greatest geopolitical achievement is not its economy. It is its navigation, for six decades, of being a tiny Chinese-majority city-state in a Malay-Muslim region — with deeply complex relationships with both Malaysia (its nearest neighbour, with whom relations oscillate between warm and tense) and Indonesia (the world’s largest Muslim-majority country, which once conducted Konfrontasi military operations against Malaysia and Singapore in the 1960s).

The rise of China presents Singapore’s most complex long-term strategic challenge. China is Singapore’s largest trading partner. A very large portion of Singapore’s ethnic Chinese population has cultural and linguistic ties to China. Yet Singapore’s entire national model — rule of law, meritocracy, openness, strategic autonomy — is premised on not being a satellite of any great power, including China.

Singapore has skilfully maintained this balance through what its diplomats call “principled pragmatism” — insisting on its sovereignty and the international rule of law while maintaining excellent economic and diplomatic relations with Beijing. The 2016 episode in which a Hong Kong customs authority seized nine Singapore military vehicles returning from training exercises in Taiwan was a reminder of how fragile this balance can be.

As the US-China strategic competition intensifies across the 2020s, Singapore’s room for manoeuvre narrows. It cannot afford to fully align with either Washington or Beijing — but the demand for alignment intensifies with every passing year.

The Island Facing the Sea

There is one challenge facing Singapore that no amount of good governance, meritocratic education, or sovereign wealth can solve by policy alone: the rising sea.

Singapore’s mean elevation above sea level is only about 15 metres, with large coastal areas much lower than that. A warming planet, thermal ocean expansion, and possible significant ice sheet melt create a genuine existential risk for a low-lying island city-state. The government has recognized this with characteristic early action: Singapore is spending SGD 100 billion over the next 50 to 100 years on coastal protection — sea walls, drainage upgrades, reclaimed land raised to higher elevations.

Lee Kuan Yew once said that Singapore was “an air-conditioned nation” — a place that had used technology to master its tropical environment. What he could not have known was that the air conditioning itself — the carbon emissions of the global economy that Singapore so efficiently serves — would eventually require it to build walls against its own coastline.

This is Singapore’s deepest paradox: a nation built on global openness now building walls against the world’s warming sea.

Epilogue — Model, Miracle, or Mirage?

Return, for a moment, to that weeping man on the television screen. August 9, 1965. Lee Kuan Yew, 41 years old, leading a nation that no one expected to survive.

In the 60 years that followed, Singapore went from a per-capita income of approximately $511 to over $65,000 — a journey that took other countries three times as long, or that other countries are still on. A city of squatter settlements became a city of gleaming towers, immaculate parks, and functioning public institutions. A patchwork of ethnic communities that had rioted against each other became, imperfectly but genuinely, a multiracial nation. A fishing port became the world’s busiest transshipment hub and one of its great financial centres.

Is Singapore a model? Yes, with asterisks. Its policies on meritocracy, anti-corruption, long-term planning, and institutional design hold genuine lessons for any nation serious about development. The CPF is an institutional design that deserves global study. The EDB model has been partially adopted by Ireland, Rwanda, and others.

Is it a miracle? In the colloquial sense — something that shouldn’t have been possible — yes. In the religious sense — unexplainable by human agency — absolutely not. Singapore is a miracle of applied human intelligence. It was not handed down from heaven. It was designed, argued about, sometimes brutally imposed, constantly revised, and relentlessly executed by a particular group of remarkable people who happened to find themselves leading a tiny island at a particular moment in history.

Is it a mirage? The critics say yes — that Singapore’s cleanliness, efficiency, and prosperity rest on the suppressed labour of hundreds of thousands of migrant workers from Bangladesh, India, and Myanmar who built its towers and maintain its systems, but who have no path to citizenship and no political voice. That its celebrated harmony is partly enforced silence. That its meritocracy legitimises inequality by calling it deserved. These are serious critiques. They are not wrong.

And yet: the weight of evidence suggests that Singapore is not a mirage. It is a difficult truth — one of those historical facts that challenges easy ideological positions. It is not a model to be copied wholesale. It cannot be divorced from its specific geography, its historical moment, its founding generation’s particular genius. But it proves something vital: that the gap between poverty and prosperity is not primarily about natural resources, or favourable geography, or racial destiny, or colonial advantage. It is about the quality of decisions made by people in positions of power on behalf of the people who depend on them.

That is Singapore’s most uncomfortable lesson for the rest of the world. Not that you must do what Singapore did. But that you could choose to govern as well as Singapore did. And that the fact that so few countries make that choice is not a natural law — it is a failure of will.

Lee Kuan Yew’s last public words, compiled in his final book before he died, included this:

“We built this from nothing. If I had to do it again, I would. But the generation coming next must remember: this was not a gift. This was a choice. Every day, it is a choice.”

Every day, it is a choice.
