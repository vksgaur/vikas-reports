🇧🇷 The Eternal Future: Brazil’s Promise, Its Failures, and What They Mean for the World

The Most Instructive Development Story Never Fully Told

In 1964, Charles de Gaulle — hosting Brazil’s military president at the Élysée Palace — reportedly muttered to an aide: “Brazil is not a serious country.” The line is perfect de Gaulle: dismissive, witty, irresistible. And it has been repeated so often, in so many languages, that it has settled into something approaching received wisdom about Brazil — the country of tomorrow, perpetually. The country that should be a superpower and perpetually isn’t. The country whose future is always arriving and never quite here.

This report rejects that dismissal — not out of sentimentality but out of analytical precision. Brazil is, in fact, the most serious country in the world to study, precisely because its failures are not the failures of small ambition, limited resources, or inadequate human talent. They are the failures of extraordinary endowment repeatedly undone by institutional inheritance. Brazil is the development economist’s great puzzle: a country with every natural ingredient for greatness that has, repeatedly, found ways to transform abundance into dysfunction. Understanding why is not merely interesting — it is essential for every developing country, every reformer, every policymaker who wants to understand why development is hard even when the conditions seem ideal.

India and Brazil share more than geography’s accident of scale. They share colonial legacies that wrote inequality into their foundational institutions. They share democratic politics that make reform simultaneously necessary and politically treacherous. They share extraordinary human creativity and equally extraordinary institutional fragility. Studying Brazil is, for any Indian observer, the uncomfortable and necessary act of looking directly at a mirror.

The carnival is real. The football is genuinely extraordinary. The music — samba, bossa nova, tropicália, funk carioca — has enriched humanity beyond measure. The saudade — that uniquely Brazilian ache of longing for something beautiful just out of reach — is perhaps the most accurate metaphor for Brazil’s development story. This report will spend its 12,000 words trying to understand why the beautiful thing keeps staying just out of reach — and whether the 21st century might, finally, be different.

CHAPTER 1 — The Most Blessed Nation on Earth: Brazil’s Extraordinary Natural Endowment

The Scale That Defies Comprehension

Brazil is so large that the facts about it require a moment of stillness to absorb. It is the world’s fifth-largest country by area — 8.5 million square kilometres, larger than the continental United States, larger than Europe west of Russia. Within its borders lies the Amazon rainforest: 5.5 million square kilometres of the most biologically rich ecosystem on Earth, home to an estimated 10% of all species alive today, producing 20% of the world’s oxygen, cycling rainfall patterns that determine agricultural productivity from São Paulo to Peru. If the Amazon region were a separate country, it would be the world’s fifth-largest by territory.

The numbers, delivered in sequence, become almost surreal: Brazil controls approximately 12% of the world’s total fresh water supply — 300,000 km of rivers, the Amazon river system alone discharging 20% of all the fresh water that flows into the world’s oceans daily. It has the world’s largest cattle herd (approximately 215 million head — more cattle than people). It is the world’s largest exporter of soybeans, sugar, coffee, orange juice, and beef — simultaneously. Its cerrado (the vast central savannah) has been transformed by extraordinary agricultural science from infertile scrubland into, arguably, the world’s most productive agricultural region per hectare for tropical crops. Iron ore reserves in Minas Gerais and Pará are among the world’s largest; the Carajás mine in Pará is the single largest iron ore mine on Earth.

And then, in 2006, Brazil found something else: the pre-salt oil fields — vast petroleum reserves trapped beneath a layer of salt under the Atlantic Ocean floor, at depths of up to 7,000 metres, off the coast of Rio de Janeiro and São Paulo states. Initial estimates suggested 50–100 billion barrels of recoverable oil — enough to make Brazil one of the top five oil producers in the world. It was as if a country already holding every card had just been dealt another ace.

The Geographic Paradox: Abundance Without Infrastructure

Yet Brazil’s geography — the same vast scale that makes it extraordinary — creates infrastructure challenges that no other large economy has faced with equivalent intensity.

A soybean farmer in Mato Grosso — the heart of Brazil’s agricultural miracle, a state larger than Germany — harvests his crop and needs to get it to a port for export. The distance from Mato Grosso’s agricultural heartland to the port of Santos in São Paulo state is approximately 2,000 kilometres. The infrastructure available: frequently unpaved roads that turn to mud in the rainy season, making truck transport impossible for months; virtually no railways (Brazil’s rail network is a fraction of what a country its size requires); and ports chronically congested by the bottleneck of insufficient capacity. The result: Brazilian soybean farmers pay transport costs 3–5 times higher than their American competitors in Iowa shipping to the Mississippi River system. Brazil grows more efficiently than almost anywhere on Earth and then spends the efficiency advantage moving its output to market.

This is Brazil’s geographic paradox: extraordinary productive capacity in the interior, inadequate connections to the coast and to global markets. The Amazon basin — a transport miracle in the era of river commerce — became a connectivity challenge in the era of roads and railways, as the river system runs east-west while Brazil’s main ports face the Atlantic in the south and southeast. Building modern infrastructure across 8.5 million square kilometres of largely unpopulated interior, much of it environmentally sensitive, is a challenge that has defeated successive Brazilian governments with their optimism and their contractors.

The Resource Curse — Complicated

The resource curse — the paradox that natural resource abundance tends to produce worse long-term development outcomes than resource poverty — has a Brazilian version that is more complex than the textbook petrostate model.

Brazil is not a simple petro-state. Its economy is genuinely diversified: agriculture contributes 6% of GDP but over 25% of exports; manufacturing (still significant despite decades of premature deindustrialisation) contributes to the remaining export basket; the service sector is the largest single component of GDP; and technology services in São Paulo are growing rapidly. Brazil is not dangerously dependent on oil revenues in the way that Venezuela or Nigeria became dependent.

Yet the political economy of resource abundance has been deeply distorting in Brazil’s specific institutional context. When commodity prices are high — when Chinese demand for soybeans and iron ore is flooding Brazil with export revenue — the political pressure to make hard institutional reforms eases. There is always enough money flowing to sustain the clientelistic political system, to fund the patronage networks, to paper over the fiscal gaps that genuine reform would require closing. When commodity prices fall — as they did in 2015 — there is rarely sufficient political stability to implement the reforms that the boom period deferred. Brazil has been trapped in this cycle not once but repeatedly, across 200 years of economic history.

The Human Richness of Extraordinary Diversity

Brazil’s demographic profile is unlike that of any other large country on Earth. Its 215 million people are the descendants of:

Portuguese colonizers who began arriving in 1500, bringing their language (now spoken by more people in Brazil than in Portugal by a ratio of 14:1), their Catholicism, and their administrative culture. Enslaved Africans — approximately 4.9 million men, women, and children transported across the Atlantic between the 1500s and 1850, making Brazil the largest single destination of the transatlantic slave trade. Indigenous peoples — hundreds of distinct nations who inhabited the continent before Portuguese arrival, whose populations collapsed catastrophically (estimated from 2-5 million to under 300,000 by 1900) from disease, violence, and dispossession, yet whose genetic and cultural inheritance remains woven into Brazilian identity. European immigrants — over 4 million Italians (particularly to São Paulo), over 1.7 million Germans (to Rio Grande do Sul and Santa Catarina), significant numbers of Spaniards, Poles, and other Europeans who came in the late 19th and early 20th centuries as labor for coffee plantations and factories. Japanese immigrants — over 1.5 million, beginning with the Kasato Maru in 1908, making Brazil home to the largest Japanese diaspora outside Japan — concentrated in São Paulo, where today’s Liberdade neighbourhood preserves the soul of immigrant Japan in Brazilian form. Lebanese and Syrian immigrants — including the families of former presidents Michel Temer and Dilma Rousseff’s opponent Aécio Neves — who built commercial networks throughout Brazil’s interior.

This confluence of cultures produced something that has no equivalent elsewhere: a creative synthesis in which the fusion of African, Portuguese, and indigenous elements generated art forms of extraordinary vitality. Samba — born in Rio de Janeiro’s hillside favelas from the meeting of African rhythmic traditions and Portuguese musical forms — is one of the great musical inventions of the 20th century. Bossa nova — the 1950s and 1960s synthesis of samba’s rhythmic sophistication with jazz harmonic language — produced The Girl from Ipanema and changed popular music globally. Carnival — particularly Rio’s and Salvador’s — is the world’s greatest participatory art event, a week-long transformation of urban space in which the usual social hierarchies are partially suspended in a collective celebration of extraordinary visual and musical intensity.

Brazilian football is not merely a sport; it is a cultural philosophy. The jogo bonito (beautiful game) — the idea that football should be played with creativity, individual expression, and aesthetic pleasure, not just tactical efficiency — is Brazil’s most widely exported cultural concept. Pelé, Garrincha, Ronaldo, Ronaldinho, Neymar: the list of Brazilians who have changed how football is understood and played reads as a gallery of creative geniuses.

The paradox: this extraordinary creative diversity, which produced beauty that has enriched the world, also carried deep social fractures. The colour line in Brazil — invisible by law since abolition in 1888, structured into every dimension of social and economic life in practice — remains one of Brazil’s most persistent sources of inequality. Afro-Brazilians, who constitute approximately 56% of the population, earn on average 40% less than white Brazilians, are dramatically underrepresented in universities and elite professions, and are overrepresented in incarceration, police violence statistics, and precarious employment. The diversity that produced the carnival has never fully resolved the hierarchy that slavery created.

CHAPTER 2 — The Colonial Foundation: 300 Years of Extraction (1500–1822)

April 22, 1500: The Day That Wrote Brazil’s Future

The Portuguese navigator Pedro Álvares Cabral arrived on the coast of what is now Bahia on April 22, 1500, planted a cross, celebrated Mass, and dispatched a letter to King Manuel I of Portugal announcing the discovery of a large and apparently rich new land. The flag he raised — the Portuguese cross on Brazilian soil — inaugurated a colonial relationship that would last 322 years and write, in the language of land ownership, labour coercion, and racial hierarchy, the first chapters of problems that Brazil has not yet fully resolved.

The Portuguese colonial project in Brazil was organized around a single principle: extraction. Unlike the British in North America — who were (initially) building a settler society with institutions designed to attract and retain European settlers, including property rights, rule of law, and eventually self-governance — the Portuguese were primarily interested in extracting Brazil’s resources and returning them to Lisbon. The colony existed to enrich the metropolis, not to build a community.

The Latifúndio: Inequality Written in Land

The foundational institution of colonial Brazil was the latifúndio — the large estate. The Portuguese Crown distributed vast tracts of Brazilian territory to a small number of favored individuals (donatários and, later, sesmeiros) in grants of extraordinary size. A single land grant could encompass territory larger than Portugal itself. This initial distribution — in which a tiny number of people received an enormous share of the most productive land while everyone else had access to nothing — created the land concentration that persists, in attenuated form, to the present day.

Brazil’s Gini coefficient of land distribution — one of the world’s highest at approximately 0.87 (where 1 represents complete concentration in one owner) — reflects directly a colonial distribution that 200 years of republic have barely modified. The large landowner (fazendeiro, today’s ruralista in Congress) has been among the most consistently powerful forces in Brazilian politics from 1500 to the present, preserving a system of land concentration that makes genuine agrarian reform politically impossible and rural poverty chronically severe.

The economic structure built on this land: first Brazilwood (pau-brasil, the red dye-wood from which the country takes its name); then sugar (the plantation commodity that made Brazil the world’s most valuable colonial territory in the 17th century); then gold and diamonds discovered in Minas Gerais in the early 18th century; then cotton; and finally, with extraordinary productivity in the 19th century, coffee.

4.9 Million Souls: The Scale That Haunts

The labour on which all of these extraction systems depended was enslaved. Brazil was not merely a slave society — it was the largest slave society in the Western Hemisphere, and within that hemispheric superlative, its scale was staggering.

Approximately 4.9 million enslaved Africans were transported to Brazil between the early 1500s and 1850 — a figure that represents roughly 40% of the entire transatlantic slave trade. More enslaved Africans came to Brazil than to all other destinations in the Americas combined. They came from West Africa, from the Congo basin, from Mozambique, from Angola — men, women, and children whose names were erased and replaced, whose family structures were destroyed, whose humanity was legally denied and whose labour, across three centuries, built the agricultural and mining wealth on which Brazil’s colonial economy rested.

Slavery was not abolished in Brazil until May 13, 1888 — making Brazil the last country in the Americas to abolish it, doing so 23 years after the United States’ Civil War and 54 years after the British Caribbean. The Lei Áurea (Golden Law) that Princess Isabel signed in 1888 freed approximately 700,000 enslaved people — and freed them with no land, no compensation, no education access, no civic infrastructure to support their transition to freedom. Abolition in Brazil was emancipation without reconstruction — the legal status changed, the structural conditions did not.

The consequences reverberate to the present with unmistakeable clarity. Brazil’s racial inequality — Afro-Brazilians significantly poorer, less educated, more incarcerated, more subject to police violence than white Brazilians — is not an accident or a mystery. It is the directly traceable inheritance of 350 years of slavery followed by emancipation without support, combined with the social mobility barriers that racialised systems reliably produce across generations. Every economic statistic about Brazil must be understood through this lens: Brazil’s inequality problem and Brazil’s race problem are not two separate problems.

The Portuguese Model: Extraction Without Institution

The Portuguese colonial model differed from its British and Spanish counterparts in ways that left lasting institutional consequences.

Portugal — a small country with a population of perhaps 1.5 million at the height of its colonial empire — simply lacked the administrative and intellectual capacity to build institutions across its vast Brazilian territory. The British colonies in North America had Harvard (founded 1636), William & Mary (1693), Yale (1701) — universities building a colonial educated class that would eventually staff courts, legislatures, and commerce. Brazil’s first university was founded in 1920 — 300 years after Harvard, and only after independence.

The lack of higher education institutions meant that colonial Brazil produced no domestic intellectual and professional class. Government positions were staffed by appointees from Lisbon; professional services relied on men trained in Coimbra. The civic infrastructure of courts, local governance, professional associations, and educational institutions that creates rule of law from the bottom up simply did not exist in colonial Brazil at any adequate scale.

The governance model was instead clientelism — the distribution of government appointments, land grants, and economic favors to those who rendered political loyalty to the Crown and its local representatives. This practice — in which access to economic opportunity flowed through personal political relationships rather than through impersonal rules — became the foundational mode of Brazilian political economy. It persists today in the presidencialismo de coalizão (coalition presidentialism) that requires Brazilian presidents to distribute ministerial positions and budget appropriations to their legislative coalition partners in exchange for congressional support.

Elite-Managed Independence: The Pattern That Never Changed

Brazilian independence in 1822 was achieved in a manner that set the pattern for every subsequent Brazilian political transition: it was managed by elites to preserve elite interests while changing the formal governing arrangements.

Napoleon’s invasion of Portugal in 1807 drove the Portuguese royal family — King João VI, his court of approximately 15,000 — to Brazil aboard British warships, setting up the royal government in Rio de Janeiro. When Napoleon was defeated and Portugal wanted its king back, the king’s son Dom Pedro remained in Brazil. On September 7, 1822, standing on the banks of the Ipiranga river in São Paulo, Dom Pedro announced Brazilian independence — the famous grito do Ipiranga (cry of Ipiranga) — and declared himself Emperor of Brazil.

Independence was bloodless by Latin American standards. There was no revolutionary war, no liberating army, no radical rupture with the social order. The same families who owned the land before independence owned it after. The same slaves who worked the plantations before 1822 worked them after. The same clientelistic political culture that governed the colony governed the empire. The flag changed; the underlying structure did not.

This pattern — revolution from above, managed by elites, preserving essential inequality — would repeat at every subsequent turning point in Brazilian history: the abolition of slavery (1888), the proclamation of the republic (1889), Vargas’s 1930 coup, the 1964 military coup, the return to democracy in 1985. Brazil’s political culture has consistently prioritized the managed transition over the rupture that genuine reform sometimes requires. The result: Brazil has changed its political forms frequently and its structural inequalities rarely.

CHAPTER 3 — Coffee, Oligarchs, and the Old Republic: Brazil’s First Development Failure (1822–1930)

When Coffee Was King and King Was Coffee

The 19th century transformed Brazil’s economic geography around a single crop. Coffee — which had arrived in Brazil in the early 18th century and found in São Paulo state’s iron-rich terra roxa (red soil) conditions of extraordinary productivity — became by the 1850s Brazil’s dominant export, eventually accounting for over 60% of total export revenue. By the 1880s, Brazil produced approximately 70% of the world’s coffee supply. The coffee barons of the Paulista (São Paulo) interior became the richest men in Brazil, the most powerful political force in the country, and the architects of the economic and political system that would define the Old Republic.

São Paulo’s coffee economy transformed the state into Brazil’s economic dynamo. With the wealth from coffee came railways (São Paulo had the most extensive railway network in South America by 1900), then factories (coffee profits were reinvested in urban industry), then European immigrants (Italians, Spaniards, Portuguese) whose labour built the factories and whose presence gave São Paulo the densest urban-industrial workforce in Brazil. The city of São Paulo grew from 65,000 people in 1890 to over 500,000 by 1920 — one of the fastest urban growth rates in the world at that time.

Café com Leite: Oligarchy Dressed as Democracy

The Old Republic (1889–1930) — Brazil’s first attempt at constitutional republican government — was, in practice, an oligarchic system so carefully managed that the term “democracy” barely applied. Its governing logic was expressed in the famous phrase “café com leite” (coffee with milk): the political bargain between São Paulo’s coffee oligarchy and Minas Gerais’s dairy farmers (Minas was Brazil’s most populous state and had the political weight to balance São Paulo’s economic dominance). The presidency alternated between Paulista coffee men and Mineiro politicians; as long as this bargain held, the rest of Brazil — the impoverished Northeast, the urban workers of Rio, the rubber tappers of the Amazon, the immigrants flooding into São Paulo’s factories — was excluded from meaningful political consideration.

The political mechanism: coronelismo — the system of rule by powerful local landowners (coronéis, colonels, a title many of them actually held in the National Guard) who delivered votes to state governments in exchange for local autonomy, government jobs, and immunity from prosecution. Elections in Brazil’s interior were managed theater: the coronel told his tenant farmers and employees who to vote for, votes were counted by his allies, and the results were certified by state machines allied to the Paulista-Mineiro federal arrangement. Universal male suffrage existed on paper; effective political participation was available only to those whom the oligarchy chose to include.

Privatizing Gains, Socializing Losses: Brazil’s Original Template

The coffee valorization schemes of the early 20th century established a pattern of political economy that Brazil has never fully escaped: the benefits of success flow to the powerful; the costs of failure are distributed to everyone else.

When global coffee prices fell — as they did periodically, since Brazil’s own production capacity was so large it could drive down world prices — the coffee oligarchs did not accept market losses. They lobbied (and controlled) the federal government to intervene. The mechanism: Brazil borrowed money from international banks (particularly in London) to buy and stockpile surplus coffee, removing it from the market to support prices. The international borrowing was charged to the Brazilian government — and ultimately to Brazilian taxpayers, including the poor farmers who grew crops other than coffee and the urban workers who made nothing from the scheme. The planters’ revenues were protected; the national debt increased.

This was the original Brazilian template for what economists call “private gains, socialized losses” — a pattern that would repeat with remarkable consistency across the next century: in military-era state corporations that accumulated losses charged to the public, in banking crises whose resolution was funded by taxpayers rather than shareholders, and in the Petrobras corruption scandal whose costs fell ultimately on pension funds, small investors, and the Brazilian public.

The First Industrial Sparks — and Their Suppression

São Paulo’s coffee economy generated, almost accidentally, Brazil’s first significant manufacturing base. The city’s growing population needed goods; the immigrant workers needed jobs in slow agricultural seasons; the railway workshops needed mechanical skills. By the 1900s, São Paulo had a significant textile industry, food processing plants, basic metal fabrication workshops, and a working class — predominantly Italian immigrants — of growing political consciousness.

Brazil’s industrialists and the coffee oligarchs had a complex relationship with this working class. On one hand, they needed cheap urban labor. On the other, they needed to prevent the labour organization that might raise wages. The coffee oligarchs’ response to early Brazilian labour militancy was consistent: repression. Strikes were broken by police. Labour organizers were deported (immigrant workers could be expelled for union activity). The anarcho-syndicalist unions that built São Paulo’s early labour movement were systematically suppressed throughout the Old Republic.

The fundamental contradiction of Brazil’s early industrialization: the same political system that needed cheap industrial labour to grow its factories refused to allow the institutions — independent unions, labour law, minimum wages — that might make industrial workers into a stable middle class. This contradiction could not be sustained indefinitely. In 1930, it wasn’t.

The Crash That Changed Everything

Brazil’s economy in 1929 was premised on a single assumption: global coffee prices would remain high enough to service the country’s external debts and maintain the political bargain between São Paulo and Minas Gerais. The Great Depression destroyed this assumption within months.

Global coffee prices collapsed by nearly 70% between 1929 and 1931. Brazil’s export revenues evaporated. The federal government, committed to maintaining coffee price supports, could not simultaneously service its international debt — and in 1931, Brazil defaulted. The financial underpinning of the café com leite bargain was gone. São Paulo’s planter class, exposed and desperate, was no longer able to dominate federal politics as confidently as before.

In October 1930, Getúlio Vargas — the governor of Rio Grande do Sul and unsuccessful presidential candidate in the 1930 election — led a military-backed coup that ended the Old Republic. It was Brazil’s first successful extra-constitutional transfer of power since the monarchy’s abolition in 1889 — and the beginning of the most consequential era in modern Brazilian history.

CHAPTER 4 — Getúlio Vargas: The Father of Modern Brazil and Its First Great Paradox (1930–1954)

The Most Complex Man in Brazilian History

Getúlio Dornelles Vargas defies easy categorization — which is precisely why he remains the most argued-about figure in Brazilian political history, simultaneously revered and reviled, claimed by both the Brazilian left and the Brazilian right, and present in contemporary political rhetoric in ways that reveal how incompletely Brazil has processed his legacy.

He was born in 1882 in São Borja, in Brazil’s southernmost state of Rio Grande do Sul — a gaucho culture that was simultaneously deeply Brazilian and shaped by proximity to Argentina and Uruguay. He was a lawyer and politician of exceptional intelligence and even more exceptional political instinct: a man who seemed to know intuitively what people wanted to hear and to adjust his message to every audience without ever appearing merely manipulative. He had a gift for ambiguity that served him extraordinarily well in a country where every political coalition required accommodating contradictory interests.

Vargas came to power in 1930 as a reformer — a man who promised to end the corruption and exclusion of the café com leite oligarchy and bring Brazil’s excluded masses into the political and economic system. He kept this promise, in his own complicated way.

The Estado Novo: Authoritarian Modernization

After governing Brazil through a provisional government for seven years, Vargas in 1937 — anticipating an election he might not win — staged a self-coup, dissolved Congress, and established the Estado Novo (New State): an authoritarian regime modeled partly on Portugal’s Salazar regime and partly on Italian corporatism, with traces of Mussolini in its aesthetic and its suppression of political opposition.

The Estado Novo’s political record was straightforwardly authoritarian: political parties were banned, the press was censored by the DIP (Department of Press and Propaganda), political opponents were imprisoned and tortured, trade unions were brought under state control and stripped of independence. By any honest assessment, it was a dictatorship.

Yet simultaneously, the Estado Novo implemented the most ambitious program of state-led industrialization in Brazilian history — achievements that genuinely transformed the country:

Volta Redonda (1941): Brazil’s first integrated steel mill, built over the explicit objections of the United States (which preferred to sell steel to Brazil rather than help it produce its own). Vargas leveraged US anxiety about Brazil’s neutrality in World War II to extract American financing for a plant that became the foundation of Brazil’s heavy industry. Today, Volta Redonda is a symbol of national industrial sovereignty in a way that New Deal infrastructure projects are symbols in American memory.

Petrobras (1953): The state oil company that Vargas established with the nationalist slogan “O petróleo é nosso” (The oil is ours) — a direct refusal of the international oil companies’ expectation that they would control Brazil’s petroleum resources. Petrobras became one of the world’s great oil companies, a genuine technical pioneer in deepwater drilling, and a source of national pride that made any subsequent privatization politically explosive.

Consolidação das Leis do Trabalho (CLT, 1943): Brazil’s comprehensive labour code — a system of minimum wage, maximum working hours, paid vacation, maternity leave, and employment protections that transformed the lives of Brazil’s urban working class. The CLT was, simultaneously, a genuine improvement in workers’ conditions and a mechanism of control: labour unions were required to register with and be approved by the government, union finances were managed by the Ministry of Labour, and the union structure was organized to make the state the mediator between workers and employers rather than allowing independent worker organisation.

The Vargas Paradox: What He Built and What He Left Behind

Understanding Getúlio Vargas requires holding two apparently contradictory assessments simultaneously.

What he built: The foundations of modern Brazil — its industrial infrastructure, its labour rights system, its state enterprise sector, its national identity as something more than a coffee export economy. Brazil entered the Vargas era as a commodity-dependent agricultural economy; it left it as a country with steel mills, automotive ambitions, and the institutional framework (however flawed) of an industrial society. Without Vargas, there is no path to the São Paulo that became Latin America’s largest industrial centre.

What he left behind: The corporatist capture of labour that prevented the development of genuinely independent trade unions — leaving Brazilian workers dependent on the state for protection rather than capable of negotiating it themselves. The desenvolvimentista ideology — the belief that industrialization required state direction, that foreign capital was inherently exploitative, that national economic sovereignty required insulating Brazil from global integration — that would produce both genuine industrial achievement and spectacular fiscal dysfunction in every subsequent decade. The cult of the charismatic nationalist leader who could transcend institutions, rally the people, and govern through personal relationship rather than impersonal rules — a political template that Lula, Bolsonaro, and every Brazilian populist since Vargas has, in their different ways, inherited.

“I Leave Life to Enter History”: August 24, 1954

By 1954, Vargas was back in power — elected democratically in 1950, governing a post-Estado Novo democracy, increasingly embattled by military pressure and opposition from conservative forces who feared his growing alliance with urban labour. An assassination attempt against a journalist critical of Vargas, carried out by his palace bodyguard, provided his enemies with the scandal they needed. The Brazilian military issued an ultimatum: resign.

On August 24, 1954, in his bedroom in the Catete Palace in Rio de Janeiro, Getúlio Vargas pressed a pistol to his heart and pulled the trigger. He left a letter:

“I fought against the looting of Brazil. I fought against the looting of the people. I have fought bare-breasted. The hatred, infamy and calumny did not prostrate my spirit. I gave you my life. Now I offer my death. Nothing remains. Serenely I take the first step on the road to eternity and I leave life to enter history.”

It was perfectly theatrical — deliberately so. Vargas understood that a dramatic death would transform him from a beleaguered politician into a martyr, that his suicide note would be read as an indictment of his enemies, that the military ultimatum that preceded his death would be read as evidence of the foreign and elite interests that had “looted” Brazil. He was right. The announcement of his death triggered riots in Rio de Janeiro. His political legacy — nationalist, pro-labour, developmentalist — became the dominant current in Brazilian politics for the next 30 years.

The Vargas suicide note, with its imagery of the leader sacrificed by the forces of capital and foreign interest, remains the most powerful text in Brazilian political mythology. Every subsequent Brazilian populist — Lula and Bolsonaro in their entirely different ways — has drawn on this mythology: the leader who fights for the people against the forces that seek to destroy him. It is a mythology that makes it extraordinarily difficult for Brazilian democracy to distinguish between a genuine reformer under attack and a corrupt politician using the language of victimhood to escape accountability.

The Most Optimistic Presidency in History

Juscelino Kubitschek — known universally in Brazil as JK, a nickname whose two initials somehow contain the warmth of a man who was simply impossible to dislike — was elected President of Brazil in 1955 on a platform of extraordinary optimism expressed in a single unforgettable phrase: “Cinquenta anos em cinco” — fifty years of progress in five.

This was not a modest promise. Brazil in 1955 was, for all the genuine progress of the Vargas years, still an overwhelmingly agricultural, regionally unequal, institutionally fragile country with minimal road connectivity, negligible automotive manufacturing, and a capital city (Rio de Janeiro) that was simultaneously magnificent and inadequate for a country of Brazil’s ambitions. JK intended to change all of this, at the speed of a man who had an impatient country to run and four years to run it.

What JK delivered was remarkable in its audacity and real in its achievement: in five years, he attracted Volkswagen, Mercedes-Benz, Willys, and Simca to establish automobile manufacturing operations in Brazil (creating an industry essentially from nothing); built a network of new roads connecting Brazil’s interior for the first time; expanded electricity generation through major hydroelectric projects; and established a new capital city from scratch in the central cerrado. GDP grew at approximately 7% per year throughout his administration. Industrial output doubled.

Brasília: The City That Shouldn’t Exist

The construction of Brasília — announced in JK’s first week in office, begun within months, completed in 1960 — remains the most audacious act of deliberate nation-building in the 20th century. Designed by the architect Oscar Niemeyer (whose curving, sculptural forms gave the new city its otherworldly quality) and the urban planner Lúcio Costa (whose plano piloto — pilot plan — organized the city around an axial cross visible from the air), Brasília was conceived as a declaration: Brazil was a modern country, a country of the future, and it was building its capital to prove it.

The city was built on the barren cerrado plateau of Goiás state, 1,000 kilometres from the coast, at an altitude of 1,172 metres. There was nothing there — no existing town, no infrastructure, no labour force. Everything was brought from outside: the candangos — migrant workers recruited overwhelmingly from Brazil’s impoverished Northeast, men who left their drought-stricken home states for the construction sites of the new capital, living in temporary camps called invasões, working 18-hour shifts, often without safety equipment or adequate food. They built Niemeyer’s impossible curved concrete structures — the National Congress with its twin towers and contrasting dome and bowl, the Presidential Palace floating on its reflecting pool, the Supreme Court with its blind-justice sculpture — and then were largely forgotten by the gleaming city they made from nothing.

The cost: approximately $2 billion in 1960 dollars — a staggering sum for a country of Brazil’s income level. JK financed it primarily by printing money. The logic: Brazil had no alternative financing mechanism at the scale required; the development bank could not absorb the cost; foreign borrowing was limited; so the money supply expanded to fund the construction. The inflation that resulted — rising from manageable single digits to 40% annually by the time JK left office — was not an incidental consequence of his programme but its direct fiscal result. The extraordinary advance of Brasília’s modernism was purchased on the instalment plan, and the instalments were paid by every Brazilian who subsequently watched their savings eroded by inflation.

This is JK’s paradox in miniature: the achievement was real (Brasília is genuinely extraordinary — a UNESCO World Heritage Site, a functioning capital of 3 million people, an architectural marvel), the ambition was necessary (connecting Brazil’s vast interior required exactly this kind of audacious commitment), and the method was devastating in its long-term consequences. The inflationary genie released in 1956–1960 would not be returned to its bottle for 34 years.

CHAPTER 6 — Military Rule and the Brazilian Miracle — With a Dark Asterisk (1964–1985)

The Coup That Changed the Trajectory

On April 1, 1964, the Brazilian military removed President João Goulart from office — a man who had been moving visibly leftward, proposing land reform, nationalizing foreign oil refineries, and embracing increasingly radical nationalist rhetoric. The coup was welcomed by Brazilian business elites, the middle class (frightened by inflation and communist rhetoric), and the United States government — whose ambassador Lincoln Gordon had been cable-communicating with Washington about the necessity of removing Goulart for months.

The CIA’s role in supporting the conspiracy — providing moral support, encouraging the plotters, and positioning an aircraft carrier task force off the Brazilian coast in case military support was needed — was not publicly confirmed until decades later. But the US government’s warm reception of the coup’s results established an ominous precedent: Brazil’s democratic experiment could be cancelled if the results became uncomfortable for Washington or for domestic elites, without significant international consequence.

The military government that followed — lasting 21 years, from 1964 to 1985, governing under five generals — was not consistently brutal by Latin American standards. Its political repression was real but calibrated: political opponents were expelled, imprisoned, and tortured, but a wholesale terrorizing of the population on the Pinochet model was avoided. The press was censored but not entirely silenced. Political parties were permitted in a neutered two-party form (ARENA for the government, MDB for a controlled opposition). The formula was sophisticated: enough freedom to maintain a façade of democratic legitimacy, enough repression to prevent genuine challenge.

The Brazilian Miracle: Real and Rotten at the Core

Between 1968 and 1973 — the years Brazilians call the milagre econômico, the economic miracle — Brazil achieved average annual GDP growth of approximately 10%. This was the period when international opinion genuinely wondered whether Brazil was following Japan’s trajectory: a country transforming itself at extraordinary speed from agricultural backwardness to industrial modernity.

The achievements were real. The Itaipu hydroelectric dam — completed in 1984, built jointly with Paraguay on the Paraná river — was at the time the world’s largest hydroelectric power plant, a genuine engineering triumph of extraordinary scale (its turbines generate enough electricity to supply 17% of Brazil’s needs and 75% of Paraguay’s). The Transamazônica highway cut through thousands of kilometres of Amazon forest. The Embraer aircraft company was founded in 1969 — a state enterprise that would eventually become the world’s third-largest commercial aircraft manufacturer. The petrochemical, steel, and telecommunications sectors all expanded dramatically. Per capita income tripled in a decade. A genuine Brazilian middle class — white-collar workers, engineers, managers — grew substantially.

But the miracle had three fatal structural flaws that transformed achievement into catastrophe:

The debt bomb: The military government financed its infrastructure ambitions substantially through foreign borrowing — from the international banks that were desperate to recycle the petrodollar deposits flooding in after the 1973 oil price shock. Brazil’s external debt grew from $3 billion in 1964 to $100 billion by 1984 — accumulated at variable interest rates that seemed manageable at 4% but became catastrophic at 20% when the Volcker Shock hit. The assumption that tomorrow’s growth would service today’s debt was not irrational in 1970; by 1982, when global interest rates quadrupled, it was the foundation of a crisis.

The dark asterisk of repression: The miracle years were simultaneously the years of AI-5 — the Institutional Act Number 5 issued in December 1968, which permitted the government to close Congress, sack elected officials, revoke citizens’ political rights, suspend habeas corpus, and censor all media. The porões (basements) of the military’s political police were places of systematic torture. Hundreds of Brazilians were killed; thousands were tortured; tens of thousands more were harassed, exiled, or professionally destroyed. The international magazine covers celebrating the Brazilian economic miracle did not typically discuss the vaquejada (the bucking steer) as torture vocabulary.

Growth without distribution: Finance Minister Antonio Delfim Netto — the rotund, brilliant, politically dexterous economist who was the intellectual architect of the miracle years — famously argued that criticizing income distribution during rapid growth was premature: “First you grow the cake, then you distribute it.” The cake grew enormously. The distribution never arrived. Between 1960 and 1980, GDP per capita roughly trebled — but the share of income going to the richest 1% of Brazilians actually increased, from 12% to 17%. The miracle made rich Brazilians dramatically richer and poor Brazilians only modestly better off. Brazil’s Gini coefficient — the measure of income inequality — remained among the world’s highest throughout the miracle period.

The Volcker Shock and the Miracle’s End

In 1979, two events converged to destroy the miracle:

The second oil shock — triggered by the Iranian Revolution — doubled oil prices again, devastating an economy that imported 80% of its petroleum consumption. And in October 1979, US Federal Reserve Chairman Paul Volcker raised American interest rates dramatically — to 20% by 1981 — to break American inflation. International borrowing costs soared. The variable-rate debt that Brazil had accumulated at 4–6% in the early 1970s now cost 18–20% to service.

By August 1982, Mexico announced it could not service its international debt — and the Brazilian crisis became explicit. International lending to Latin America froze overnight. Brazil, with $87 billion in external debt and an economy contracting rapidly, entered negotiations with the IMF for emergency support. The miracle was over. The debt was real. The reckoning had arrived.

Abertura: The Military’s Careful Retreat

The military’s withdrawal from power between 1974 and 1985 — the abertura (opening) — was managed with the same elite-negotiated caution as every major Brazilian political transition.

General Ernesto Geisel, who became president in 1974, began a gradual, controlled political liberalisation: easing press censorship, releasing some political prisoners, allowing more competitive congressional elections. The rationale was clear: with the economic miracle faltering, the military’s legitimacy had eroded; a managed exit was preferable to a forced one. His successor, General João Batista Figueiredo, continued the process, ultimately amnestying political exiles (allowing them to return), legalizing multiple political parties (ending the neutered two-party system), and permitting direct gubernatorial elections in 1982.

The transition culminated in 1985 with the election — by an electoral college, not direct popular vote — of Tancredo Neves as Brazil’s first civilian president in 21 years. Tancredo became one of history’s more poignant political figures: elected to the presidency, he fell terminally ill on the eve of his inauguration and died before he could take office. His vice president, José Sarney — a political conservative from the Northeast, previously a member of the pro-military ARENA party — assumed the presidency in his place. The return to democracy began not with the reformer Brazil had elected but with a politician whose every instinct favoured the status quo.

CHAPTER 7 — The Inflation Monster: How Hyperinflation Destroyed a Generation (1985–1994)

The World’s Most Extraordinary Economic Horror Story

A schoolteacher in São Paulo — let us call her Marlene — receives her monthly salary on the last Friday of November 1993. She holds in her hand 500,000 cruzeiros. This is not a large sum: after a decade of inflation, the cruzeiro has so many zeros that Marlene’s salary, which would have been 5,000 cruzeiros ten years ago, is now 500,000 cruzeiros. But the number is meaningless in itself. What matters is what happens if she waits.

If Marlene deposits her salary and pays her bills normally over the following four weeks, the money she saved at the end of November will be worth, in the groceries it can buy, approximately 20% less than when she received it. This is not a calculation she made — it is the daily arithmetic of survival in a country experiencing 80% monthly inflation. So she doesn’t wait. She immediately takes her salary and converts it into everything she knows will hold value: bags of rice, cooking oil, sugar, anything storable; United States dollars if she can find anyone to exchange with (technically illegal, but universal); perhaps a television or a washing machine — because appliances lose value more slowly than cruzeiros. She shops as if the money in her hands is burning. Because it is.

This is Brazil in 1993 — not in a war, not in a natural disaster, not in a famine — but in a peacetime, formally democratic country with a diversified economy and extraordinary natural resources. The monthly inflation rate of 80% implied an annual inflation rate of approximately 2,500%. The school where Marlene teaches adjusts its cafeteria prices twice a week. The supermarket she shops at employs two full-time workers whose sole job is to walk the aisles continuously, updating price tags — not because quantities change, but because prices do, sometimes multiple times per day.

How Brazil Got Here: The Road to Hyperinflationary Hell

Hyperinflation does not appear suddenly. Brazil’s reached 2,500% through a 30-year accumulation of fiscal irresponsibility and institutional failure.

The mechanism: governments facing fiscal deficits — spending more than they collected in taxes — financed the gap by printing money. The money supply expanded faster than economic output, making each cruzeiro worth less than the one before it. As prices rose, wage earners demanded higher wages to maintain their purchasing power; as wages rose, businesses raised prices to protect their margins; as prices rose, the government’s spending on wages, pensions, and indexed obligations rose, deepening the deficit that required more money-printing. This was the inflationary spiral — and once it began, every structural feature of the Brazilian economy seemed specifically designed to accelerate it.

The central structural accelerant was indexation — correção monetária (monetary correction). Developed in the 1960s as a rational response to moderate inflation, indexation linked financial contracts, wages, rents, and government bonds to measured inflation — automatically adjusting their value to compensate for past price increases. The logic was humane: protect people from inflation’s erosion of their savings. The effect was perverse: by making every contract automatically adjust upward for past inflation, indexation embedded the expectation of future inflation into every economic transaction, creating a self-fulfilling prophecy. You expected 200% inflation because your contracts guaranteed you 200% compensation for past inflation. Because everyone expected 200% inflation, they demanded 200% wage increases and imposed 200% price increases. The expectation created the reality.

Financial institutions — banks, brokers, insurance companies — discovered that the indexation system was, for them, enormously profitable. Overnight money markets (overnight) allowed institutions to continuously roll their cash into inflation-indexed instruments, earning the inflation rate plus a spread. The Brazilian financial system became extraordinarily sophisticated at extracting profits from inflation — and therefore had powerful institutional incentives to preserve the system, however obviously destructive it was for ordinary people.

Five Currencies, Zero Solutions

Between 1986 and 1994, Brazil launched five stabilization plans, introduced four new currencies, and failed to cure inflation every single time. Each failure left the country more exhausted, more cynical, and more deeply trapped.

The Cruzado Plan (1986): President Sarney’s Finance Minister Dilson Funaro froze prices overnight — an economy-wide price control that instantly created shortages (merchants simply hoarded goods rather than sell them at frozen prices) and temporary euphoria (Brazilians briefly experienced stable prices, a sensation so novel that Sarney’s approval ratings soared). Inflation reappeared within months; the plan collapsed by year’s end. A new currency (the cruzado, replacing the cruzeiro at a rate of 1:1,000) was left discredited within a year of its introduction.

The Bresser Plan (1987): A partial price freeze with some fiscal adjustment. Failed within months.

The Summer Plan (1989): Yet another new currency (the cruzado novo replacing the cruzado), another price freeze, another set of confidence-inspiring announcements. Failed within weeks.

The Collor Plan I (1990): The most radical and most destructive of all stabilization attempts. Fernando Collor de Mello — elected in December 1989 as Brazil’s first directly elected president since the 1964 coup, a photogenic 40-year-old former governor from the small northeastern state of Alagoas who ran against “the maharajahs” of Brazil’s bloated bureaucracy — announced on his first day in office a measure of extraordinary aggression: the overnight freezing of 80% of all bank deposits in Brazil.

The logic: inflation existed because there was too much money in the system. Therefore: remove the money. Essentially confiscate the entire liquidity of the Brazilian economy for 18 months. It was, in concept, a monetary big bang. The execution was a humanitarian catastrophe. Businesses could not make payroll. Small construction firms could not purchase materials. Factories closed. Workers were laid off. Hundreds of thousands of small and medium businesses — which had done nothing wrong except keep money in banks — were destroyed by the sudden unavailability of cash. The Brazilian Middle class was devastated.

And then: inflation came back anyway. By 1992, it was over 1,000% again. Collor himself was impeached the same year — not for the economic catastrophe but for a corruption scandal involving kickbacks from businessmen managed by his campaign treasurer PC Farias, in the first presidential impeachment in Brazilian democratic history. He resigned before the Senate could remove him.

The Itamar Plan and its variants (1992–1993): Collor’s vice president and successor Itamar Franco — a gruff, honourable, politically uninspiring man whose most distinctive quality was a genuine absence of corruption — cycled through several Finance Ministers and several more failed plans before, in mid-1993, appointing a sociologist named Fernando Henrique Cardoso to the Finance Ministry. This appointment would change everything.

CHAPTER 8 — The Real Plan: Brazil’s Economic Revolution and the Lula Years (1994–2010)

The Most Brilliant Economic Plan in History

The economists who designed the Plano Real — the stabilization plan that finally slew Brazil’s inflation monster in 1994 — faced a problem that seemed genuinely unsolvable: every previous stabilization plan had failed, and its failure had made the next attempt harder by deepening public cynicism. How do you implement a credible new currency when the population has watched four new currencies fail in eight years?

The PUC-Rio economists who designed the plan — André Lara Resende, Pérsio Arida, Edmar Bacha, and their colleagues, working under Finance Minister Fernando Henrique Cardoso — answered this question with an insight of genuine originality.

Instead of launching a new currency and hoping it would be trusted (the approach of every previous plan), they would break the inflationary expectation mechanism from the inside before introducing the new currency. The tool: a virtual unit of account called the URV (Unidade Real de Valor — Real Unit of Value).

Beginning in March 1994, all prices, wages, and contracts in Brazil were expressed in both cruzeiros real (the existing currency) and URVs. The cruzeiro continued to inflate — the daily URV/cruzeiro exchange rate, updated by the central bank, reflected exactly the ongoing inflation. But expressed in URVs, all prices were stable. For several months, Brazilians observed that while their cruzeiro salaries were rising in nominal terms, their URV salaries were constant. While supermarket prices in cruzeiros rose daily, they were stable in URVs.

By the time the Real replaced both the cruzeiro and the URV on July 1, 1994, something extraordinary had happened: prices were already stable in the unit that would become the new currency. The psychological trick was the key: the inflationary expectation had been dismantled during the URV transition period rather than through a shock at the moment of currency introduction. When the Real was introduced at 1:1 with the URV, inflation did not spike — because there was no sudden loss of confidence in the new unit. It had already established its credibility over months.

The results were immediate and dramatic. Monthly inflation of 40–50% in June 1994 fell to 1.5% per month in July 1994 — after the Real’s introduction. By tightly controlling monetary policy and eliminating the indexation mechanisms (the URV’s replacement of cruzeiro indexation effectively broke the expectation spiral), the Real Plan reduced annual inflation from 2,500% in 1993 to 22% in 1995 — and to single digits by 1997. Millions of Brazilians who had spent years unable to save, plan, or defer consumption could suddenly do so. The formal consumer and credit economy erupted: tens of millions of Brazilians who had operated in cash because banks were useless during hyperinflation now opened bank accounts, took consumer credit, and made purchases on instalment plans.

Fernando Henrique Cardoso: The Reformer of Settled Minds

Fernando Henrique Cardoso — known as FHC — won the 1994 presidential election on the wave of Real Plan popularity and governed for two terms (1995–2002). He was an unlikely Brazilian president: an academic sociologist who had written one of Latin America’s most influential intellectual works (Dependency and Development in Latin America, co-authored with Enzo Faletto), who had spent years in exile during the military period, who was famous within Brazilian intellectual circles but entirely unknown to mass audiences before the Real Plan made him a national hero.

FHC’s presidential record was one of the most consequential in Brazilian history after Vargas:

Privatization: Telecomunicações Brasileiras (Telebrás), Vale do Rio Doce (the mining giant), and dozens of smaller state enterprises were sold to private investors, generating revenue, reducing the government’s fiscal burden, and — in telecoms — triggering an expansion in telephone penetration that transformed connectivity across Brazil within a decade.

Regulatory reform: Independent regulatory agencies — modeled partly on American examples — were created for electricity, telecommunications, oil and gas, water, and other sectors. This was a genuine institutional innovation: creating agencies with technical competence and political independence (or at least reduced political dependence) in a country where every regulatory decision had previously been subject to direct political interference.

Fiscal Responsibility Law (2000): The Lei de Responsabilidade Fiscal — requiring state and municipal governments to maintain fiscal balance, limiting debt accumulation, and establishing transparent reporting requirements — was the most important piece of institutional fiscal architecture Brazil had ever produced. It directly constrained the clientelistic fiscal behavior of state governors, who had historically spent freely and passed the debt problems to the federal government.

What FHC left undone: education quality (Brazil expanded access dramatically but quality remained poor), land reform (effectively abandoned to the political power of agribusiness), and the social programs needed to translate economic stability into poverty reduction at scale. These were the items on the agenda Lula would inherit.

Lula: The Worker Who Terrified and Then Reassured the World

Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva is one of modern history’s most remarkable political figures — a man whose biography reads like the kind of story that would be dismissed as implausible in a novel.

He was born in 1945 in Garanhuns, in the drought-stricken backlands of Pernambuco state — the seventh of eight children of a family so poor that they lived in a single room and shared their food with hunger. At age seven, his family joined the vast migration of nordestinos (Northeasterners) to São Paulo’s industrial belt. Lula, who had not completed elementary school, became a metalworker at age fourteen, lost a finger in a factory accident, became a union leader in the ABC industrial region of São Paulo in the 1970s, led the great strikes of 1978 and 1979 (the first major strikes of the military period, conducted in defiance of the law), co-founded the Workers’ Party (PT) in 1980, and ran for president in 1989, 1994, and 1998 — losing each time, in elections where his lack of formal education and his radical union background allowed opponents to cast him as a dangerous revolutionary.

By the time he won in 2002, Lula had transformed himself. The Carta ao Povo Brasileiro (Letter to the Brazilian People) that he published before the election was a strategic document of deliberate reassurance: Brazil would honour its international debt obligations; Petrobras and the Real would not be nationalised; the Central Bank would remain independent. Financial markets, which had driven up country risk spreads in anticipation of a Lula victory, were mollified. The revolutionary had accepted the constraints of governance.

And in governance, Lula was — by virtually any measure — the most successful Brazilian president in modern history.

Bolsa Família: The Programme That Changed a Country

The Bolsa Família (Family Grant) programme — expanded by Lula from a predecessor programme created under FHC — was conceptually simple and operationally transformative: the federal government paid a monthly stipend to Brazil’s poorest families, conditional on keeping their children attending school and fulfilling basic health requirements (vaccinations, prenatal care, growth monitoring).

The amounts were modest — R$50–150 per family per month at launch — but in the context of Brazil’s extreme poverty, transformative. A family living on R$100 per month whose income doubled because of Bolsa Família experienced a genuine improvement in nutritional status, in children’s school attendance, in healthcare utilization, and in their ability to plan consumption beyond the next meal. By 2010, Bolsa Família covered approximately 12.4 million families — roughly 50 million people — at an annual cost of approximately 0.5% of GDP: one of the most cost-effective poverty reduction programmes ever documented.

The Harvard economists Joshua Angrist and Alan Krueger, World Bank researchers, and independent economists across the political spectrum who studied Bolsa Família’s results agreed on the core finding: it lifted approximately 30 million Brazilians out of extreme poverty between 2003 and 2010. School enrollment in poor communities rose. Child malnutrition fell. The infant mortality rate — already declining before Lula, but declining faster after — reflected the programme’s health impact. It was, without qualification, one of the most successful targeted social programmes in the history of development economics.

India’s connection: India’s own Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT) system and the PM-KISAN programme draw explicitly on the Bolsa Família model. Conditional cash transfers — direct payments to poor families with behavioral requirements around education and health — are now the global template for poverty reduction, largely because of what Brazil’s Bolsa Família demonstrated was possible. This is Brazil’s most significant export to global development policy.

The Golden Decade and the Oil Temptation

The “golden decade” (2003–2010) represents the closest Brazil has come to fulfilling its potential. The numbers: GDP growth averaging 4% per year; unemployment falling from 12% to 5%; the middle class (by Brazil’s expanding income definition) growing by 40 million people; the Gini coefficient — for the first time in modern Brazilian history — falling measurably. Brazil was hailed as a BRIC miracle; Goldman Sachs economists who had coined the BRIC framework pointed to Brazil as proof that the emerging market growth story was real. Lula left office with an 83% approval rating — the highest recorded exit popularity for any Brazilian leader in the polling era.

The pre-salt oil discovery announced in 2006 — vast petroleum reservoirs beneath a salt layer under the Atlantic seabed, at depths previously beyond the reach of any drilling technology — appeared to be the final gift to a country already blessed beyond reason. Initial estimates of 50–100 billion barrels of recoverable oil suggested that Brazil could join the ranks of Saudi Arabia, Russia, and the Persian Gulf states as a major petro-state. Petrobras’s stock price soared. International banks competed to finance the exploration programme. Brazilian pride was justified.

What the oil discovery actually created was the conditions for the next catastrophe. The assumption — explicit in the legislation governing the pre-salt royalties — was that oil revenues would finance social spending, infrastructure, and education without the need for fiscal discipline. The political economy calculus shifted: if the oil was coming, there was less urgency to build the institutional mechanisms (efficient taxation, prudent expenditure, independent institutions) that resource-poor countries like South Korea had been forced to develop. The oil might be one of the worst things that ever happened to Brazil’s institutional development — not because of what it is, but because of what it promised.

The Paradox of the Succeeding Successor

Lula left office in 2010 with the highest recorded approval rating in Brazilian political history and hand-picked his successor: Dilma Rousseff, his Chief of Staff, a woman of remarkable personal courage (she had been a Marxist guerrilla in the 1960s resistance to the military dictatorship, been captured, and survived torture before devoting herself to the left-wing politics that eventually brought her to the PT) who had served competently as Energy Minister and then Chief of Staff under Lula. She was, by every political and personal measure, a formidable figure.

She was also profoundly unsuited for the presidency of the world’s eighth-largest economy entering a period of global headwinds. She had no training in economics, little interest in the macroeconomic framework that underpinned Brazil’s golden decade stability, and what her advisors would charitably describe as “strong views” on how the economy should function — views that combined the desenvolvimentista instincts she had inherited from the Brazilian left with a technocratic confidence in state direction that left little room for market signals.

Dilma’s “New Economic Matrix” (Nova Matriz Econômica) — the set of policies she implemented beginning in 2011 — was, in isolation, a coherent attempt to stimulate growth and reduce inequality through state mechanisms. Combined, it was catastrophic:

Tax breaks for favored industries (exempting manufacturers from payroll taxes to stimulate employment) reduced government revenue without demonstrably increasing employment — because the firms that received the breaks used them to improve margins rather than hire workers. Forced reduction of electricity prices — ordered by the government to reduce costs for industry and consumers — successfully reduced prices short-term and created an electricity sector that could not invest in new generation capacity, precipitating a generating crisis and ultimately a massive tariff increase that wiped out the initial saving and more. Subsidized credit through state banks (BNDES, Caixa Econômica Federal) expanded lending to favored sectors — particularly construction and manufacturing — at below-market rates, crowding out private sector lending and creating a credit bubble in politically connected sectors. Fuel price controls at Petrobras — the most fiscally devastating single decision — required Petrobras to sell domestic fuel below import parity, effectively subsidizing Brazilian consumers at Petrobras’s expense. Between 2011 and 2014, these controls cost Petrobras approximately $40 billion in cumulative losses — money it compensated for by borrowing heavily, destroying the company’s financial position.

The result: Brazil’s economy, which had grown at 7.5% in 2010 (Lula’s final year), grew at 2.7% in 2011, 1.9% in 2012, 3% in 2013, and then collapsed into recession in 2014. The fiscal surplus that Cardoso and Lula had carefully maintained — Brazil running primary surpluses (revenues exceeding non-interest spending) as a signal of fiscal responsibility — turned into a deficit. The deficit widened rapidly as the economy deteriorated. A crisis that had been building through policy errors became unmistakeable as the global commodity supercycle — the Chinese demand boom that had flooded Brazil with export revenue — reversed.

Operation Car Wash: The Corruption Machine Exposed

On March 17, 2014, federal police in Curitiba, Paraná arrested a black-market currency dealer named Alberto Youssef for money laundering. A routine financial crime case. But Youssef’s ledgers revealed a connection to a service station in Brasília — which led to a PT politician — which led to Petrobras executives — which led to something that investigators came to understand, over months, was not a simple kickback scheme but a systematized industrial-scale corruption machine of breathtaking scope.

The Petrobras kickback cartel functioned as follows: Brazil’s largest construction companies — Odebrecht (Latin America’s largest construction group), OAS, Camargo Corrêa, Andrade Gutierrez, and others — had formed an informal cartel. When Petrobras needed to contract major construction projects (refineries, pipelines, platforms), the cartel companies agreed in advance who would win each bid and at what price, eliminating genuine competition. They then built the bribe into the contract price: typically 1-3% of contract value flowed to Petrobras executives who facilitated the arrangement, and further percentages flowed to politicians (primarily PT politicians, though the investigation ultimately implicated politicians from virtually every major party) who provided the political protection that allowed the scheme to continue.

The scale was extraordinary. Federal prosecutors eventually estimated that $2–5 billion had been diverted from Petrobras over at least a decade. Petrobras’s total losses — including the subsidy losses from Dilma’s fuel price controls and the asset write-downs from projects whose costs had been artificially inflated — reached $17 billion in 2014–2015 write-downs: the largest in any Latin American company’s history. The company that Vargas had built as a symbol of national sovereignty had been systematically looted by a consortium of the country’s largest companies in collaboration with the politicians who were supposed to oversee it.

Odebrecht’s confessions — extracted through plea agreements with prosecutors — extended the scandal well beyond Brazil. The company admitted to paying bribes in 12 countries: Peru, Argentina, Colombia, Venezuela, Mexico, Ecuador, Panama, Dominican Republic, Guatemala, Angola, Mozambique, and the United States. Odebrecht was simultaneously the most corrupt and the most globally connected construction company in the world — a achievement that required, and received, extraordinary levels of systematic political protection across three continents.

Judge Sérgio Moro and Brazil’s Greatest Legal Drama

The Lava Jato (Car Wash) investigation was led, at the federal level in Curitiba, by Judge Sérgio Moro — a federal judge from Paraná who became, within two years, the most famous and most controversial figure in Brazilian public life.

Moro, 41 at the investigation’s start, had written a master’s thesis on the Italian Mani Pulite (Clean Hands) anti-corruption investigation of the early 1990s and had spent years studying how to break corruption networks through judicial cooperation, plea agreements, and the sequencing of charges to generate progressively higher-level confessions. In Car Wash, he applied this learning with extraordinary energy and equally extraordinary willingness to push the procedural boundaries of Brazilian criminal law.

The drama of Car Wash peaked with the prosecution of Lula himself — who was accused on the basis of plea agreement testimony of receiving as a bribe a triplex apartment on the coast of São Paulo that had been renovated by OAS (one of the cartel companies) and informally made available to the Lula family. Lula denied ownership; he had never held the title. Moro convicted him on the evidence of the benefit received and sentenced him to nine years in prison.

Lula’s conviction in July 2017, his appeal dismissal in January 2018, and his imprisonment in April 2018 — proceeding to jail voluntarily after a tense standoff in which his supporters (metalworkers from his ABC São Paulo base) attempted to physically prevent his incarceration — was one of the most dramatic episodes in Brazilian political history. A former president, the most popular leader in the country’s democratic history, convicted and imprisoned.

The controversy: Lula’s lawyers and his PT supporters argued — and a Supreme Court investigation later confirmed — that Moro had conducted private messages with the lead prosecutor, sharing tactical information about the investigation in ways that raised serious questions about his impartiality. The Supreme Court eventually ruled that Moro had been biased in the Lula case, voiding the conviction and restoring Lula’s political rights. Moro, for his part, had by then resigned as a judge and served as Minister of Justice in Bolsonaro’s government — a decision that seemed, in retrospect, to lend weight to arguments that his prosecutorial choices had been politically motivated.

The honest assessment: Brazil’s corruption was real and enormous — Car Wash exposed genuine crimes by real perpetrators who genuinely diverted public funds for private benefit. And the investigation was conducted with real procedural problems that raised real questions of fairness and political bias. Both things are true simultaneously — which is precisely the uncomfortable complexity of Brazil’s anti-corruption experience.

Dilma’s Impeachment: Reform or Coup?

On August 31, 2016, Brazil’s Senate voted 61-20 to remove Dilma Rousseff from the presidency — the second presidential impeachment in Brazilian history, less than 25 years after the first. The ostensible legal basis: pedaladas fiscais (fiscal pedaling) — a practice in which the Dilma government delayed transfers to state banks for social programmes they financed, effectively borrowing from the banks to delay recognizing the federal deficit. The Senate ruled this violated the Fiscal Responsibility Law.

The debate over whether this impeachment was a constitutional exercise of legitimate legislative oversight or a parliamentary coup (golpe) remains genuinely unresolved — and the unresolved quality is itself revealing. The pedaladas practice was not invented by Dilma; previous governments had engaged in similar budget management. The legal standard applied to her had not been applied to her predecessors. The congressional forces that voted to remove her were predominantly men facing their own corruption charges or politically motivated by the PT’s removal from power after 13 years. Dilma herself had not been charged with personal enrichment.

On the other hand: Brazil’s constitution did grant the legislature impeachment authority, a congressional majority voted for it, and courts did not overturn it. The process was constitutionally legal even if politically motivated. Brazil’s constitutional text does not specify that impeachment requires personal corruption — fiscal violations are listed as impeachable offenses.

The truth is uncomfortable: Dilma’s government had economically devastating effects that resulted in genuine popular suffering; she probably deserved to lose the next election. Whether she deserved to be removed through a process that looked substantially like a political vehicle for her opponents’ ambitions rather than a judicial evaluation of her fiscal practices is a question that Brazilian legal scholars still debate.

The Temer Interregnum: Necessary Changes, Zero Legitimacy

Michel Temer — Dilma’s Vice President, a 75-year-old São Paulo political veteran whose PMDB party had been part of Dilma’s governing coalition before joining the impeachment coalition — assumed the presidency under circumstances that left him with essentially no popular support. His approval rating never exceeded 5%. He faced no election, had no democratic mandate, and is remembered primarily as Brazil’s most unpopular president.

Yet Temer’s government implemented one measure that was both genuinely necessary and politically courageous: the Teto de Gastos — the 20-year constitutional spending cap, approved in December 2016, which froze federal government spending in real terms for two decades. This was the most significant fiscal constraint adopted by any Latin American government in the democratic era — and it was deeply controversial, accused of constitutionally locking in austerity that would prevent social investment.

The defence of the spending cap: Brazil’s fiscal trajectory under Dilma had been genuinely catastrophic, with deficit spending accelerating toward 10% of GDP at its worst. Without a credible mechanism to return to fiscal balance, Brazil faced a debt crisis that would have imposed far more suffering than the spending cap’s constraints. The cap was a blunt instrument for a real problem.

Temer himself left office in January 2019 under investigation for corruption — a fitting, if depressing, coda to the period.

CHAPTER 10 — Bolsonaro, Lula’s Return, and the Unresolved Question (2018–Present)

The Wave That Made a Bolsonaro Possible

By October 2018, when Jair Bolsonaro won the Brazilian presidency with 55% of the vote against PT candidate Fernando Haddad, Brazil was a country in a specific psychological state: exhausted, furious, and longing for a rupture with the political class that had, in the preceding years, produced Dilma’s economic catastrophe, Lava Jato’s parade of corruption convictions, the worst economic recession in Brazilian recorded history, and an unprecedented security crisis (Brazil had been recording over 60,000 homicides per year — more than any other country on Earth — with the victims concentrated overwhelmingly in the favelas and peripheries of major cities).

Bolsonaro — a retired army captain who had spent 27 years as a backbench congressman in Rio de Janeiro, famous mainly for outrageous statements about women, gay people, and indigenous communities, and for an enthusiastic nostalgia for the military dictatorship — would have seemed, a decade earlier, impossible as president of a major democracy. His candidacy required the simultaneous alignment of several factors:

The evangelical Christian vote: Bolsonaro’s embrace of conservative evangelical Christianity gave him access to one of Brazil’s fastest-growing political blocs — evangelical Protestants who constitute approximately 30% of the Brazilian population (up from 15% in 2000) and who vote with unusual discipline for politicians who speak their moral vocabulary. The cultural anxiety around gender, family, sexuality, and values that drives evangelical political mobilization globally had its Brazilian expression in Bolsonaro’s candidacy.

The anti-PT anger: Thirteen years of PT governments, ending in economic catastrophe and the largest corruption scandal in Latin American history, had produced in large sections of the Brazilian middle class a visceral rejection of Lula, PT, and anything associated with them — including, eventually, governance norms those voters might otherwise have defended.

The social media revolution: Bolsonaro’s campaign was conducted substantially through WhatsApp groups and YouTube — platforms that could circumvent Brazil’s traditional media, distribute misinformation at scale, and mobilize supporters who felt that the legacy media ignored or dismissed them.

Paulo Guedes and the Incomplete Liberal Revolution

Bolsonaro’s great economic appointment — the one that gave his candidacy credibility with financial markets and economically liberal voters — was Paulo Guedes, a University of Chicago-trained economist who promised a comprehensive liberal transformation of Brazil: privatization of hundreds of state enterprises, a complete tax reform simplifying Brazil’s notoriously complex system, pension reform, financial market deregulation, and a dramatic reduction in the state’s role in the economy.

What Guedes actually delivered was partial but not trivial. The pension reform of November 2019 — raising retirement ages, increasing contribution requirements, and eliminating several special privileges for public sector workers — was the most significant fiscal reform in Brazilian history, projected to save R$800 billion over a decade and to address the most explosive element of Brazil’s long-term fiscal challenge. It was politically courageous (pension reform in any democracy is a political minefield, since every beneficiary of the existing system is a motivated voter against change) and fiscally necessary.

Beyond pension reform, the liberal revolution stalled. Bolsonaro’s political instincts — which were nationalist, protectionist, and focused on satisfying his political base rather than reforming the economy — consistently conflicted with Guedes’s market-liberal agenda. State banks continued to operate at scale. Privatizations were slow and selective. The comprehensive tax reform remained perpetually “almost ready.” The deregulation that Brazil’s business environment desperately needed made marginal progress against the Brazilian bureaucratic resistance to change.

COVID: A President’s Choice to Let Brazil Die

The COVID-19 pandemic arrived in Brazil in February 2020 and found a president uniquely, almost deliberately, unsuited to managing it.

Bolsonaro’s response to COVID-19 was not merely inadequate — it was actively counterproductive. He called the virus “uma gripezinha” (a little flu). He appeared at anti-lockdown rallies, unmasked, shaking hands with supporters. He fired his first health minister for recommending social distancing measures; his second health minister resigned for the same reason; the third was a general with no medical training. He repeatedly promoted hydroxychloroquine and ivermectin — drugs with no demonstrated efficacy against COVID-19 — as treatments, pressuring the health system to prescribe them. He actively delayed vaccine procurement, allowing negotiations with Pfizer to collapse multiple times while dismissing concerns about the Coronavac (Chinese-developed, Butantan-institute-produced) vaccine as politically motivated.

Brazil’s COVID death toll: over 700,000 deaths — the second-highest absolute death count in the world after the United States, and one of the highest per-capita death rates among major nations. The victims were not evenly distributed: Afro-Brazilians, favela residents, workers in the informal economy who could not isolate, and the poor elderly without access to the private healthcare system that buffered wealthier Brazilians — these were the populations that absorbed the disproportionate burden of a deliberately mismanaged public health response.

The economic consequences: GDP contracted 4.1% in 2020. Unemployment rose above 14%. The informal economy — where approximately 40% of Brazilian workers earn their living — was devastated. The fiscal cost of the emergency assistance programmes (auxílio emergencial) that the government reluctantly adopted, under congressional pressure, after initially resisting, was substantial — but so was the economic stabilisation they provided, and the political effect: the cash transfers that reached 68 million Brazilians during 2020 paradoxically boosted Bolsonaro’s approval ratings among the very poor, even as his health response devastated their communities.

Lula’s Extraordinary Return

The story of Lula’s 2022 presidential campaign is, by any measure, one of the more remarkable political restorations in democratic history.

Between April 2018 and November 2019, Lula sat in a prison cell in Curitiba, reading, writing, receiving visitors, and demonstrating the political resilience that had defined his extraordinary career. The Supreme Court’s November 2019 ruling preventing the imprisonment of defendants whose appeals had not been fully exhausted opened Lula’s cell. In March 2021, Justice Edson Fachin ruled that the Curitiba court had lacked jurisdiction to try Lula — annulling all of his convictions and restoring his political rights. Subsequent Supreme Court rulings found Moro to have acted with bias. Lula was — legally if not in the eyes of everyone — cleared.

He announced his candidacy for the October 2022 election at age 76 — the oldest candidate for the Brazilian presidency in democratic history. His running mate was the former governor of São Paulo Geraldo Alckmin, a center-right politician and former PSDB member who was Lula’s opponent in 2006 — a coalition-building choice that signaled Lula’s pragmatic willingness to build the broadest possible coalition against Bolsonaro.

The election result: Lula won with 50.9% against Bolsonaro’s 49.1% — the narrowest margin in Brazilian presidential history, but a margin nonetheless. The closeness of the result was simultaneously a testament to Lula’s political resilience and a warning: nearly half of Brazil voted for a president who had dismissed COVID as a flu, promoted misinformation, and expressed nostalgia for dictatorship. Brazil was divided almost exactly in half — between two irreconcilable political visions, with entirely different understandings of recent history, different media consumption, and different frameworks for understanding what Brazil’s problems were and who was responsible for them.

January 8, 2023: Democracy Tested

On January 8, 2023, one week after Lula was inaugurated, thousands of Bolsonaro supporters — mobilized by social media claiming the election had been stolen — stormed and vandalized the three centers of Brazilian democratic power: the Presidential Palace, the National Congress, and the Supreme Court. Windows were smashed, art was destroyed, furniture was burned. The images — mobs in yellow-and-green Brazilian football shirts ransacking the architectural symbols of the republic — were unmistakeable in their echo of January 6, 2021 in Washington DC.

The attack was repelled within hours. Bolsonaro’s justice minister and governors of PSDB-governed states responded effectively once it became clear that the federal government intended to respond. Lula, who was in São Paulo, declared a federal intervention in security in the federal district. Over 1,500 participants were arrested. The military, which under Bolsonaro had been a politically sympathetic audience, showed no inclination to support the rioters.

January 8, 2023 demonstrated that Brazilian democracy — though genuinely threatened — had developed sufficient institutional strength to survive an assault that would have succeeded in many other countries. The Supreme Court’s independence, the existing legal framework, and ultimately the absence of military support for the coup attempt were the decisive factors. Whether this resilience is a temporary feature of the current institutional alignment or a permanent characteristic of Brazilian democracy is the most important open question about Brazil’s political future.

CHAPTER 11 — The Eternal Question: Why Brazil? And Is There a Way Out?

The Hardest Question in Development Economics

After 500 years of history, after extraordinary natural endowments and extraordinary cultural creativity, after real economic achievements (the Real Plan, Bolsa Família, the Embrapa agricultural revolution) and repeated catastrophic failures (hyperinflation, the military debt crisis, the Petrobras looting, the Dilma recession), the central question remains: Why does Brazil keep failing to fulfill its extraordinary potential?

The honest answer requires holding five structural explanations simultaneously:

1. The Colonial Inheritance: The Long Shadow of the Plantation

Brazil entered independence in 1822 with three structural inheritances bequeathed by 322 years of Portuguese colonialism, and 200 years of subsequent history have never fully dismantled any of them.

Extreme land concentration: Brazil’s latifundio system — the distribution of enormous land grants to a tiny elite in the colonial period — created a land ownership pattern so unequal that it required, and received, only cosmetic modification through two centuries of independence. The Brazilian agriculture sector remains dominated by large farms; the MST (Landless Workers’ Movement) has campaigned for land reform for 40 years, producing limited progress against the political power of the bancada ruralista (agricultural bloc) in Congress, which has been one of the most consistently powerful legislative forces in every Brazilian government of the democratic era.

Racial inequality as structural feature: The 4.9 million enslaved Africans who built Brazil’s colonial economy were emancipated in 1888 without land, compensation, education, or civic infrastructure. The racial hierarchy their enslavement created — Afro-Brazilians at the bottom of every economic and social indicator — was never systematically dismantled. Brazil’s racial inequality today is not an anomaly; it is the predictable result of extreme extraction followed by emancipation without reconstruction, in a society that for most of its history denied that race was even a relevant category (the myth of democracia racial — racial democracy — held that Brazil had transcended racial division, a comfortable fiction that protected the status quo from even naming the problem).

Elite accommodation as political culture: Every Brazilian political transition of consequence — independence (1822), abolition and republic (1888–1889), Vargas’s rise (1930), the military coup (1964), the return to democracy (1985) — preserved the essential interests of the landowning, commercial, and later industrial elite while changing the formal governing arrangements. Brazil has never had a revolution that genuinely redistributed power; it has had a succession of elite-managed transitions that changed who ran the state without fundamentally changing whose interests the state protected.

2. Institutional Weakness: The Capture That Prevents the Rule of Law

Brazil has excellent institutions on paper. Its constitution is admirably comprehensive, its legal system is formally sophisticated, its independent courts have (recently, with Car Wash and the January 8 response) demonstrated genuine capacity for independent action. But Brazil’s institutions have been repeatedly and systematically captured by the interests they were supposed to regulate:

Regulatory capture: The pattern of amakudari — the revolving door between regulatory positions and industry — has its Brazilian equivalent. Regulators who will later work in the industries they regulate have compromised incentives for rigorous oversight. Banks too big to fail, construction companies too politically connected to prosecute, agricultural exporters whose land titles would never be disturbed — the list of institutional captures in Brazil is long and consistent.

Political capture of the judiciary: The Brazilian Supreme Court (Supremo Tribunal Federal, STF) — eleven justices appointed by the President, confirmed by the Senate, serving until they turn 70 — is a body whose composition reflects the political preferences of the governments that appointed its members. This is a systemic design feature that has made the STF simultaneously the most important institutional actor in Brazilian democracy and a perpetually contested arena of political conflict. The Lula-vs-Moro legal drama was conducted with the STF as referee and ultimate arbiter — with the partisan valence of the court itself a constant subject of political accusation.

The custo Brasil (Brazil Cost): Brazil’s business regulation is, by every measurement, among the most complex and burdensome in the world — a thicket of overlapping federal, state, and municipal taxes (Brazil has approximately 60 different taxes, some applied simultaneously, some interacting in ways that require specialist lawyers to navigate), labour regulations of extraordinary rigidity, and bureaucratic requirements that consume resources and incentivize informality. The custo Brasil is not the result of malice; it is the accumulated product of decades of legislation in which every new provision was designed to protect someone’s interest — and the cumulative weight crushes the formal economy while driving business into informality.

3. The Inflation Psychology: The Scars That Never Fully Healed

Three decades of hyperinflation left psychological and behaviorual imprints on Brazilian society that no stabilization plan could immediately erase.

The distrust of long-term planning: A generation of Brazilians who learned that any financial commitment could be destroyed by inflation brought that psychology with them into the stable economy after 1994. Business investment timelines remained shorter than rational models would predict. Personal saving rates remained below potential. Consumer credit — while expanding dramatically after the Real — carried an implicit suspicion of long-term commitment that reflected accurate historical experience.

The demand for immediacy in government: Populations who experienced hyperinflation’s destruction of the future’s value develop a rational preference for immediate benefits over long-term investment. Politicians who promise concrete, immediate transfers (Bolsa Família, auxílio emergencial) are more credible to people who don’t trust that the promised future benefit will actually be delivered — because their experience is that the future doesn’t hold its value. This rational psychology makes it harder for Brazilian politicians to build coalitions for the patient investments — in education quality, infrastructure, institutional development — that pay off only in years or decades.

4. The Commodity Trap: When Natural Wealth Defers Necessary Reform

Brazil’s extraordinary natural abundance has repeatedly created the political economy conditions for deferred reform. The mechanism is subtle but powerful:

When commodity prices are high — when Chinese demand is flooding Brazil with soybean and iron ore revenue, when oil prices are making the pre-salt fields spectacularly valuable — the immediate fiscal pressure to reform diminishes. The clientelistic political machine can be sustained with natural resource revenues without requiring the kind of institutional development (efficient taxation of non-resource sectors, productivity growth, knowledge economy investment) that resource-poor countries must force themselves to achieve. South Korea had no oil, no iron ore, no agricultural giant’s scale; it was compelled to build institutions that could generate growth from human capital and technology. Brazil, perpetually endowed with the next commodity boom, could perpetually defer that compelled institutional building.

When commodity prices fall — as they did in 2014–2016 — the fiscal crisis they create is so acute that political stability is insufficient to implement the deep structural reforms that the boom period deferred. Every commodity bust in Brazilian history (1896 coffee crash, 1929 depression, 1979 oil shock, 2015 commodity reversal) has produced political crisis rather than institutional reform — because reform requires political coalitions that the crisis itself destroys.

5. Inequality’s Permanent Veto: The Self-Reinforcing Trap

Brazil’s extraordinary inequality — a Gini coefficient of approximately 0.53, one of the highest among major economies and unchanged in its essential magnitude for a century — is not merely an outcome of Brazil’s development path. It is an active constraint on its future.

A small, wealthy elite with every incentive to preserve the system: the 1% of Brazilians who control 48% of the country’s wealth have enormous political resources to deploy in defense of the status quo — land tenure, tax arrangements (Brazil taxes wages much more heavily than capital and inheritance), judicial treatment (wealthy defendants can afford lawyers capable of stretching proceedings far beyond what ordinary defendants have access to), and political access. Their incentive to accept the institutional reforms that would genuinely level Brazil’s playing field is, rationally, close to zero.

A large, poor majority with every incentive to support populist redistribution over patient institution-building: the 40% of Brazil’s 215 million people who live in or near poverty have experienced repeated betrayals by institutions that were supposed to serve them — court systems that favoured the wealthy, labour regulations that protected formal sector insiders over informal workers, governments whose benefits went to political allies rather than the needy. Their rational response is to support leaders who promise immediate, concrete, accessible transfers — Lula’s Bolsa Família, Bolsonaro’s auxílio emergencial — rather than patient institutional reform whose benefits are distant and uncertain.

The result: Brazilian democracy is trapped between a minority with the power to block reform and a majority with the immediate preference (rationally derived from its experience) for redistribution over institutional development. Both sides are behaving rationally. The institutional equilibrium that results is one in which neither genuine redistribution nor genuine reform occurs with sufficient consistency to escape the trap.

What Brazil Has Actually Achieved: The Underappreciated Victories

Any honest account of Brazil must balance the structural failure narrative with the genuine achievements that offer real evidence of what is possible:

The Real Plan (1994): Slaying a 30-year hyperinflationary monster that had devastated the savings of multiple generations — without a depression, without mass unemployment, through intellectual creativity and institutional design — is among the greatest acts of economic governance in the developing world’s history.

Bolsa Família (2003–present): The programme that proved conditional cash transfers at scale could reduce extreme poverty while preserving fiscal discipline. Its lessons have been adopted in 61 countries. Brazil gave the world its most effective poverty reduction framework.

Embrapa and the Cerrado Revolution: The Empresa Brasileira de Pesquisa Agropecuária (Brazilian Agricultural Research Corporation) — established by the military government in 1973, funded through the subsequent democratic era — is one of the great scientific institutions in the history of applied research. Its agronomists and soil scientists took the cerrado — the vast central savannah, previously dismissed as infertile scrubland with acidic soils incapable of supporting commercial agriculture — and over 30 years transformed it into the world’s most productive agricultural frontier through lime application, soil chemistry research, and the development of tropical soybean varieties that could harness the region’s sun and rainfall. The cerrado today produces 60% of Brazil’s agricultural output. This transformation — fundamentally achieved by Brazilian public science — is one of the greatest agricultural achievements of the 20th century, and unlike the Green Revolution of the 1960s, it is largely unknown outside Brazil.

Embraer: Founded in 1969 as a state company, privatised in 1994 during FHC’s reform era, Embraer has become the world’s third-largest commercial aircraft manufacturer, competing directly with Bombardier of Canada and increasingly with Chinese COMAC. The company’s E-Jet series — regional jets from 70 to 130 seats — conquered regional airline fleets globally on the basis of genuine engineering excellence and cost efficiency. Embraer proved that Brazilian technological capacity, combined with appropriate institutional support and eventually private sector discipline, can produce world-class industrial outcomes. It is the counter-argument to every claim that Brazil cannot compete in advanced manufacturing.

Brazil and India: The Mirror Conversation

The Indian professional reading this report will have noticed, throughout, the uncomfortable frequency with which Brazil’s story rhymes with India’s own.

The colonial inheritance: India emerged from British colonialism in 1947 with extreme land concentration, racial/caste hierarchy written into the social structure, and institutional frameworks designed primarily for extraction and control rather than development and inclusion. India’s caste system and Brazil’s racial hierarchy are different in form and different in the specific inequalities they produce, but share the essential quality of inherited hierarchy that each subsequent generation must actively work to dismantle — and that has proven, in both countries, extraordinarily durable.

The commodity temptation: India in the 21st century faces its own version of Brazil’s commodity trap — not in agricultural exports but in the political temptation to finance social programmes from fiscal expansion rather than institutional reform, and in the preference for short-term electoral payoffs over long-term structural investment. India’s MNREGA (rural employment guarantee), its farm loan waivers, its fuel subsidies — these are not Brazil’s Bolsa Família or the coffee valorization schemes in content, but they share the structural dynamic of immediate political logic overriding long-term institutional development.

The inequality that makes reform hard: India’s extreme vertical inequality (the richest 1% hold approximately 40% of national wealth — approaching Brazil’s historical levels) and its horizontal inequalities (caste, religion, region, gender) create a political economy in which reform coalitions are similarly difficult to build and similarly easily fractured by identity mobilization that substitutes cultural solidarity for economic class interest.

Where India has done better: India’s success in building technically capable institutions (the IITs, the RBI, the Election Commission, the Supreme Court, the ISRO) — institutions that, whatever their political constraints, have accumulated genuine technical expertise and institutional identity — represents an advantage over Brazil’s comparable institutions, which have more consistently been captured by political appointment and patronage. India’s engineering education system, despite its enormous quality variance, has produced a technical workforce that has driven genuine export achievement in software and pharmaceutical manufacturing. Brazil’s education system — particularly its public schools — has chronically underperformed, with PISA scores consistently among the lowest in Latin America.

What India should not do: Brazil demonstrates with extraordinary clarity the cost of deferred institutional reform during commodity booms — and India’s current coal, steel, and tech boom years are precisely the moment to build the institutions (regulatory independence, judicial efficiency, land title systems that protect the poor as well as the wealthy, labour regulations that include rather than exclude informal workers) that the boom’s political goodwill enables and that the inevitable bust will require.

The Way Out: Is There One?

The honest answer to whether Brazil has a path out of its development trap is: yes, but it requires things Brazilian politics has consistently failed to sustain.

Brazil’s successful episodes all share the same characteristics: technical competence insulated from political interference (the Real Plan team had genuine autonomy from political pressure during the design phase), institutional design that constrains future governments (the Fiscal Responsibility Law, the spending cap, the Central Bank independence formalized in 2021), and social investment that builds human capital rather than just distributing transfers (Bolsa Família’s conditionality was a mechanism for building education and health capital alongside the immediate poverty reduction).

The enemy of all three — in Brazil as in every developing democracy — is the electoral short-term horizon combined with the inequality that makes patient institution-building politically harder to sustain than immediate redistribution.

Lula’s third government (2023–present) faces this challenge with full clarity: he has inherited a fiscal position that requires discipline, a political coalition that requires accommodation, a commodity export sector that provides revenue but also creates the familiar deferral temptation, and a divided country in which every reform generates immediately mobilized opposition from the half that voted for Bolsonaro and views any Lula initiative as politically suspect.

Whether this government — or any subsequent Brazilian government — can sustain the multi-decade institutional consistency that genuine development requires is the central open question. History counsels modesty about the answer.

Saudade: The Metaphor Brazil Earned

Saudade — the Portuguese and Brazilian word that has no precise equivalent in any other language — describes a longing for something beautiful that is absent, a nostalgic ache for something that may never have fully existed, a melancholic tenderness toward a beloved thing that is just out of reach. It is the emotion of the sailor who has been away from home for years and feels, simultaneously, the memory of the home’s warmth and the awareness of the sea’s distance.

It is also, improbably and precisely, the most accurate available metaphor for Brazil’s development story.

The Brazil of the country’s deepest imagination — abundant, generous, creative, just, equal, its football’s jogo bonito extended to the full social life — is real in the aspiration, in the carnival’s annual enactment, in the warmth of Brazilian social culture, in the extraordinary beauty of the land and its people. What is just out of reach is not the imagination but the institutions that would make the imagination permanent, that would transform the carnival’s egalitarian joy into a daily social arrangement rather than a temporary exception to the hierarchy.

The carnival comes every year. The music plays continuously. The football is genuinely beautiful. The country’s saudade — its longing for its own better version — is not an illusion or a self-deception. It is an accurate perception of what Brazil could be, combined with an historically justified uncertainty about whether Brazil’s politics will finally deliver what its potential promises.

Charles de Gaulle was wrong. Brazil is not not-serious. It is the most serious country in the world to study — precisely because its failures are the failures of extraordinary potential meeting inadequate institutions, of enormous creativity meeting structural inheritance, of real achievement meeting stubborn obstacles that every developing democracy must recognize and, over time, overcome.

The 21st century has given Brazil tools it lacked in previous centuries: a generation shaped by Bolsa Família’s education conditionality, a Central Bank whose independence is now legally entrenched, a civil society robust enough to bring down Car Wash’s corruption networks, a digital connectivity that reaches favelas where traditional institutions never arrived. These are not guarantees. They are ingredients.

The eternal country of the future has, for the first time, reason to believe that the future might arrive on schedule.
