🏝️ The Silicon Shield: How a Tiny Island Became the Brain of the Modern World

Taiwan’s Extraordinary Journey from Colonial Backwater to Semiconductor Superpower

Hold a modern smartphone chip between your fingers. It is smaller than your thumbnail. It contains approximately 19 billion transistors — more computing elements than there are stars in the Milky Way galaxy. Each transistor is 3 nanometres wide: smaller than most viruses, one-thousandth the width of a human hair. The machines that made it cost $200 million each and can only be built by one company on Earth. And this chip — the most complex manufactured object in human history — was very likely made on a mountainous island of 23 million people, 180 kilometres off the coast of China, in a narrow strait that could become the most dangerous waterway in the world.

The island is Taiwan. The company is TSMC. And the story of how this happened — how survival pressure, visionary leadership, educated emigrants, one revolutionary business insight, and three generations of engineering genius came together on this particular island to manufacture the brain of modern civilization — is one of the most astonishing national transformations in human history.

This is that story.

CHAPTER 1 — The Island at the Center of the World: Taiwan’s Geography and Its Destiny

The Map That Explains Everything

Draw a map of the global semiconductor supply chain — the chain that produces every chip in every device in every country on Earth — and at its center you will find an island that almost nobody learns about in school.

Taiwan is 394 kilometres long and 144 kilometres wide. It sits in the Western Pacific, separated from the southeastern coast of China by the 180-kilometre Taiwan Strait. It is mountainous, typhoon-prone, earthquake-prone, and almost entirely devoid of natural resources. There is no oil beneath its waters, no iron ore in its mountains, no coal of any significance, no minerals of particular industrial value. Geographers studying only its physical endowments would predict a small, poor island economy — fishing, perhaps some agriculture in its western coastal plains, tourism if it’s lucky.

Instead: TSMC manufactures over 90% of the world’s most advanced semiconductors here. Without Taiwan, there are no iPhone processors, no AI training chips, no jet fighter avionics, no electric vehicle controllers, no advanced satellite systems, no modern medical imaging equipment. The Taiwanese semiconductor industry is so integral to global civilization that economists have constructed a specific term — the Silicon Shield — for the geopolitical protection that this indispensability provides against the 1.4-billion-person nuclear power that claims Taiwan as its own territory.

How did this happen? The answer begins not with technology but with geography, history, and the extraordinary sequence of catastrophes and decisions that shaped one of the most distinctive peoples on Earth.

The Long Before: Aboriginal Taiwan and the Waves of Colonialism

Long before Chinese settlers, Dutch colonizers, or Japanese administrators arrived, Taiwan was home to the Austronesian peoples — ancestors of the same linguistic and cultural family that eventually populated the Philippines, Malaysia, Indonesia, Polynesia, and ultimately Hawaii and New Zealand. Linguistic and genetic evidence suggests Taiwan may have been the origin point of Austronesian dispersal across the Pacific — one of the greatest maritime migrations in human history, launched from this island’s shores thousands of years before Europeans learned to navigate the open sea.

These aboriginal peoples — today recognized in 16 official indigenous groups — occupied Taiwan’s mountains and eastern coast for millennia, sustaining sophisticated cultures based on hunting, agriculture, and inter-village trade. They represent Taiwan’s deepest continuous human heritage, a fact increasingly acknowledged in Taiwan’s democratic political culture.

The Dutch period (1624–1662) brought the island’s first encounter with colonial mercantile capitalism. The Dutch East India Company established a trading post at Tainan, developing the island as an entrepôt in the broader Asian spice and silk trade. They encouraged Han Chinese immigration from Fujian Province to work sugar and deer hide plantations — beginning the demographic transformation that would eventually make Taiwan predominantly ethnically Chinese. The Dutch brought bookkeeping, plantation agriculture, and the first systematic cartography of the island. They also brought smallpox, which devastated indigenous communities. Colonial modernity, in its most ambivalent form.

Koxinga (Zheng Chenggong, 1624–1662) — son of a Chinese pirate lord and a Japanese mother, raised on loyalty to the Ming dynasty that the Manchu Qing conquerors had just overthrown — arrived at Taiwan in 1661 with 25,000 soldiers, expelled the Dutch after a nine-month siege, and used the island as a base for his failed counter-offensive against the Qing on the mainland. Koxinga died in 1662, his dream of restoring the Ming unfulfilled, but his legacy was enormous: he had established Taiwan as a refuge for those unwilling to accept the mainland’s political transformation — a role the island would play again, with world-historic consequences, three centuries later.

The Qing dynasty incorporated Taiwan in 1683 — reluctantly, and only because the strategic threat of a hostile base on a nearby island was intolerable. For two centuries, the Qing administrators viewed Taiwan as a distant, difficult frontier — a source of sugar and camphor, plagued by malaria and indigenous resistance, not worth serious investment. The island’s relative neglect during this period is historically significant: Taiwan did not accumulate the deep Confucian mandarin administrative traditions of mainland China’s core provinces. This would later make it more amenable to the kind of institutional innovation that economic transformation requires.

The Japanese Paradox: Colonial Infrastructure and Its Bitter Legacy

In 1895, the Qing dynasty lost the First Sino-Japanese War and was forced to cede Taiwan to Japan in the Treaty of Shimonoseki. China had owned Taiwan for two centuries without particularly wanting it; Japan acquired it and immediately began developing it with the systematic intensity of a modern industrial state.

The Japanese colonial period (1895–1945) is one of the most paradoxical episodes in Taiwan’s history, and understanding its paradox is essential to understanding how Taiwan was positioned for the economic transformation that followed.

The brutality was real: the Japanese military suppressed Taiwanese resistance with summary executions. Taiwanese were treated as second-class subjects, denied the political rights of Japanese citizens, and subjected to racial discrimination in education, employment, and public life. Indigenous resistance movements — particularly the Wushe Incident of 1930, in which Seediq tribespeople killed 134 Japanese at a sports event and were subsequently hunted to near-extinction — were crushed with overwhelming force. Colonial rule was authoritarian, often violent, and premised on Japanese racial superiority.

The infrastructure investment was also real, and it was extraordinary by East Asian colonial standards. The Japanese built Taiwan’s north-south railway backbone (still the core of Taiwan’s rail network today). They established an island-wide electricity grid, clean water systems, and a public health infrastructure that reduced the malaria death rate from catastrophic to manageable. They created a modern agricultural extension system — bringing the techniques of Japanese scientific agriculture to Taiwanese farmers, dramatically increasing rice and sugar yields. They built schools — though the curriculum emphasized Japanese language and culture — and by 1945 had achieved literacy rates significantly higher than most of Asia.

Most importantly, the Japanese left Taiwan with a functioning modern administrative infrastructure — customs, taxation, land registration, public health, banking — that had been built by pragmatic colonial administrators motivated by the desire to extract sustainable economic value from their territory. The British in India built railways to extract resources; the Japanese in Taiwan built institutions to manage a productive colony. The outcome, in terms of the physical and organisational foundation for later development, was similar: the colonised people were made less poor, while being made decisively less free.

When Japan was defeated in 1945, it left Taiwan with railway tracks, hospital buildings, irrigation systems, and administrative habits that made it one of Asia’s more developed territories by the standards of that devastated era. The colonial experience had been unjust; its material legacy was, paradoxically, valuable. The economy and society that the next set of rulers would inherit was not a blank slate but a platform — damaged, traumatised, politically contested, but developmentally functional.

1947: The Wound That Never Healed

Taiwan’s return to Chinese rule in 1945 — administered by Chiang Kai-shek’s Nationalist (KMT) government — began with celebration and descended into catastrophe within two years.

The mainlanders who arrived to administer Taiwan were, from the Taiwanese perspective, another set of colonizers replacing the previous ones. They spoke Mandarin in a population that spoke Taiwanese (Min Nan) and Japanese. They treated the island as a conquered territory to be exploited rather than liberated. KMT officials confiscated Japanese properties (which should have been returned to Taiwanese owners), manipulated the currency to extract resources for the mainland war effort, and appointed mainlanders to all significant administrative positions over experienced Taiwanese professionals.

By early 1947, the resentment had become explosive. On February 28, 1947, a minor incident — KMT agents beating a widow selling untaxed cigarettes on a Taipei street, then killing a bystander who protested — triggered a spontaneous island-wide uprising. For several days, Taiwanese civilians and former Japanese military veterans took control of most of the island, establishing local governance committees and negotiating with the KMT governor.

Chiang Kai-shek’s response was military reinforcement and massacre. When mainland troops arrived in early March, they conducted systematic killings across the island — targeting specifically the educated, the articulate, the politically aware. Doctors, lawyers, journalists, teachers, local politicians, and community leaders were identified and shot. The dead numbered between 10,000 and 30,000 — estimates vary because the full truth was suppressed for four decades under martial law.

The 2-28 Massacre (named for its February 28 start date) is the deepest wound in Taiwanese identity and politics. It eliminated an entire generation of native Taiwanese leadership — the people most capable of articulating and organizing Taiwanese political identity. It created an unbridgeable gulf of distrust between the mainland Chinese ruling class and the native Taiwanese majority. And it established the KMT’s authoritarian rule through the most extreme form of violence — setting the context for the 38-year martial law that followed.

For decades, the massacre was unspeakable — literally forbidden as a subject of public discussion. When Taiwan finally democratized, one of the first acts of reckoning was the formal investigation, acknowledgment, and official presidential apology for 2-28. The date is now a national holiday; memorials stand in cities across the island. The wound is not healed, exactly, but it has been acknowledged — and that acknowledgment is itself a measure of the democratic maturity Taiwan has achieved.

1949: The Retreat That Changed History

In 1949, the most consequential migration in post-war East Asia began. Mao Zedong’s Communist People’s Liberation Army had routed Chiang Kai-shek’s nationalist forces in the Chinese Civil War. City after city fell. The mainland was lost.

Chiang Kai-shek evacuated to Taiwan with approximately 1.2 million people — soldiers, government officials, intellectuals, businesspeople, and their families — along with the entirety of China’s gold reserves from the national treasury (approximately 375 tonnes of gold) and the priceless imperial art collection from the Forbidden City (now housed in the National Palace Museum in Taipei, still the largest collection of Chinese imperial art in the world).

Taiwan now contained two political entities inhabiting one territory: a government of mainland refugees claiming to be the legitimate government of all China, ruling over a native Taiwanese majority that had no say in that claim and bitter memories of what mainland Chinese rule meant. The People’s Republic of China on the mainland and the Republic of China on Taiwan each insisted it was the real China, and each insisted the other would eventually be eliminated.

This was the stage — a traumatised, militarised, politically fractured island of 8 million people (plus 1.2 million new arrivals) — on which one of the 20th century’s greatest development stories would be written.

CHAPTER 2 — Chiang Kai-shek and the Nationalist Foundation: Authoritarianism with a Development Vision (1949–1975)

The Generalissimo’s Paradox

Chiang Kai-shek is one of history’s most difficult figures to render in simple moral terms. He was a military dictator who ordered the 2-28 massacre; he was also a Methodist Christian who genuinely believed in national destiny. He ran the bamboo curtain of political repression for decades; he also implemented the most successful land reform in Asian history. He kept Taiwan under martial law for 38 years; he also built the educational and institutional foundations that his successors would use to create a democratic economic powerhouse. He was an authoritarian who created the conditions, despite himself, for one of Asia’s great democracies.

Born in 1887 in Zhejiang Province, trained in military academies in China and Japan, Chiang rose through the Nationalist movement under Sun Yat-sen, eventually becoming its military and then political leader. He fought Japanese invasion, fought Communist insurgency, and ultimately lost the world’s most populous nation in a civil war — the decisive military and political failure of his life. Taiwan was his refuge and his second chance.

On Taiwan, everything was different from mainland China. Chiang had no powerful landlord class loyal to him that he needed to protect. He had no competing warlords. He had a relatively small, manageable territory. He had an educated bureaucracy (many of China’s best-trained officials had come to Taiwan) and — crucially — he had massive American support. These differences allowed him to implement on Taiwan what he had failed to implement on the mainland: genuine institutional reform.

The Land Reform: The Foundation of the Miracle

The Land-to-the-Tiller Programme of 1950–1953 is, in the judgment of most development economists who study Taiwan, the single most important policy decision in Taiwan’s economic history — more important, even, than the founding of Hsinchu Science Park or TSMC. It created the social and political conditions without which no subsequent economic miracle would have been possible.

Taiwan in 1950 had a deeply unequal land tenure system inherited from Japanese colonialism: large landlords owned enormous tracts of agricultural land, leasing it to tenant farmers who paid rents absorbing 50–70% of their harvest. The vast majority of Taiwanese farmers were effectively serfs, with no ownership stake in the land they worked and therefore limited incentive to invest in improving it.

The reform proceeded in three steps: first, rent reductions capped rents at 37.5% of crop yield. Second, public land was sold to tenants at favourable prices. Third — and most radically — the government compulsorily purchased large private landholdings (above a certain threshold) and sold them to the farmers who worked them. The landlords were compensated in government bonds and shares in state-owned enterprises — which turned them from feudal rent collectors into industrial investors.

The consequences were transformative in multiple dimensions simultaneously:

Agricultural productivity soared as farmers who now owned their land invested far more in improving it. Taiwan moved from a rice-deficit to a rice-surplus economy within a decade.

Rural inequality collapsed. The Gini coefficient (a measure of inequality ranging from 0 to 1) in the agricultural sector fell dramatically. A rural middle class of small owner-farmers emerged, with a genuine economic stake in stability and growth.

Political stability was consolidated. The 1.2 million mainland arrivals were concentrated in the military and government; the native Taiwanese majority were now land-owning peasants with something to protect and a reason to prefer stability over upheaval. The social base for KMT governance, however authoritarian, was constructed from this land-owning rural class.

Industrial capital was created. The landlord class, compensated in enterprise shares, became Taiwan’s first industrial investors — channeling wealth from feudal land rents into modern manufacturing. The transition from agrarian to industrial society was partly funded by the savings of former landlords who had been converted into capitalists.

India’s land reform comparison is instructive here: India debated land reform for decades, implemented it partially and inconsistently through state-level legislation, and never achieved anything approaching Taiwan’s comprehensive redistribution — because India’s Congress party was partly dependent on the rural landlord class for political support. Taiwan’s KMT, as a government of mainland outsiders, had no such dependence. They could implement radical reform precisely because they owed nothing to the Taiwanese landed class they were dispossessing.

The US Umbrella and the Strategic Context

The Korean War, which began in June 1950, transformed Taiwan’s geopolitical situation overnight. President Truman ordered the US Seventh Fleet to patrol the Taiwan Strait, effectively preventing a mainland Chinese invasion of Taiwan — which had seemed imminent before Korea changed the strategic calculation. Taiwan became a frontline Cold War ally, absorbed into the American security architecture alongside Japan and South Korea.

Between 1951 and 1965, the United States provided Taiwan with approximately $1.5 billion in economic aid — a sum that seems modest by today’s standards but that, applied to a small economy in the 1950s, was substantial. Critically, this aid funded infrastructure (roads, ports, electricity), education, and institutional development rather than consumption — and it was deployed by a government with a genuine development vision.

The combination of US security guarantee (removing the immediate invasion threat) and economic aid (funding the infrastructure of development) gave Taiwan’s government the strategic breathing room to focus on long-term economic transformation rather than immediate survival. This is the crucial role that external security provision plays in development: it is very difficult to build a modern economy when you are genuinely uncertain whether your country will exist next year.

The Technocratic State: Taiwan’s Development Architecture

The Taiwanese state developed a distinctive approach to economic management that combined features of the Japanese developmental state model with pragmatic adaptation to Taiwan’s specific circumstances.

The key institution was the Council for Economic Planning and Development (CEPD) and its predecessor bodies — a technocratic planning agency staffed by economists and engineers rather than politicians, tasked with designing and coordinating industrial policy. This institution operated with remarkable insulation from the kind of short-term political pressures that distort economic policy in democracies — and in this particular phase, its insulation came from simply being part of an authoritarian state that could make long-term commitments that electoral politics makes difficult.

The state’s approach was not Soviet-style planning (direct ownership and command allocation of resources) but market-guiding strategic coordination — identifying sectors with development potential, providing targeted incentives (tax breaks, subsidized land, co-investment), developing the human capital (education) and physical infrastructure (roads, ports, electricity) that private investment required, and stepping back when the private sector could sustain itself.

K.Y. Yin (尹仲容, 1903–1963) is the largely forgotten architect of this system — the man whose policy vision deserves more credit for Taiwan’s miracle than almost any other individual except Morris Chang. Yin served in multiple economic planning roles between 1949 and his death, designing the structure of Taiwan’s early industrialization. His key insights were prescient: Taiwan’s labour cost advantage was a temporary window, not a permanent strategy. The island needed to continuously upgrade — from low-skill labour to higher-skill manufacturing to technology — or it would be competed away. He pushed for export orientation when the prevailing wisdom in development economics favoured import substitution. He championed the creation of development institutions that would survive individual governments.

Yin died in 1963 before seeing the full fruits of his vision. But the institutions he built — the planning agencies, the development banks, the industrial policy framework — ran Taiwan’s economic management for the next four decades.

The Numbers That Tell the Story

Taiwan’s per capita income in 1950 was approximately $100 — comparable to sub-Saharan Africa at the time, below the Philippines, far below Latin America. By the time Chiang Kai-shek died in 1975, it had reached approximately $900 — a nine-fold real increase in 25 years. Life expectancy had risen from 53 to 70. Literacy had reached 85%. The structural transformation from agriculture to industry was largely complete.

This was not yet the “Taiwan miracle” — that phrase would be earned in the decades that followed. But the foundation had been laid: an educated, relatively equal population; functioning institutions; physical infrastructure; external security; and a development model that had shown it could work. Chiang Kai-shek was an authoritarian who caused immense suffering; he was also a man who, in the face of existential threat, made developmental choices that his more democratic successors would build on to create something extraordinary.

CHAPTER 3 — The Taiwan Miracle: Export-Led Industrialization and the Rise of a Tiger (1960s–1980s)

The Fork in the Road

In the 1950s and 1960s, developing countries faced a fundamental choice in economic strategy, and the choice they made would determine their prosperity for generations.

Import substitution industrialisation (ISI): build domestic industry by protecting it from international competition — high tariffs, import restrictions, government ownership of key industries. This was the path chosen by India under Nehru, by most of Latin America, and by many African nations after independence. The logic seemed compelling: protect infant industries until they are strong enough to compete internationally. The result, in practice: industries that never grew up, that required permanent protection, that produced expensive, low-quality goods for a captive domestic market, while the foreign exchange to pay for necessary imports dried up.

Export-oriented industrialisation (EOI): build industry by selling to global markets from the beginning — accepting international competition as a discipline, focusing on producing things the world actually wants at prices the world will pay. This was the path chosen by Japan, South Korea, Singapore, Hong Kong — and Taiwan. The discipline was harsh: if your products weren’t competitive globally, there was no protective tariff to hide behind. But the rewards were equally harsh in the other direction: a domestic market of 8 million people couldn’t fund serious industrialisation; a global market of billions could.

Taiwan’s choice of export orientation, against the prevailing development consensus of the time (which favoured ISI), was the strategic decision that made everything else possible.

Phase 1: The Labour Factory of the World (1960s)

Taiwan’s first export wave was built on the simplest possible comparative advantage: cheap, educated, disciplined labour. Textiles, plastics, processed food, toys, footwear, small electronics assemblies — products requiring intensive hand assembly but little technology or capital — flooded out of Taiwan’s factories and into American department stores.

The Export Processing Zones (EPZs) established in 1966 were the institutional mechanism that made this possible at scale. The Kaohsiung Export Processing Zone — the first in Asia — offered foreign investors a complete package: duty-free import of raw materials and equipment, pre-built factory facilities, streamlined customs, reliable power and water, and access to Taiwan’s labour force at wages (perhaps $0.30 per hour in 1966) that no American, European, or Japanese manufacturer could match.

The Kaohsiung EPZ was the template that Deng Xiaoping subsequently studied — specifically, explicitly — when designing China’s Special Economic Zones in 1979. The Shenzhen SEZ is, in its intellectual genealogy, a direct descendant of Taiwan’s Kaohsiung EPZ. The irony that Taiwan’s development model was adapted by the communist government that had defeated Chiang Kai-shek’s nationalists is one of history’s more pointed jokes.

By the late 1960s, Taiwan had become the world’s largest exporter of several categories of cheap consumer goods. Taiwanese products were everywhere in American homes; Taiwan itself was largely invisible.

Phase 2: Going Heavier (1970s)

The Oil Shock of 1973 and the inevitable rise of Taiwanese wages (as the economy grew) both pushed Taiwan to upgrade. Light manufacturing was gradually moving to lower-wage competitors; Taiwan needed heavier industry.

Chiang Kai-shek’s son and designated successor Chiang Ching-kuo launched the Ten Major Construction Projects — a deliberate state investment programme to build the physical infrastructure of a more advanced economy. The projects included: a north-south freeway (Taiwan’s first), railway electrification, the Taichung Port, Taoyuan International Airport, a steel mill (China Steel Corporation, which became one of Asia’s finest), a shipyard, three major petrochemical plants, and the Hualien Port.

This was industrial policy in its most direct form: the state identifying infrastructure bottlenecks and removing them through public investment, creating the platform that private industrial investment needed in order to locate in Taiwan. The steel mill in particular would prove transformative: an advanced, domestically owned steel industry provides the raw material for the downstream industries that moved Taiwan up the value chain.

Phase 3: The Technology Pivot (1980s)

By the 1980s, Taiwan’s planners — K.Y. Yin’s intellectual successors at the CEPD — could see clearly what the next wave required. Wages were rising; Southeast Asian countries were undercutting Taiwan on labour costs; the sustainable competitive advantage could only lie in technology and knowledge intensity. Taiwan had to move from making things with hands to making things with minds.

This was the moment that set Taiwan’s trajectory toward semiconductor dominance. The decisions made in this decade — the Hsinchu Science Park, the ITRI technology licensing, the policy of attracting Silicon Valley returnees — were the seeds of the world-transforming industry that grew from them.

Chiang Ching-kuo: The Most Remarkable Personal Transformation in 20th-Century Asian Politics

The story of Chiang Ching-kuo (1910–1988) is one of the most extraordinary political biographies of the modern era. He was the son of the Generalissimo, educated in the Soviet Union (where he spent twelve years and married a Russian woman), who returned to China as a committed communist — and then became a committed anti-communist when his father’s political trajectory demanded it.

He ran Taiwan’s feared secret police (the Garrison Command) in the 1950s — responsible for the White Terror, the political repression that imprisoned and executed thousands of suspected communists and political dissidents in the years after 1949. He was, in this phase of his life, one of Asia’s most effective authoritarian enforcers.

And then, in the last decade of his life, he became something else entirely: Taiwan’s democratic reformer.

In 1986, when opposition politicians gathered illegally to found the Democratic Progressive Party — directly violating the laws of the martial law state he ran — Chiang Ching-kuo did not arrest them. He allowed the party to form. In 1987, he lifted martial law after 38 years — voluntarily, without being forced to by popular revolution or international pressure. He appointed Lee Teng-hui — a native Taiwanese (a member of the majority that had been systematically excluded from political leadership since 1949) — as his Vice President and eventual successor.

He died in January 1988, having set in motion the democratic transition he would not live to see completed. The man who once ran Taiwan’s secret police had become the man who dismantled the system it served.

Why did he do it? Historians debate this. He was genuinely ill (diabetes was destroying him); perhaps facing mortality clarified his values. He was educated enough to understand that authoritarianism was ultimately unsustainable and that a Taiwan with democratic legitimacy would be more defensible internationally than a Taiwan as just another authoritarian relic of the Cold War. He may have genuinely believed that the Taiwanese people had earned the right to choose their own government through decades of hard work and demonstrated civic responsibility.

Whatever the reason, his decision to democratize stands as one of history’s rare examples of an authoritarian leader voluntarily surrendering the system that sustains authoritarian power — choosing institutional legacy over personal control.

The SME Model: Taiwan’s Deep Industrial Root System

Taiwan’s industrialisation was built not on the giant conglomerate model of South Korea (where the chaebol — Samsung, Hyundai, LG, SK — dominated entire industries and were effectively quasi-governmental institutions) but on a vast ecosystem of small and medium enterprises (SMEs).

In the 1970s and 1980s, a typical Taiwanese industrial district contained hundreds of small, family-owned firms, each specialising in a narrow step in a production process, trading with each other in dense local supply networks. A television manufacturer might buy plastic casings from one SME, circuit boards from another, speakers from a third, and final assembly services from a fourth — all within a few kilometres of each other. This horizontal specialisation was individually less efficient than the vertically integrated Korean chaebol — but it was collectively far more resilient and adaptable.

When a global market shifted — when cheap plastic toys gave way to plastic electronics, when simple electronics gave way to complex electronics — the Taiwanese SME ecosystem adapted more quickly than the Korean chaebol, because the SMEs were already specialists in components and processes that could be recombined into new products. They were Lego pieces, infinitely recombinable. The chaebol were complete factories, harder to redesign.

This SME root system would later become the deep industrial base of Taiwan’s semiconductor supply chain — the hundreds of specialty chemical companies, component manufacturers, precision equipment makers, and specialist service providers that make TSMC’s manufacturing possible by supplying the ecosystem it depends on.

The data: Taiwan’s exports grew from $164 million in 1960 to $67 billion in 1990 — a 400-fold increase in 30 years.

CHAPTER 4 — The Hsinchu Science Park and the Birth of Taiwan’s Silicon Valley (1980s)

The Insight That Changed History

In the late 1970s, a clear-eyed assessment of Taiwan’s development trajectory produced an uncomfortable conclusion: the island was trapped. Its labour cost advantage was eroding with rising wages. Its light manufacturing industries were facing competition from lower-wage neighbours. Its capital-intensive heavy industries were competing against countries that had more of the natural resources (steel ore, oil) they required. The next competitive advantage — the one that would sustain growth into the 21st century — could not be in cheaper hands. It had to be in better minds.

Sun Yun-suan — one of Taiwan’s great statesmen, who served as Premier from 1978 to 1984 — understood this with unusual clarity. An electrical engineer by training, he had spent his career in Taiwan’s power sector before moving into government planning. He knew that technology was not just another sector; it was a multiplier, a foundation that amplified productivity across every other industry. And he knew that Taiwan had an extraordinary, underexploited asset: a global diaspora of highly educated Taiwanese engineers who had gone to American universities and then built careers in Silicon Valley.

The solution he championed: build a technology park that would attract those engineers home.

Hsinchu Science-Based Industrial Park: Design as Strategy

The Hsinchu Science-Based Industrial Park, established in 1980, was not simply an industrial estate with tax breaks. It was a comprehensive ecosystem design — a deliberate attempt to create the conditions in which technology companies could emerge, grow, and sustain each other.

The location was chosen carefully: Hsinchu City, home to National Chiao Tung University and National Tsing Hua University — two of Taiwan’s best science and engineering universities. The assumption, proved entirely correct, was that proximity to universities would create feedback loops between research and industry: professors would consult for companies, graduate students would work in company labs, company problems would become university research questions, university breakthroughs would become company products.

The incentives were comprehensive: a 5-year tax holiday followed by reduced rates; subsidized land leases; co-investment from government venture funds; simplified customs for equipment imports; modern infrastructure (power, water, roads, telecommunications) inside the park. But the most important design decision was the least visible: a deliberate, sustained policy of recruiting overseas Chinese — primarily the Taiwanese engineers of Silicon Valley — to return and start companies.

The Silicon Valley–Hsinchu Corridor: The World’s Most Consequential Knowledge Transfer

In the 1970s and 1980s, Silicon Valley was the undisputed global centre of semiconductor technology. And working in Silicon Valley’s leading companies — Intel, National Semiconductor, Texas Instruments, Fairchild, Hewlett-Packard — were thousands of engineers born in Taiwan, who had come to American universities for education and stayed for careers.

These engineers occupied positions across the spectrum of semiconductor technology: process engineers who understood how to build fabs, design engineers who understood chip architecture, business managers who understood global markets, and entrepreneurs who had already built startup companies in Silicon Valley and knew what it took.

Taiwan’s government — through institutional recruitment, through conferences that connected the diaspora with investment opportunities, through investment in making Taiwan a viable place for technology companies — created the conditions in which these engineers could return. Not all of them did; many remained in Silicon Valley. But enough returned — hundreds during the 1980s, thousands in subsequent decades — to seed Hsinchu with a quality of technological capability that simply could not have been built from scratch through domestic training alone.

This diaspora mobilization — using overseas talent as a technology transfer mechanism — is one of the most powerful and least-replicable economic development strategies in history. It works because you cannot simply buy the kind of tacit, practical knowledge these engineers carried. International semiconductor equipment companies will sell you machines. Technology licensing agreements will give you patents. But the knowledge of how to actually run a fab — the thousands of small decisions made daily by experienced engineers, the intuitions that come from years of troubleshooting — this lives in human beings, not documents. Taiwan acquired it by acquiring the human beings who held it.

India’s equivalent: India has its own diaspora of extraordinary depth in Silicon Valley — perhaps 400,000 Indian-born engineers working in American technology companies, including CEOs of Google, Microsoft, Adobe, IBM, and countless others. The question of whether India can mobilise even a fraction of this diaspora as a technology transfer and company formation mechanism — as Taiwan’s Hsinchu model did — is one of the most important questions in Indian technology policy. The potential is there; the institutional machinery to exploit it is not yet built.

ITRI and the RCA Technology License: How Taiwan Acquired Semiconductor DNA

Before Hsinchu could attract semiconductor companies, Taiwan needed existing semiconductor capability to act as a seed. In 1976, Taiwan’s Industrial Technology Research Institute (ITRI) negotiated a technology licensing agreement with RCA Corporation (Radio Corporation of America) — one of the most consequential technology transfer agreements in modern economic history.

RCA was in the process of exiting the semiconductor business… and for $3.5 million in licensing fees, it transferred the technology for CMOS integrated circuit manufacturing to ITRI’s Electronics Research and Service Organisation (ERSO). A team of 40 Taiwanese engineers was sent to RCA’s Somerville, New Jersey facility to learn semiconductor manufacturing from the inside — not just the documented processes, but the tacit practice.

These 40 engineers returned to Taiwan carrying semiconductor manufacturing knowledge in their heads and hands. They built ERSO’s first demonstration semiconductor production line. They trained other engineers. They became the seed crystal around which Taiwan’s semiconductor industry later crystallized. Most significantly, they later spun out to found or join the companies that would dominate global semiconductor manufacturing — including TSMC.

ITRI’s model — licensing foreign technology, mastering it in a government laboratory, then spinning out commercial companies — became Taiwan’s primary technology acquisition mechanism. It was strategic, patient, and brilliantly executed. The government absorbed the risk of early-stage technology development; the spin-outs took the technology to commercial scale.

The Moment Everything Changed

By 1985, Taiwan had Hsinchu Science Park, ITRI’s semiconductor engineers, a growing ecosystem of technology companies — and a clear sense that something larger was possible if the right leader could be found.

Taiwan’s economic planners were looking for someone to establish a world-class semiconductor company. They wanted someone with deep semiconductor industry experience, a global reputation that would open doors with American technology companies, and the strategic vision to build something that had never been built before.

The person they found was a 54-year-old retired Texas Instruments executive living in California, a man who had spent 25 years at one of the world’s great semiconductor companies, who had been passed over for the top job, and who was looking for a new challenge. His name was Morris Chang.

In 1985, Morris Chang returned to Taiwan. What he built there would change the world.

A Man Who Changed the World by Solving a Problem Nobody Knew Was Solvable

Morris Chang (張忠謀) was born in Ningbo, China in 1931 — the same year Japan invaded Manchuria, four years before the Second Sino-Japanese War would engulf his country, 18 years before the Communist revolution would remake everything. His childhood was a series of city-to-city escapes from advancing Japanese armies: Nanjing, Hong Kong, Chongqing, Shanghai. He grew up, as he has said, “always moving, always uncertain about the future.”

He came to America for university in 1949 — the year Mao’s forces took the mainland — entering Harvard as an 18-year-old with excellent English, a passion for literature, and no settled idea of what to study. He switched to mechanical engineering after his first year, graduated from MIT with a master’s degree, and took a job at Sylvania Electric Products in Boston. He failed the company’s entrance physical — too thin — and the doctor recommended he eat more bananas to gain weight. It is perhaps the most inconsequential medical advice in technology history.

He joined Texas Instruments in 1958 — arriving in Dallas at the moment the company was pioneering the integrated circuit, the invention that would become the foundation of the modern world. Over the next 25 years, Chang rose through TI to become Group Vice President and one of the semiconductor industry’s most respected operational executives. He understood semiconductors not just as a technology but as an industry — understood the economics of manufacturing, the dynamics of customer relationships, the strategic logic of technology investment.

In 1983, TI’s CEO job opened. Chang was the obvious internal candidate based on achievement. He did not get it. The decision was reportedly not about competence but about corporate culture — TI was a Texas company in a Texas era, and Chang, for all his brilliance, was not what the board had in mind. It was the kind of quiet, politely non-discriminatory discrimination that shaped the careers of many talented professionals who didn’t fit conventional expectations. This rejection, like many of history’s pivotal non-events, ultimately redirected enormous capability into an outcome far more consequential than the alternative.

Chang spent two years at General Instrument Corporation and then received a call from Taiwan.

The Revolutionary Insight: Separating What No One Had Separated Before

To understand what Morris Chang invented, you need to understand what the semiconductor industry looked like in 1987 when he founded TSMC.

Every semiconductor company that mattered was an Integrated Device Manufacturer (IDM) — a company that both designed and manufactured its own chips. Intel designed Intel chips and manufactured them in Intel fabs. Motorola designed Motorola chips and manufactured them in Motorola fabs. NEC, Texas Instruments, Hitachi — the same model throughout. This integration made a certain kind of sense: the design and manufacturing processes were so deeply interconnected that having both activities in the same company allowed for rapid iteration and optimisation.

But this vertical integration had a severe structural consequence: manufacturing semiconductors required enormous capital. A leading-edge semiconductor fabrication plant (“fab”) in the mid-1980s cost hundreds of millions of dollars to build and equip — an investment that was available only to large, established companies with substantial revenues. This created an oligopoly: chip manufacturing was the province of a small number of giant companies, and any new entrant into the semiconductor industry either needed to raise hundreds of millions of dollars for manufacturing infrastructure or it couldn’t exist.

The consequence for innovation: fabulous chip design ideas existed in the minds of engineers who didn’t have access to manufacturing. Entire categories of chips that could have been designed and sold were never designed and sold — not because the ideas weren’t there, but because the capital barriers to manufacturing prevented smaller players from participating.

Chang’s insight — which seems obvious in retrospect, as revolutionary ideas always do — was to completely separate chip design from chip manufacturing. Create a company — a “pure-play foundry” — that did nothing except manufacture chips to other companies’ specifications, with world-class precision at commercial scale, without designing any chips of its own and therefore never competing with its customers. Any company that had a chip design could bring it to the foundry for manufacturing. No fab required. The capital barrier to chip design was eliminated.

This was not just a business model innovation — it was the creation of an entirely new category of company that had never existed before, enabling an entirely new category of company (the fabless chip designer) to exist. The foundry and the fabless model are dialectically connected: each created the other.

Consider what the fabless model eventually produced. NVIDIA — today the world’s most valuable semiconductor company, the engine of the AI revolution — has never owned a semiconductor manufacturing facility in its history. It was founded by Jensen Huang in 1993 with a design team and no fab, sending its designs to a foundry (and eventually almost exclusively to TSMC) for manufacturing. Without the foundry model, NVIDIA as we know it could not exist. Without NVIDIA’s chips manufactured by TSMC, the current AI revolution — GPT, Gemini, every large language model — would not exist in its current form.

Qualcomm, which designs the chips inside most Android smartphones. Apple, which designs the A-series and M-series chips that power every iPhone and Mac on Earth. AMD, which under Lisa Su’s leadership became the world’s most impressive chip design company. MediaTek, Broadcom, Marvell — the list of companies whose existence depends on the foundry model that Morris Chang invented is the list of companies that make the digital world possible.

The Founding: Faith Against Skepticism

TSMC was founded on February 21, 1987, in Hsinchu, Taiwan, with $100 million from the Taiwan government (which took a 48% stake) and critical strategic partnership from Dutch electronics giant Philips (which took a 27.5% stake and, more importantly, provided technology licensing and the reputational validation that American customers needed to trust a Taiwanese contract manufacturer). The remainder was raised from private Taiwanese investors.

The early years were hard. American chip designers were skeptical. The most valuable thing in their world was their chip designs — the months or years of engineering work embedded in the circuit layouts. Handing these designs to a contract manufacturer in Taiwan required trust: trust that the manufacturer wouldn’t steal the designs, wouldn’t share them with competitors, and could actually produce chips at the precision and yield rates the designs required.

TSMC had to earn that trust, one customer at a time, one successful production run at a time. The company established extraordinary confidentiality practices — physical separation of customer design data, non-disclosure agreements, the entire operational culture of a company whose business was built on never betraying the customers who trusted it with their most valuable intellectual property.

By the early 1990s, as the personal computer revolution drove unprecedented demand for semiconductor chips, the foundry model’s advantages became irresistible. Companies that couldn’t afford fabs could now design chips; TSMC had enough customers to justify the enormous capital investments that advancing manufacturing technology required; the ecosystem expanded with extraordinary speed.

Moore’s Law and the Technology Treadmill

In 1965, Gordon Moore — co-founder of Intel — observed that the number of transistors that could be packed onto a chip was doubling approximately every two years, with the cost per transistor falling proportionally. This Moore’s Law became the defining dynamic of the semiconductor industry for the next 60 years: a relentless, compounding treadmill of miniaturisation that made chips continuously more powerful (more transistors = more computing capability) and continuously cheaper (smaller transistors = lower cost per transistor).

The numbers that describe this progression are almost incomprehensible:

  • 1971: Intel’s 4004 processor contained 2,300 transistors; the smallest transistor was 10 micrometres (10,000 nanometres) wide.
  • 1987: When TSMC was founded, leading-edge manufacturing was at 1,000 nanometres (1 micron).
  • 2000: 180 nanometres.
  • 2010: 32 nanometres.
  • 2020: 5 nanometres.
  • 2022: 3 nanometres — TSMC’s current leading edge.
  • 2025: 2 nanometres, in development.

At 3 nanometres, a transistor is fewer than 15 silicon atoms wide. If you physically scaled a modern chip up to the size of a football field, each transistor would be smaller than a human red blood cell. TSMC manufactures billions of these transistors — on thousands of chips — every single day, at precision so extreme that a single dust particle or a vibration invisible to the human eye can ruin an entire wafer.

The ASML extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machine is the instrument that makes 3nm manufacturing possible. It works by firing extremely short-wavelength ultraviolet light (wavelength of 13.5 nanometres, invisible to the human eye) through a system of mirrors polished to atomic smoothness — 50 times smoother than anything previously achievable — to project circuit patterns onto silicon wafers with sub-nanometre precision. Each machine costs approximately $200 million. TSMC has more than 100 of them. They can only be made by one company on Earth: ASML, based in Eindhoven, Netherlands. Each machine contains over 100,000 parts, sourced from over 5,000 suppliers in 24 countries. The global supply chain for the machine that makes the machines is itself a wonder of coordination.

This is the technology context in which TSMC operates. The achievement is not just making chips — it is sustaining, at commercial scale, the most precision-intensive manufacturing process in human history, year after year, generation after generation.

The Financial Scale

TSMC today (2024) has a market capitalisation of approximately $650–750 billion — making it one of the ten most valuable companies in the world. Its annual revenue exceeds $70 billion. It employs approximately 75,000 people. It invests approximately $30–40 billion per year in capital expenditures — more than the entire GDP of some countries — to build new fabs and upgrade existing ones to the next technology generation.

This financial scale is both the source of TSMC’s competitive advantage and the barrier to replication: no company without TSMC’s customer base and revenue stream can justify the investment needed to match its technology generation. And without matching its technology generation, you cannot attract TSMC’s customers. The circle is nearly unbreakable.

CHAPTER 6 — The Semiconductor Ecosystem: Why Taiwan, and Only Taiwan

The Question That Confounds Governments

If one company — TSMC — is so strategically critical to global civilization, why not simply build a copy of it somewhere else? The United States is spending $52 billion trying to do exactly this. Japan has contributed billions to attract TSMC fabs. Germany and the European Union have their own chip sovereignty initiatives. Why is everyone struggling so hard to do something that should, in principle, be achievable with sufficient money and political will?

The answer reveals one of the most important and counterintuitive principles in economic geography: complex industries are not just companies. They are ecosystems.

The Cluster Effect: What You See vs. What You Need

When people discuss Taiwan’s semiconductor dominance, they focus on TSMC. TSMC deserves the focus — it is the most important single institution. But TSMC does not operate in a vacuum. It operates in the center of a dense, highly specialised ecosystem of hundreds of companies that make TSMC’s manufacturing possible.

Consider what TSMC needs to manufacture a 3nm chip: - Silicon wafers of extraordinary purity (one atom of contamination per billion silicon atoms is too much): sourced from specialised wafer makers, several of which are in Taiwan and Japan. - Photolithography equipment (the EUV machines): ASML, Netherlands — the only source on Earth. - Chemical mechanical planarisation (CMP) equipment: multiple suppliers, many in the US, Japan, and Taiwan. - Ultra-pure process gases (nitrogen, oxygen, fluorine compounds) at purities of 99.9999%: specialty gas suppliers, several Taiwanese. - Photoresists (light-sensitive chemicals used in the photolithography process): specialised chemical companies in Japan and the US. - Etch and deposition equipment: Applied Materials (US), Lam Research (US), Tokyo Electron (Japan). - Testing and measurement equipment: KLA Corporation (US), Advantest (Japan). - Electronic design automation (EDA) software used by TSMC’s customers to design the chips: Cadence and Synopsys (both US). - Chip packaging and testing after manufacturing: ASE Group, Amkor, OSAT companies — many in Taiwan.

And this is only the first-level ecosystem. Each of these suppliers has its own supply chain of specialised sub-suppliers. The semiconductor supply chain is not a chain — it is a web of extraordinary complexity, most of whose nodes have been developing their capabilities for decades.

Transplant TSMC to Arizona — as is happening — and you get a TSMC fab without the ecosystem. The fab itself can work (with massive logistical effort and supply chain management). But the response time to problems is slower; the local supply of experienced technicians is thinner; the iterative culture of rapid problem-solving that develops when engineers from all parts of the supply chain can meet over lunch is absent. These are soft factors, invisible in a factory balance sheet, and critically important for manufacturing at the frontier of human precision.

Three Generations of Human Capital

Taiwan’s deepest semiconductor advantage is not equipment or institutions — it is people.

Taiwan has been manufacturing semiconductors for nearly 50 years. Three generations of engineers have entered the industry, mastered it, and transmitted their knowledge to successors. The first generation of engineers — trained through the ITRI/RCA technology transfer in the late 1970s — built the foundation. The second generation scaled it. The third generation is pushing toward 2nm and beyond.

The knowledge these engineers carry is largely tacit — it cannot be fully documented, written down, or transferred through a manual. It lives in embodied practice: the technician who knows from the sound of the pump that something in the process is subtly wrong; the engineer who can look at a wafer’s surface pattern and diagnose the source of a yield problem; the manager who knows which process parameters to adjust when a new batch of photoresist arrives with slightly different properties. This knowledge develops over careers of hands-on practice and cannot be accelerated by spending money.

When TSMC builds its Arizona fab and recruits American engineers, it faces this knowledge gap directly. TSMC has brought large numbers of Taiwanese engineers to Arizona to train local staff — transfers that are expensive, logistically complex, and create friction in the American political context (there have been cultural tensions about working conditions and expectations). The knowledge is being transferred, but slowly and incompletely.

The Self-Reinforcing Cycle

TSMC’s competitive position is self-reinforcing in a way that makes catching it progressively harder over time:

  1. More customers → more revenue → more capital to invest in the next technology generation
  2. More advanced technology → more customers who can only get this capability at TSMC
  3. More customers → more manufacturing volume on each technology generation → better yields through experience → lower effective cost
  4. Lower cost + best technology → even more customers

This flywheel has been spinning for 35 years. The gap between TSMC (and Samsung at the leading edge) and every other semiconductor manufacturer is measured not in machines or money but in years — perhaps 5–10 years of technology and practice. That gap does not close easily.

TSMC’s Customers: The World’s Most Illustrious Dependency

The roster of TSMC’s major customers reads like a who’s-who of the global technology economy:

Apple accounts for approximately 25% of TSMC’s revenue — its largest single customer. Every A-series chip (iPhone, iPad) and M-series chip (MacBook, Mac) is manufactured by TSMC. When Apple introduces a new processor product, the manufacturing capability that makes it possible lives in Hsinchu.

NVIDIA manufactures all of its AI training chips — the H100, A100, and the next-generation B100 — exclusively at TSMC. The AI revolution’s infrastructure is literally manufactured on this island.

AMD — whose resurgence under Lisa Su has made it a serious Intel competitor in CPUs and GPUs — is almost entirely TSMC-dependent for its advanced chips. AMD’s best processors outperform Intel’s partly because AMD could access TSMC’s more advanced manufacturing while Intel struggled with its own manufacturing challenges.

Qualcomm designs the Snapdragon processors that power most advanced Android smartphones. Manufactured at TSMC.

Intel — even the iconic American chip company that built the personal computer revolution — has turned increasingly to TSMC for manufacturing as its own manufacturing programme fell behind the technological frontier.

The combined market capitalisation of TSMC’s major customers exceeds $10 trillion. These are the most valuable technology companies in human history, and at their manufacturing core, they depend on one company, on one island.

The MediaTek Dimension: Taiwan’s Design Giant

The global picture of Taiwan’s semiconductor strength extends beyond manufacturing. MediaTek — headquartered in Hsinchu — is the world’s largest smartphone chip designer (by shipment volume), designing chips that power roughly 40% of the world’s smartphones. MediaTek’s Dimensity chips power billions of devices across Asia, Africa, and Latin America — the technology engine of the mass smartphone market.

MediaTek’s success demonstrates that Taiwan’s semiconductor excellence is not just manufacturing but covers the entire stack: design, manufacturing, packaging, testing, and the supply chain that feeds all of it.

CHAPTER 7 — Democracy Under Threat: Taiwan’s Political Miracle

The Party That Should Not Have Existed

On September 28, 1986, a group of Taiwanese opposition politicians gathered in a Taipei hotel and announced the formation of the Democratic Progressive Party (DPP). This was, technically, illegal. Taiwan was under martial law. Political parties other than the KMT were prohibited. Every person in that room was committing an act for which they could be arrested and imprisoned.

They announced the party anyway. And Chiang Ching-kuo had them watched — and left them alone.

His decision not to arrest the DPP founders was not passive indifference. It was the pivotal moment of Taiwan’s democratic transition — an explicit choice by the authoritarian ruler to allow the space for legal opposition to develop rather than crushing it while he still could. Chiang had concluded, apparently, that Taiwan’s long-term survival required democratic legitimacy. An authoritarian Taiwan, in a world of increasingly free societies, would be harder to sustain internationally and would eventually face the same internal contradictions that had broken authoritarian regimes across Asia and Latin America.

Lifting the Bamboo Curtain: 1987

On July 14, 1987, Chiang Ching-kuo signed the order ending martial law in Taiwan. Thirty-eight years, seven months, and seventeen days of the world’s longest continuous martial law period ended with a signature.

The opening was immediate and extraordinary. Newspapers that had been restricted began publishing; political prisoners were released (though the hardest-core cases had already been quietly dealt with); political discussion that had been forced underground moved into the open. The freedom of press and political assembly that most democratic societies take for granted arrived in Taiwan in a compressed, exhilarating burst.

Accompanying the martial law lifting: the Taiwan government announced that citizens could visit family members on the mainland for the first time since 1949 — acknowledging, practically if not legally, that the two sides of the strait were separate political entities rather than one China temporarily divided. This was one of the early admissions, however implicit, that the KMT’s position of representing all of China was becoming increasingly fictional.

Lee Teng-hui: The Taiwanese Who Redefined What Taiwan Was

Lee Teng-hui (李登輝, 1923–2020) is one of Asia’s great democratic statesmen — a figure whose significance is still underappreciated because he operated in a country that occupies a peculiar status in international affairs.

Born in Tamsui, near Taipei, Lee was educated in Japan during the colonial period and earned a doctorate in agricultural economics from Cornell University in 1968. He was, by background and training, the embodiment of the native Taiwanese professionals whose class had been decimated by the 2-28 Massacre: technically expert, internationally educated, deeply committed to Taiwan’s welfare, and long excluded from political power by the mainland-dominated KMT.

Chiang Ching-kuo had appointed Lee as Vice President — an extraordinary choice that reflected Chiang’s late understanding that the KMT needed to be Taiwanised if it was to govern a democratic Taiwan. When Chiang died in January 1988, Lee became President — the first native Taiwanese to hold the position. He then navigated, with extraordinary skill, the political minefield of completing the democratic transition while managing the KMT old guard who resented his ascent and the opposition who distrusted his origins.

Lee’s great political accomplishment was the 1996 presidential election — the first direct popular election for president in Chinese history. The symbolism was immense: for the first time, 23 million Taiwanese would choose their own leader in a free, competitive election.

China’s response was to fire missiles. In March 1996, the PLA conducted military exercises and ballistic missile tests, firing missiles into waters just off Taiwan’s coast — the most provocative military action across the Taiwan Strait since the 1950s. The calculation in Beijing was that the threat of military force would intimidate Taiwanese voters into choosing a candidate more amenable to Beijing’s preferences. The assessment was catastrophically miscalculated.

The United States responded immediately and decisively: President Clinton ordered two carrier battle groups — USS Independence and USS Nimitz, with their accompanying destroyers, cruisers, and submarines — to sail through the Taiwan Strait and position in waters near Taiwan. It was the largest US naval deployment in Asia since the Vietnam War. The signal was unmistakable: America backed Taiwan.

The Taiwanese voters responded in equally unmistakable terms: turnout reached 76%, and Lee Teng-hui won with 54% of the vote — a larger margin than any American president in decades. The missile intimidation had produced the opposite of its intended effect, stiffening Taiwanese resolve rather than bending it.

The 1996 crisis crystallised the strategic reality that has governed the Taiwan Strait since: the United States is practically, if not legally, committed to Taiwan’s defence, and China is not yet strong enough to challenge that commitment.

Lee Teng-hui also articulated, in terms the world outside Taiwan could understand, the concept of distinct Taiwanese identity. In a 1999 interview with a German radio station, he described the relationship between Taiwan and mainland China as “special state-to-state relations” — a formulation Beijing found so provocative that it nearly precipitated a crisis. But Lee’s articulation reflected something real and growing: the Taiwanese people, through their democratic experience, their prosperity, and their distinct political culture, were developing an identity that was not simply “Chinese.”

The Living Complexity of Taiwanese Identity

Taiwan’s identity is one of the most genuinely complex in the modern world — and oversimplified treatments of it (whether by Chinese nationalists who insist Taiwan is simply a wayward province or by Western advocates who treat Taiwan as simply-a-democracy-standing-up-to-autocracy) miss the texture.

The population is approximately 95% ethnically Han Chinese — descendants of the waves of Fujian and Guangdong migrants who came to the island over centuries, plus the 1.2 million mainlanders who came in 1949. By ethnicity, culture, language (Mandarin, plus regional dialects), and much of their cultural heritage, Taiwanese are Chinese. Chinese New Year, Confucian family values, traditional Chinese medicine, the classical written language — all are living parts of Taiwanese life.

Yet polls consistently show that a growing majority — now exceeding 60% when asked — identify as “Taiwanese” rather than “Chinese” or “both.” Among the young (under 40), identification as purely Taiwanese exceeds 80%. This shift is not ethnic but political and experiential: the generation that has grown up under Taiwan’s democracy, that has seen what political freedom means in practice, and that watches Hong Kong’s autonomy destroyed by Beijing — this generation has no interest in the political system across the strait, regardless of cultural connection.

The status quo is Taiwan’s diplomatic tightrope and its greatest strategic achievement: Taiwan does not formally declare independence (which would trigger immediate military response from Beijing) and Beijing does not invade (because the costs remain too high). This ambiguous equilibrium has been maintained for 75 years through a combination of American deterrence, Taiwan’s self-restraint, and Beijing’s rational calculation that the prize is not worth the price — yet.

CHAPTER 8 — The Silicon Shield: Geopolitics of Semiconductors

The Theory and Its Logic

The Silicon Shield is not a formal defence treaty or a military alliance. It is an economic and strategic theory — the idea that Taiwan’s indispensability in global semiconductor manufacturing creates a powerful deterrent against Chinese invasion that supplements (and in some analyses, exceeds) the deterrent provided by American military commitment.

The logic runs as follows: if China invades and militarily conquers Taiwan, what happens to TSMC’s fabs? Best case for China: significant damage from combat operations, but the fabs are captured intact and operational. Worst case: Taiwan implements a variant of its existing contingency planning and the fabs are rendered inoperable or destroyed before Chinese forces can secure them. Whether the outcome is capture or destruction, the global semiconductor supply chain is gone — or severely compromised — for several years.

The economic consequences would be devastating for the global economy — including China’s own. China is the world’s largest consumer of semiconductors. Its manufacturing sector, its consumer electronics industry, its automobile industry (increasingly dependent on semiconductors as cars become computers on wheels), its telecommunications infrastructure — all depend on chips that would no longer be available if TSMC’s output stopped.

Economists who have modeled this scenario estimate the immediate economic damage at $2–3 trillion in the first year — larger than the COVID-19 economic shock. The damage to China’s own economy from the semiconductor supply disruption, combined with the comprehensive international sanctions that would follow an invasion of a liberal democracy, would create economic conditions that could threaten the Chinese Communist Party’s political stability. A party whose legitimacy rests substantially on delivering rising living standards to 1.4 billion people is deeply vulnerable to an economic shock of this magnitude.

The Silicon Shield theory therefore proposes: China’s leadership rationally understands this. The conquest of Taiwan, even if militarily achievable, would be economically catastrophic. The price is too high. Taiwan’s technological indispensability is its protection.

The Shield’s Limits

The Silicon Shield theory is compelling. It is not complete.

Rational actor assumption: The Shield depends on Beijing making decisions based on rational cost-benefit analysis. Great power decisions about territorial disputes are not always made on purely economic grounds. Nationalism, regime legitimacy, historical grievance, and domestic political pressure can override economic rationality. Xi Jinping has staked his personal legacy on “the great rejuvenation of the Chinese nation,” which includes, in the CCP’s official formulation, “reunification” with Taiwan. Nations sometimes make very costly decisions for reasons that transcend economic calculation.

China’s self-sufficiency drive: If China substantially reduces its dependence on Taiwanese semiconductors — by developing its own advanced chip manufacturing capability — the economic deterrent of the Silicon Shield weakens proportionally. Beijing understands this perfectly; it is the primary motivation for China’s trillion-renminbi semiconductor self-sufficiency drive.

The timeline question: Every year that passes changes the calculus. China’s navy, air force, and missile capabilities grow. The window in which crossing the strait and seizing Taiwan would be militarily and economically too costly for Beijing may not be permanent.

The US-China Semiconductor War

The recognition, in Washington, of semiconductors as a geopolitical weapon and a strategic vulnerability began emerging seriously around 2018 and accelerated dramatically in 2022.

The Huawei blacklisting of 2019 — the US Commerce Department’s decision to add Huawei to the Entity List, restricting American companies from selling Huawei equipment or software without a license — was the opening shot. Huawei, at the time the world’s largest telecommunications equipment company and a leading smartphone maker, was cut off from the American supply chain. Most critically: it was cut off from Google’s Android software and, eventually, from TSMC’s manufacturing capability, because TSMC uses American-origin technology throughout its processes and is therefore subject to US export control rules.

The result: Huawei’s smartphone business effectively collapsed. Its flagship Kirin processor, manufactured by TSMC, became unavailable. Huawei pivoted to older, less capable domestically-manufactured alternatives. Its global smartphone market share fell from approximately 15% to under 3%. The blacklisting demonstrated something important: the semiconductor supply chain is a weapon, and the United States controls critical chokepoints within it.

The CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 ($52.7 billion allocated primarily to rebuild US semiconductor manufacturing, attract TSMC and Samsung fabs to American soil, and fund domestic R&D) represented the most significant US industrial policy in decades — an acknowledgment that the decades-long trend of offshoring manufacturing had created a strategic vulnerability.

The export control regime expanded in October 2022 to be arguably the most aggressive technology export controls since the Cold War: restrictions on exporting to China any chip above a certain performance threshold, any equipment used to manufacture advanced chips, and — through foreign policy pressure on allies — any such equipment from any country using American-origin technology.

The Netherlands, under US diplomatic pressure (reportedly intense), restricted ASML from exporting its EUV machines to China in 2023. Since China cannot manufacture EUV machines itself — the technology requires decades of accumulated precision engineering knowledge and a global supply chain of hundreds of specialised components — this restriction effectively prevents China from achieving advanced chip manufacturing through any near-term path that ASML equipment would have enabled.

China’s Response: The Billion-Dollar Self-Sufficiency Drive

China’s response to the semiconductor technology restrictions has been predictable: massive state investment in domestic semiconductor capability, with the explicit goal of reducing dependence on foreign technology.

China’s “Big Fund” (National Integrated Circuit Industry Investment Fund) has invested hundreds of billions of renminbi in domestic semiconductor companies across the supply chain: SMIC (Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation) for manufacturing, CXMT for memory chips, hundreds of design companies for various chip categories.

SMIC has achieved 7nm manufacturing — a notable accomplishment, apparently achieved by using DUV (deep ultraviolet) lithography machines (which China can still import) in multiple passes to approximate the capability of single-pass EUV manufacturing. This is technically ingenious and commercially significant — it proved China can achieve more than was expected under export control pressure.

But the gap between SMIC’s 7nm and TSMC’s 3nm (or 2nm, now in development) is enormous — measured not just in transistor size but in yield rates (the percentage of chips on a wafer that work correctly), energy efficiency, performance per watt, and cost per transistor. And SMIC cannot access EUV machines; without EUV, achieving TSMC’s leading edge is mathematically extremely difficult. The gap is unlikely to close to competitive parity within the horizon of most strategic planning.

TSMC’s Global Expansion: The Paradox of the Distributed Shield

In response to US, Japanese, and European political pressure to geographically diversify semiconductor manufacturing, TSMC has agreed to build fabs outside Taiwan for the first time:

  • TSMC Arizona (Phoenix): Two fabs announced, one producing 4nm chips (now operational), one targeting 3nm. Investment: approximately $40 billion. US federal CHIPS Act subsidies: $6.6 billion plus loans.
  • TSMC Kumamoto, Japan: Producing 12nm and 16nm chips for Japanese automotive and industrial customers. Investment: approximately $8.6 billion, subsidized by the Japanese government.
  • TSMC Dresden, Germany: Producing 12nm and 16nm specialty chips for European automobile and industrial sectors. Investment: approximately $10 billion, heavily subsidized by the EU.

These fabs will produce real chips and provide some supply chain diversification for their host countries. They are also, in the strategic analysis that matters most, not able to replicate Taiwan’s indispensability.

The most advanced chips — the ones that matter most for AI, military systems, and future technology leadership — will continue to be made in Taiwan for the foreseeable future. The Arizona fab, even at 3nm, will not immediately match Taiwan’s yield rates, cost efficiency, or engineering responsiveness. The Japan and Germany fabs are producing chips that are several technology generations behind the leading edge.

The paradox: the global effort to reduce dependence on Taiwan may, in its current form, actually strengthen TSMC’s dominance of the most advanced manufacturing, because it concentrates TSMC’s leading-edge investment in Taiwan while diffusing its older technology to locations where it is less competitive pressure.

The World That Taiwan Built and the World Doesn’t Know It

Ask a random person on a global street to name a Taiwanese company and most will hesitate. Mention TSMC — blank look. Yet consider the technology world that Taiwan has built, which mostly reaches the consumer under American brand names:

The iPhone in your pocket was designed in California, manufactured in China — but its processor was made in Taiwan (TSMC), its display driver chip made in Taiwan (Novatek), its Wi-Fi chip made to a Taiwanese design (MediaTek), assembled in a Foxconn factory managed by a Taiwanese company — and the motherboard of the PC or Mac from which you ordered it was almost certainly designed by ASUS, the world’s third-largest motherboard maker, headquartered in Taipei. Taiwan is everywhere in global technology and almost nowhere in global consumer brand recognition. This invisibility is one of its defining characteristics.

Foxconn: Manufacturing at a Scale the World Has Never Seen

Hon Hai Precision Industry — universally known by its trading name Foxconn — is the world’s largest electronics contract manufacturer, a company so large and so central to global supply chains that when it coughs, the technology industry catches a cold.

Terry Gou (郭台銘) founded Foxconn in 1974 at age 24, in a rented apartment in Tucheng, with NT$300,000 (approximately $7,500 at the time) borrowed partly from his mother. His first products were plastic television knobs — small, simple, produced with his own hands in a facility with 10 employees. He built Foxconn into a company employing over 1 million people at its peak, operating factories across China, India, Vietnam, Mexico, and a dozen other countries, generating annual revenues exceeding $200 billion.

Foxconn’s core competency is precision manufacturing at extreme scale with extreme quality. Its ability to take an engineering specification — Apple’s design for an iPhone — and reproduce it in millions of units per quarter, at consistent quality, at a price that makes the economics work for both Foxconn and Apple, represents a manufacturing achievement that is underappreciated because it looks, from the outside, like “just assembly.”

The reality is far more demanding. A modern smartphone is assembled from approximately 1,000 individual components, sourced from hundreds of suppliers, assembled in a sequence that requires hundreds of precise operations, tested through multiple quality checkpoints, and delivered in a configuration where any of hundreds of failure modes must be reliably absent. Managing this process at a rate of millions of units per month requires logistics, quality management, workforce management, and process engineering of extraordinary sophistication.

Foxconn’s Zhengzhou campus — “iPhone City” — employed at its peak approximately 330,000 workers on a campus with dormitories, restaurants, hospitals, and banks, running 24 hours a day in multiple shifts. When COVID-19 lockdowns disrupted Zhengzhou operations in late 2022, Apple’s iPhone production fell measurably in the Christmas quarter — demonstrating the acute geographical concentration risk that Apple (and the world) had accepted in exchange for Foxconn’s manufacturing excellence.

Foxconn is now aggressively expanding its manufacturing presence in India — building facilities in Tamil Nadu and Karnataka — as geopolitical pressure and supply chain diversification imperatives push Apple to reduce its China exposure. The India opportunity is enormous; the execution challenges — infrastructure quality, labor training, supply chain development — are substantial. Taiwan’s Foxconn building Indian factories is the semiconductor equivalent of the 20th century’s Japanese companies bringing manufacturing methods to Southeast Asia: a knowledge and capability transfer that could, over a decade, fundamentally change Indian manufacturing.

India’s own Foxconn moment: India has attracted Foxconn investment specifically for iPhone assembly. The Tata Group has acquired Wistron’s iPhone assembly operations in Karnataka. These are real, meaningful steps toward making India a significant electronics manufacturing hub. The question that Taiwan’s experience poses to India is the next step: can India build the component ecosystem — the display driver chips, the wireless chips, the precision mechanical components — that currently come from Taiwan, Japan, and China? Taiwan took 30 years to build its component ecosystem from a base of 1960s light manufacturing. India is attempting to telescope that timeline.

ASUS, Acer, and the PC That Democratized the World

The personal computer revolution of the 1980s and 1990s made computing accessible to ordinary households globally — and the economic engine of that democratization was substantially Taiwanese.

ASUS (ASUSTeK Computer) was founded in 1989 by four electrical engineers who had previously worked at Acer. It became the world’s dominant motherboard manufacturer — the circuit board that is the central nervous system of every PC. In the 1990s, ASUS motherboards were famous for their quality, reliability, and aggressive pricing: they made the PC affordable for the global middle class. Today ASUS is one of the world’s top five laptop brands, a global gaming hardware company, and a significant server and networking equipment manufacturer.

Acer was founded by Stan Shih in 1976 with a staff of 11 and $25,000 in seed capital. It became a global personal computer brand, growing through the 1990s PC boom to reach tens of millions in annual sales. Stan Shih’s famous “Smiling Curve” concept — his observation that value in the electronics supply chain concentrates at the two ends (design and branding) rather than in the middle (assembly) — became one of the most influential frameworks in Taiwan’s industrial strategy. It predicted exactly the direction that TSMC and Taiwan’s fabless chip designers would eventually monetize.

HTC (High Tech Computer) was a Taiwanese company founded in 1997 that, for a brief brilliant moment in the mid-2000s to early 2010s, was the world’s leading Android smartphone maker and VR pioneer. HTC’s story — brilliant innovation, failure to build the brand equity that converts engineering excellence into sustainable consumer loyalty, eventual decline — is one of the cautionary tales of Taiwanese technology: the ability to build extraordinary products does not automatically translate into the ability to capture the consumer value of those products. Brand building is a different skill from engineering, and Taiwan has historically been better at the latter.

Giant Bicycles: When Low-Tech Becomes Premium

Giant Manufacturing — founded in Taichung in 1972 — is the world’s largest bicycle manufacturer by revenue and arguably the best illustration of how Taiwan has taken a “low-technology” consumer product and transformed it through engineering excellence, materials science, and global brand building into a premium market leader.

Giant began as an OEM manufacturer, making bicycles for other brands (including Schwinn of Chicago, which it eventually helped put out of business by learning enough from the OEM relationship to compete directly). In the 1980s, Taiwan’s bicycle industry faced the classic developing-country threat: South East Asian manufacturers with lower labour costs were undercutting them on cheap bikes.

Giant’s response: move upmarket. Invest heavily in carbon fibre and aluminium frame technology, precision component manufacturing, and global retail and brand building. Rather than compete on price for mass-market bicycles, become the quality leader for serious cycling — the bikes used by professional racing teams, by dedicated recreational cyclists, by the premium market that pays for engineering rather than just transportation.

Today Giant bicycles are ridden by professional riders in the Tour de France. They retail for thousands of dollars. The Taichung factory that makes them is a precision manufacturing showcase. The company is a $4 billion global brand.

The Giant story carries a lesson that Taiwan’s government and business community absorbed: there is no intrinsically low-technology industry, only industries to which insufficient technology has been applied. Bicycles, hand tools, medical devices, specialty food equipment — Taiwan has upgraded numerous product categories from commodity to premium through the application of engineering excellence. This is the industrial philosophy made concrete.

Taiwan’s Invisible Infrastructure: Semiconductor Materials and Equipment

Behind TSMC’s fabs and MediaTek’s chip designs is an ecosystem of hundreds of Taiwanese companies that manufacture the materials, components, and equipment without which semiconductor manufacturing is impossible.

Silicon wafers: The ultra-pure silicon discs on which chips are printed require silicon purity of 99.999999999% (eleven 9s). Taiwanese companies including GlobalWafers are among the world’s largest silicon wafer manufacturers.

Specialty chemicals: The photoresists, etchants, cleaning agents, and process gases used in semiconductor manufacturing require purities and formulations that only a small number of global suppliers can provide. Several Taiwanese specialty chemical companies participate in this ecosystem.

Chip packaging and testing: After TSMC manufactures chips on a wafer, the chips must be cut, tested, and packaged for use in final products. Taiwan is home to the world’s largest chip packaging and testing companies, including ASE Group (Advanced Semiconductor Engineering) — the world’s largest chip packaging company by revenue — and SPIL (Siliconware Precision Industries). These companies are becoming increasingly strategic as advanced packaging (3D chip stacking, chiplets interconnected at microscale) becomes more important to performance at smaller nodes.

This packaging and testing ecosystem is one of Taiwan’s least-discussed competitive advantages — and one of the hardest to replicate, because it requires the same kind of accumulated technical knowledge and equipment investment as manufacturing.

CHAPTER 10 — Key Figures Gallery: The Architects of Taiwan’s Miracle

Chiang Kai-shek (蔣介石, 1887–1975): The Man Who Lost China and Built Taiwan

The Generalissimo’s legacy is the most contested in modern Chinese history on both sides of the Taiwan Strait. On the mainland, he is a villain — the corrupt, incompetent nationalist who lost the civil war. In older Taiwan, he was a national hero; in younger, democratic Taiwan, he is a complicated figure whose statues are increasingly controversial.

The fair historical assessment lands somewhere more complex: Chiang was a genuine nationalist who believed in China’s greatness and had the misfortune of fighting simultaneously on two fronts — against Japan and against the Communists — with resources inadequate for either. His mainland government was corrupt, not by his personal enrichment but by the endemic corruption of a party-state that had governed through patronage for decades. His decision to flee to Taiwan rather than fight to the last on the mainland — arguably the decision that created Taiwan’s miracle — was partly military pragmatism and partly the instinct of a man who wanted to survive to fight another day.

On Taiwan, stripped of the mainland’s feudal constraints, he implemented the land reform he had never managed to implement on the mainland. He built institutions rather than just exercising raw power. The authoritarian methods were brutal; the developmental outcomes were extraordinary. He created, by accident perhaps more than design, the conditions in which a genuinely democratic and prosperous Taiwan could emerge after his death. That is a legacy few authoritarian rulers can match.

Chiang Ching-kuo (蔣經國, 1910–1988): The Authoritarian Who Chose Democracy

Chiang Ching-kuo’s biography reads like a novel about moral transformation. He was the son of privilege who spent his youth as a genuine communist revolutionary in the Soviet Union — studying, marrying a Russian woman, working in a factory, sincerely believing in the socialist project. He returned to China to work for his father’s nationalist government, renouncing his communism and becoming viciously anti-communist in a display of converted-sinner ferocity.

He ran Taiwan’s intelligence and security apparatus with the ruthlessness of a man who understood exactly how dangerous to political stability those he was suppressing were. The White Terror of the 1950s — the imprisonment and execution of thousands of suspected communists and political dissidents — happened on his watch and with his direct involvement.

And then, as if biography had run out of predictable moves, he chose democracy. The DPP founding, the martial law lifting, the native Taiwanese succession — these were not the decisions of a man clinging to power but of a man who understood that the system he represented had reached its limits, and that something better needed to replace it.

His most lasting legacy may be Lee Teng-hui — the man he chose as his successor, knowing that a native Taiwanese president was what the next phase of Taiwan’s development required. This succession choice — entrusting the continuation of his life’s work to someone from the community his father’s government had massacred — is among the most remarkable acts of political responsibility in modern Asian history.

K.Y. Yin (尹仲容, 1903–1963): The Forgotten Architect

History forgets the planners and remembers the politicians. K.Y. Yin — who shaped Taiwan’s economic strategy at the critical juncture when the basic choices were being made — is almost unknown outside specialist economic history circles.

He was an engineer by training who became Taiwan’s most important economic policymaker in the 1950s and early 1960s. His intellectual achievements: understanding, before most development economists had articulated it, that export orientation would outperform import substitution; insisting that government policy support private enterprise rather than replace it; building the institutional infrastructure of development finance without which private investment could not have scaled.

He died in 1963 — before the Export Processing Zones, before Hsinchu, before TSMC. But the institutional architecture he built was the platform on which all of these later achievements stood. Taiwan has never adequately honoured him.

Morris Chang (張忠謀, born 1931): The Most Consequential Technology Entrepreneur of the 20th Century

The competition for that title is serious: Bill Gates, Steve Jobs, Andy Grove, Gordon Moore. But there is an argument — not often made but worth making — that Morris Chang’s foundry model invention was the most consequential of all.

Gates gave the world system software. Jobs gave the world design-led consumer technology. Grove built Intel into a manufacturing giant. Moore gave us the observation that described the industry’s trajectory. Chang restructured the entire industry — created the conditions in which fabless design could exist, in which NVIDIA and Qualcomm and Apple’s silicon division and AMD and dozens of others could exist. The AI revolution of the 2020s — every large language model, every image generator, every autonomous vehicle system — runs on chips whose existence depends on the foundry model that Morris Chang invented in 1987 on a small industrial estate in Hsinchu.

He is 93 years old as this report is written. He retired from TSMC’s chairmanship in 2018 (and briefly returned to lead it again in 2009–2013 when he determined that his successor was not developing the company aggressively enough). He remains lucid, engaged, and characteristically direct in public appearances.

What makes Chang extraordinary beyond the foundry insight: his personal qualities. He is genuinely humble about individual achievement (constantly crediting his team) while being remarkably confident about strategic direction. He has made strategic decisions that seemed wrong to the market at the time — particularly the decision to invest aggressively in leading-edge manufacturing when the market would have been satisfied with trailing-edge — and been proven comprehensively right. His strategic patience is a quality that cultures oriented toward quarterly results rarely produce.

Lee Teng-hui (李登輝, 1923–2020): The Taiwanese Who Named Taiwan

Lee Teng-hui was the first Taiwanese to lead Taiwan, the first ethnic Han Chinese to win a direct popular presidential election in history, and the man who articulated — first tentatively, then explicitly — the concept of a distinct Taiwanese identity that was Chinese in culture but not Chinese in political allegiance.

His academic background (Cornell PhD in agricultural economics) gave him an analytical precision that made him unusual among politicians. His Japanese education had given him, paradoxically, some emotional distance from the nationalist narrative of Chinese identity that the KMT had promoted for decades. He saw Taiwan from the inside with the clarity of someone who had also seen it from the outside.

His most significant political achievement was not any single policy but a transformation of political culture: he normalised the idea that Taiwan’s people, not any historical claim, were the legitimate source of political authority. This seems obvious in democratic theory; in Taiwan’s specific historical context, it was revolutionary.

Terry Gou (郭台銘, born 1950): The Blue-Collar Billionaire

Terry Gou is a study in undiluted entrepreneurial will. He is not an engineer — he studied marine transport at a vocational institute — and he is not a technologist. He is a manufacturer, a builder, an operator who understands that making things requires discipline, scale, and the relentless elimination of waste and variation.

His management style has been controversial: Foxconn’s working conditions, particularly in the early years of its massive Chinese expansion, were brutal by Western standards — long hours, crowded dormitories, stressful assembly line pacing. A series of worker suicides at Foxconn’s Shenzhen campus in 2010 brought intense international scrutiny. Gou’s response — installing suicide prevention nets on buildings, raising wages, improving dormitory conditions — was pragmatic rather than philosophically driven. He is a man of the factory floor, not the philosophy seminar.

What is undeniable: he built something extraordinary. One million people working for one company under one management structure, producing products at quality and scale that no other organisation on Earth could match. That achievement represents an organisational and managerial capability that is as genuinely remarkable as any technology breakthrough.

Jensen Huang (黃仁勳, born 1963): The Man Who Saw the AI Revolution Coming

Jensen Huang was born in Tainan, Taiwan, and came to the United States at age nine. He studied electrical engineering at Oregon State University and Stanford, worked at AMD and LSI Logic, and in 1993 co-founded NVIDIA with a specific vision: graphics processors would transform computing.

He was right — but in the end, more right than he had even originally imagined. The GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) he built for gaming turned out to be the perfect architecture for the massively parallel computation that deep learning requires. When the AI revolution arrived in the 2010s — accelerated by the 2012 breakthrough of AlexNet training on a pair of NVIDIA GPUs — Huang’s company was uniquely positioned to provide the hardware.

NVIDIA’s H100 chip — the current workhorse of AI training, which every AI company in the world is desperate to acquire — is manufactured entirely by TSMC. Jensen Huang, born in Taiwan, runs a company whose most critical product is manufactured in Taiwan, whose company is the most important customer of a Taiwan-founded company. He is a living embodiment of the Taiwan-Silicon Valley nexus — the diaspora network that continues to connect the island’s manufacturing genius with the world’s technology markets.

NVIDIA’s market capitalisation briefly exceeded $3.3 trillion in 2024 — making it the most valuable company in the world at that moment — built substantially on AI chips manufactured at 3nm in TSMC’s fabs in Hsinchu.

Lisa Su (蘇姿丰, born 1969): The Engineer Who Turbo-Charged the Underdog

Lisa Su was born in Tainan, raised partly in the US, educated at MIT (BS, MS, and PhD in electrical engineering — all from MIT, a distinction that almost no technology executive can match in its academic depth). She joined AMD in 2012 and became CEO in 2014, inheriting a company that was struggling badly — losing money, losing market share to Intel, widely written off by Wall Street.

Her turnaround of AMD is a masterclass in applying engineering judgment to business strategy. She correctly identified that AMD’s existing chip architectures were not competitive and needed fundamental redesign. She bet the company on the Zen CPU architecture and the RDNA GPU architecture — multi-year engineering projects that required holding course against quarterly pressure for immediate results.

The Zen architecture, beginning with its 2017 launch, was competitive with Intel at launch and superior within two years. AMD’s stock went from approximately $2 in 2015 to over $150 by 2022. The company’s market capitalisation grew from approximately $2 billion to approximately $200 billion under her leadership — one of the most remarkable corporate turnarounds in technology history.

AMD’s best chips are manufactured at TSMC. Lisa Su — born in Taiwan, educated in the US — runs a company that depends entirely on the island’s manufacturing capability. Like Jensen Huang, she is the living bridge between Taiwan’s manufacturing genius and the global technology market.

Peter Tsai (蔡秉燚, born 1952): The Man Who Protected the World

Peter Tsai is not a corporate leader or a policymaker — he is a materials scientist. Born in Taiwan, he spent his career at Oak Ridge National Laboratory in Tennessee, where he developed melt-blown polypropylene electrostatic filtration technology — the material that makes N95 masks capable of filtering out 95% of airborne particles.

When COVID-19 spread globally in 2020, creating desperate worldwide demand for N95 masks, the material that made those masks work had been invented by a Taiwanese-American scientist working in a US government laboratory. Tsai, who had retired by then, temporarily came back to help address the manufacturing scale-up challenge.

His inclusion in this gallery is deliberate: Taiwan’s contribution to humanity is not limited to semiconductors. It extends to materials science, biomedical research, and countless other fields where the Taiwanese-American scientific diaspora has worked in institutions across the world. Peter Tsai’s N95 material may have saved as many lives as TSMC’s chips have enabled.

CHAPTER 11 — Taiwan’s Future: The Most Dangerous Island on Earth

The Military Calculus

Every serious analysis of Taiwan’s future must begin with the military question — because without security, everything else is conditional.

China’s military modernisation over the past 25 years has been extraordinary in scope and pace. The People’s Liberation Army Navy has grown from a coastal defense force of limited capability to a blue-water navy with aircraft carriers, advanced destroyers, and submarine forces capable of operations across the Indo-Pacific. The PLA’s ballistic and cruise missile forces can now range every target in Taiwan multiple times over. Advanced air defences, anti-satellite weapons, cyber capabilities, and information warfare capacities have been developed with systematic determination.

The relevant military debate: when, if ever, would China have the capability to conduct a successful amphibious invasion of Taiwan?

Military analysts note that Xi Jinping has set 2027 as the target year for completing military modernisation — sparking considerable debate about whether this implies an intent to act against Taiwan by that date. The more careful reading: 2027 is the completion target for military capability development, not a deadline for military action. Capability is not intent. But the convergence of growing capability, Xi’s personal identification with “reunification,” and the increasingly assertive Chinese foreign policy posture makes the period 2027–2035 the focus of most serious strategic planning.

An amphibious invasion of Taiwan would be the most complex military operation in history — significantly more demanding than the D-Day landings of 1944. The Taiwan Strait is 180 kilometres wide; keeping the sea lanes open for the extended period required to land sufficient forces, sustain them, and prevail against determined resistance would require defeating American naval and air forces, if they intervene, while fighting through Taiwan’s island defense. The Taiwan military, while smaller than the PLA, is positioned defensively and equipped for exactly this scenario. Geography favours the defender. The cost of failure — a failed invasion that the CCP could not survive politically — would be catastrophic for Beijing.

What has not changed: the fundamental difficulty of the military challenge. The Taiwan Strait is not a river to cross; it is a moat that turns any crossing force into a target for missile, air, and submarine attack throughout the crossing.

What has changed: the balance of forces. China’s growing grey-zone capability (cyber attacks, information warfare, economic pressure, incremental land-filling operations) provides options short of outright invasion. The question of American commitment has become less certain as US domestic politics has become more transactional. Taiwan’s own defense investment, while recently increased (toward 2.5% of GDP), remains below what serious defense analysts believe the porcupine strategy requires.

The Porcupine: Taiwan’s Defense Philosophy

Taiwan’s defense strategy must confront an obvious arithmetic: you cannot build a military that can defeat China’s military. The PLA is simply too large. The strategy therefore must be different: make Taiwan too costly to invade, not too strong to defeat.

The porcupine doctrine — articulated clearly by American strategist William Murray in 2008 and increasingly adopted in Taiwanese defense planning — focuses on asymmetric capabilities:

Mobile ground-based missile batteries that can survive a first strike and continue threatening Chinese naval forces. Anti-ship missiles — the Hsiung Feng III (capable of Mach 2+ speeds, range over 400km) and the US-supplied Harpoon — that can bring losses to any invasion fleet. Sea mines — the most cost-effective anti-ship weapon available — laid throughout the strait’s shallow waters. Hardened command-and-control networks that cannot be decapitated by early missile strikes. Stockpiles of ammunition, food, fuel, and medical supplies for extended conflict under a naval blockade. Cyber and information warfare capabilities that can degrade Chinese communication and logistics.

The goal is to ensure that any Chinese invasion force would take such severe losses — in ships, in aircraft, in troops — in the crossing and initial landing phase that the rational cost-benefit calculation for Beijing would be prohibitive. Combined with the Silicon Shield’s economic deterrent and American military commitment, the porcupine strategy aims to push the invasion decision permanently beyond the range of rational choice.

India’s interest: Taiwan’s porcupine strategy is directly relevant to India’s own defense planning against a more powerful adversary. The application of asymmetric approaches — precision missiles, naval mines, distributed decision-making, hardened infrastructure — to deny a superior power the ability to project force profitably is a concept with direct application to India’s Himalayan border challenges. Taiwan has, through necessity, become one of the world’s most sophisticated practitioners of asymmetric defense strategy. Indian defense planners study it closely.

The Generational Identity Crisis

Demographics and identity are intersecting in Taiwan in a way that has profound strategic implications.

Taiwan’s birth rate (1.09 total fertility rate in 2020) is among the lowest in the world — lower even than Japan and South Korea. The population of 23 million is aging rapidly. The TSMC engineering pipeline, already strained, will become more constrained as the domestic talent pool shrinks. TSMC has begun recruiting internationally — Indian, Vietnamese, and other Asian engineers are increasingly working in Hsinchu fabs — as a response to domestic shortages.

More significantly: the generational identity shift is accelerating and appears irreversible. Surveys consistently show that over 80% of Taiwanese aged 18–29 identify as Taiwanese rather than Chinese. This generation has grown up seeing Hong Kong’s autonomy systematically dismantled after 2019 and 2020 — the destruction of exactly the “one country, two systems” model that Beijing had proposed for Taiwan. The Hong Kong experience has been more effective at motivating Taiwanese resistance to unification than any Taiwanese government propaganda could ever have been.

The generation that remembers the mainland — that spoke of “returning” to China as an aspiration — is dying. The generation that has inherited Taiwan has no emotional connection to the mainland project and has watched the alternative up close. The political demographics of Taiwan are moving, steadily and irreversibly, toward a population that identifies as Taiwanese, not Chinese — with enormous implications for any negotiation about the island’s political future.

Taiwan in the World: The Creative Workaround State

Taiwan occupies a uniquely absurd position in international affairs: a functioning democracy of 23 million people with a $750 billion economy, a seat on no international body, formal diplomatic relations with only 12 countries (mostly small Pacific island states), and forced to participate in the Olympics under the name “Chinese Taipei.”

The creative response to this absurdity is the unofficial relations system — an elaborate diplomatic fiction maintained by nearly every country in the world. The American Institute in Taiwan (AIT) in Taipei is, in practice, the American embassy: staffed by American diplomats, managing the full range of bilateral relations, processing visas, issuing statements. It is simply not called an embassy. Every country with interests in Taiwan maintains an equivalent — “trade offices,” “cultural centres,” “economic offices” — that perform every function of an embassy without being called one.

This system is bizarre, pragmatically functional, and occasionally surreal (Taiwan cannot send a government minister to most international conferences; it must send representatives of “the government in Taipei” or “Chinese Taipei”). But it has enabled Taiwan to maintain substantive relationships with virtually every significant country in the world, to participate in trade negotiations, to coordinate on technology policy and export controls, and to function as a genuine member of the international order — without the formal recognition that would trigger war.

Taiwan’s soft power: Taiwan has also built genuine global affinity through its democratic example, its COVID-19 response (which was exemplary), its LGBTQ rights (the first country in Asia to legalise same-sex marriage, in 2019), and its cultural exports (not as dramatic as Korean Hallyu, but real — specifically with the global Mandarin-speaking diaspora). This soft power is a genuine strategic asset, generating goodwill that formal diplomatic recognition alone cannot purchase.

The Only Path That Has Always Worked

When Taiwan’s planners in the 1950s faced their circumstances — small island, no resources, hostile neighbor, limited international recognition — they made a bet. Not a calculated bet in the modern strategy-consultant sense but an instinctive one: invest in education, build institutions, attract talent, make things well enough that the world values making them here.

They could not have known that the path they chose would eventually run through 3nm transistors. They knew only that excellence — sustained, compounding, demanding — was the only path available to a country that could not rely on oil, territory, or great-power patronage for its security.

The Silicon Shield is the outcome. TSMC is the culmination. Taiwan’s 75-year bet on human capital over natural resources, on institutional quality over raw power, on manufacturing excellence over military might — has produced the most strategically valuable asset in the global technology economy.

But the Shield is not static. China’s self-sufficiency drive, if successful, weakens the economic deterrent. American commitment, if it wavers, weakens the military deterrent. The demographic clock ticks. The identity question remains unresolved. And Xi Jinping’s China grows more assertive each year, testing the limits of the ambiguous equilibrium that has kept the peace since 1949.

Taiwan’s future is genuinely uncertain. Will it remain de facto independent indefinitely? Will the US commitment hold as China’s power grows? Will American semiconductor reshoring eventually reduce Taiwan’s indispensability sufficiently that the strategic calculus changes? These are questions on which the future of the global technology order — and the peace of the world — depends.

What is not uncertain: the lesson that Taiwan’s journey offers to every small nation facing an uncertain future. The lesson is not about semiconductors. It is about the strategy of excellence:

Make yourself so good at something so important that the world cannot afford to let you fail.

Taiwan could not chose its geography, its history, its 1949 moment of crisis, or the nuclear-armed neighbor that claims it. It could choose — and it chose — to be the best in the world at something that the world desperately needed. It chose institutions over institutions’ absence, education over ignorance, technology over mere labor, democracy over the authoritarian shortcuts that felt safer in the Cold War moment.

The Silicon Shield is real. Whether it will hold is the question on which the future hangs. But the fact that it exists — that a 394-kilometre island of 23 million people who had no natural resources and were living under an immediate invasion threat manufactured the most important objects in modern civilization — is itself proof that the choice of excellence, consistently made and patiently sustained, can change everything.

In a world that increasingly organises itself around the question of whether great powers will allow small nations to exist on their own terms, Taiwan’s answer has been to make the question itself prohibitively expensive. That answer took 75 years to develop. Whether it lasts another 75 is the most important open question in geopolitics today.
