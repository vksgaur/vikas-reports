From the Currency Counter at Chennai Airport to the $7.5 Trillion Market That Moves Nations

Picture yourself at the currency exchange counter at Chennai International Airport. You slide ₹8,500 across the counter. The person on the other side counts out exactly one hundred US dollar bills and slides them back. You’ve just participated in one of seven and a half trillion dollars’ worth of the same essential transaction that will happen today — not at airport kiosks, but between banks, governments, hedge funds, and corporations on six continents, in milliseconds, simultaneously, without pause.

That ratio — 85 rupees buying one dollar — is an exchange rate. It seems like a mundane conversion, a slightly annoying arithmetic problem for travellers. But pull back the camera, layer by layer, and that number at the airport counter turns out to be the most consequential price in the global economy — a price that has toppled governments, created billionaires overnight, driven entire nations into famine, and restructured the factories of the world. The hedge fund that bet against the British pound in 1992. The Thai baht peg that unravelled and took down an entire continent’s economies in 1997. China’s deliberately cheap renminbi that hollowed out American manufacturing. The Argentine peso that destroyed the life savings of an entire middle class. The German mark that collapsed so completely that wheelbarrows of cash couldn’t buy bread.

All of it runs through exchange rates. This report is your complete, unsimplified, fully honest account of what they are, how they work, why they move, how governments manipulate them, how markets break them, and where they are heading in a world of digital currencies and shifting geopolitical power. Start with one person at one counter. End with the whole world.

CHAPTER 1 — What Is an Exchange Rate? The Most Important Price in the World

₹8,500 for $100: A Transaction Hiding Enormous Complexity

The transaction at the Chennai airport counter is simple in form and enormous in implication. The ₹85/$ exchange rate you received is not just a conversion factor — it is the single number that summarises the relative value of everything the Indian economy produces compared to everything the American economy produces. When that number moves — when the rupee weakens from ₹85 to ₹90 per dollar — the price of petrol at your local pump ticks up, because India imports 85% of its crude oil in dollars. The quarterly earnings of Infosys change. The cost of studying at an American university for an Indian student rises. The profit margin of a textile exporter in Tiruppur improves. And somewhere in Mumbai, a trader glances at a screen and makes a decision that shifts millions.

Exchange rates are not just travellers’ arithmetic. They are — in the words of economist Avinash Dixit — “the most important prices in an open economy.” More important, in their economy-wide implications, than the price of rice, petrol, or even real estate. Because exchange rates are not the price of one thing or one sector — they are the price of everything a country produces relative to everything another country produces. When the rupee weakens against the dollar, every Indian export becomes cheaper for Americans and every American import becomes more expensive for Indians. The entire price structure of the economy tilts.

What an Exchange Rate Actually Measures

At its most fundamental, an exchange rate is the relative price of two currencies — the number of units of one currency required to buy one unit of another. But the deeper truth is what that relative price represents: it is the market’s collective verdict on the relative purchasing power, productivity, and stability of two entire economies.

Two ways to quote the same rate: When financial news says “the rupee is at 85,” it means ₹85 buys $1. This is the direct quotation from India’s perspective — how many rupees per dollar. The indirect quotation would be the reciprocal: 0.0118perrupee.Theconventionmattersbecausethedirectionofmovementchangesinterpretation.Ifyouquote₹/ and the number rises (85 to 90), the rupee is weakening — you need more rupees to buy the same dollar. If you quote $/₹ and the number rises ($0.012 to $0.013), the rupee is strengthening — each rupee buys more dollars. Financial headlines use “rupee strengthens” or “rupee weakens” to avoid this confusion, but understanding the underlying arithmetic is essential for reading currency data correctly.

Nominal vs. real exchange rate: The nominal exchange rate is the number at the airport counter. The real exchange rate adjusts for inflation differentials — it is what matters for actual trade competitiveness. If the rupee depreciates 5% against the dollar but India’s inflation is also 5% higher than America’s, the real exchange rate is unchanged. The nominal depreciation has merely offset the inflation differential; Indian goods are no more competitive in world markets than before.

The Forex Market: Bigger Than Everything Else Combined

The foreign exchange market — forex or FX — is the largest financial market in the human world, by an extraordinary margin. The New York Stock Exchange trades approximately $20–25 billion per day. The entire global equity market — every stock exchange in the world combined — trades roughly $200 billion per day. The global forex market trades $7.5 trillion every single day, 365 days a year, without pause, on no single exchange, across a decentralised network of banks, brokers, and institutions connected by electronic systems.

To give a sense of scale: if you converted the daily forex trading volume into ₹100 notes and stacked them, the pile would reach the Moon. Twelve times over.

Who are the participants in this market?

Central banks — the most powerful participants. They do not trade for profit but for policy: buying or selling their currency to influence its value, managing reserves, and implementing monetary strategy.

Commercial banks — the primary market-makers. Every time an importer or exporter needs to convert currency, the bank is on the other side of the trade. The interbank market — banks trading with each other in enormous quantities — is the heart of the forex system.

Multinational corporations — businesses like Tata Consultancy Services, which earns in dollars and pays employees in rupees, or Reliance, which imports oil in dollars. Their need to convert between currencies creates constant, substantial, predictable flows through the forex market.

Hedge funds and institutional investors — some of the most aggressive participants, often trading on the basis of macroeconomic analysis, interest rate differentials, or the kind of structural vulnerability analysis that George Soros used to break the Bank of England.

Retail traders — millions of individuals trading currency through platforms, typically losing money because they are competing against institutions with vastly superior information, technology, and capital.

Spot Rates and Forward Rates

The spot rate is today’s exchange rate — the price at which you can exchange currency right now. The ₹85/$ at Chennai airport is a spot rate.

The forward rate is an agreed exchange rate for a transaction that will happen at a specific future date — typically 30, 60, or 90 days ahead, though contracts extend to years. Forward rates are not predictions; they are contractual agreements that businesses use to eliminate the uncertainty of future exchange rate movements.

Here is why forward rates matter enormously for real businesses: Infosys signs a contract today to deliver software to an American company for 10million, withpaymentdueinsixmonths.Attoday′srateof₹85/, that 10M = ₹850million—perfectlyprofitable.Butiftherupeestrengthensto₹75/ over the next six months, that same $10 million will convert to only ₹750 million — ₹100 million less than budgeted, potentially turning profit into loss. Infosys can eliminate this risk by entering a forward contract today: agreeing to sell 10millionat₹84/ in six months (the forward rate is slightly different from the spot rate, reflecting interest rate differentials between the two countries). Whatever the spot rate in six months, Infosys will receive ₹840 million. The profit is locked in. The currency risk is transferred to the bank that wrote the forward contract.

The forward market is therefore not speculation — it is insurance. And its existence is one of the reasons that large international businesses can plan, invest, and operate across borders without being destroyed by currency volatility.

The Body Temperature Analogy

If the economy were a human body, the exchange rate would be the body temperature: one single number that reflects the health of everything inside, reacts immediately to any stress, and can — if it moves too far in either direction — signal either crisis or recovery.

A normal temperature is healthy. A fever (rapidly weakening currency) signals infection — a loss of confidence, capital flight, or fiscal excess. Hypothermia (an artificially suppressed currency) might feel stable but is actually an artificial condition maintained by external force, and it cannot be sustained indefinitely without the underlying pathology reasserting itself.

This analogy does a lot of work in this report. Keep it in mind as we move to the forces that make the temperature rise and fall.

CHAPTER 2 — Why Exchange Rates Move: The Seven Forces

Force 1: Supply and Demand — The Vegetable Market Logic

Every exchange rate, at its most fundamental, is a supply and demand curve. Think of the forex market as a vegetable market where rupees are the vegetable being bought and sold.

When foreigners want rupees — to buy Indian goods, invest in Indian companies, pay for Indian IT services, or visit India as tourists — they bring their own currencies to the market and purchase rupees. Demand for rupees rises. Like a vegetable in short supply, the rupee’s price rises: it appreciates.

When Indians need foreign currency — to pay for oil imports, buy foreign machinery, send children to American universities, or invest abroad — they sell rupees in exchange for dollars, euros, or yen. Supply of rupees rises. Like a vegetable with too much supply, the rupee’s price falls: it depreciates.

The beauty of this framework is its apparent simplicity, and the complexity lies in what determines whether demand or supply dominates on any given day — which is everything that follows in this chapter.

Force 2: Interest Rate Differentials and the Carry Trade

The most powerful short-term driver of currency movements — and one of the great generators of global financial volatility — is the interest rate differential between countries.

Here is the arithmetic: if Indian government bonds offer 7% annual interest and Japanese government bonds offer 0.1%, where would you prefer your savings? India, obviously. But to invest in Indian bonds, you need rupees. So you borrow cheaply in yen (at 0.1% interest), convert to rupees, and invest at 7%. The interest rate spread — 6.9 percentage points — is largely profit, after currency risk is taken into account.

This strategy is called the carry trade — you “carry” the position, earning the interest differential. And when thousands of global fund managers simultaneously make the same calculation — borrowing in low-interest currencies and investing in high-interest currencies — the collective effect is enormous. Billions of dollars flow into the high-yield currency. The rupee strengthens. Which makes the trade even more profitable in the short term (currency gain on top of interest gain). Which attracts more capital. Which strengthens the rupee further.

And then something changes — a global risk event, a Federal Reserve rate hike, a domestic political shock — and the carry trade reverses. The fund managers need to repay their yen loans. They sell rupees, buy yen. Simultaneously. The rupee falls sharply. The yen rises sharply. The currencies that carry-trade capital had inflated on the way in deflate on the way out, with stunning speed.

This is not a theoretical risk. The 1997 Asian financial crisis — one of the most devastating economic events of the 20th century — was substantially a carry trade reversal at massive scale. Asian economies offered high interest rates; global capital poured in, inflating currencies and asset prices; when confidence cracked, the reversal was violent and the currency collapses catastrophic.

In India, this is why the RBI watches US Federal Reserve decisions with such intensity. When the Fed raises interest rates — as it did dramatically from near-zero to 5%+ between 2022 and 2023 — the interest rate differential between the US and India narrows. Capital that was in India seeking yield has less reason to stay. The carry trade partially reverses, the rupee faces selling pressure, and the RBI decides how aggressively to respond.

Force 3: Inflation and Purchasing Power Parity

If Indian inflation runs at 6% per year and American inflation at 2%, something powerful and logical happens over time: Indian goods gradually become more expensive relative to American goods (the Indian price level rises faster). To remain competitive in world markets, Indian goods must become cheaper in dollar terms — which means the rupee must depreciate. Approximately 4% per year (the inflation differential), over time, in the long run.

This is Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) — the theory that in the long run, exchange rates adjust to equalize the price of identical goods across countries. It is not a short-term forecasting tool; exchange rates deviate substantially from PPP levels for years. But over decades, it is a reliable guide to exchange rate direction.

The Big Mac Index — invented by The Economist magazine in 1986 as a lighthearted PPP application — makes this tangible. A McDonald’s Big Mac sells for approximately $5.69 in the United States and approximately ₹220 in India. If PPP held perfectly, the implied exchange rate would be ₹220 / $5.69 = approximately ₹38.7 per dollar. The actual exchange rate is ₹85 per dollar — meaning the rupee is “undervalued” by about 55% against the dollar on this measure, or that Indian prices are dramatically cheaper than American prices in dollar terms.

This undervaluation is not surprising — it reflects genuine productivity differences and the lower cost of non-tradeable services (haircuts, restaurant meals, domestic help) in India. But the Big Mac Index beautifully illustrates the gap between what simple exchange rate conversion shows and what real purchasing power shows — which is why India’s per capita GDP at market exchange rates ($2,500) looks so different from India’s per capita GDP at purchasing power parity ($8,900).

Force 4: The Current Account Balance

A country’s current account records all its international transactions in goods, services, income, and transfers. The most important component is the trade balance: exports minus imports.

When India imports more than it exports — as it typically does, running a trade deficit — it is, in aggregate, constantly selling rupees to buy foreign currency for those imports. This persistent excess supply of rupees in the global market creates downward pressure on the rupee over time.

Conversely, Germany, China, and Japan — which run persistent trade surpluses — are constantly receiving more foreign currency than they spend. They are net buyers of local currency in the global market, which supports (and in China’s case, inflates) the value of their currencies.

India’s current account deficit has historically been approximately 1–3% of GDP, fluctuating with oil prices (higher oil prices = larger import bill = larger deficit = more rupee pressure). This structural deficit — India consistently imports more than it exports — is a persistent downward force on the rupee that other factors (capital inflows) must offset for the rupee to remain stable.

Force 5: Capital Flows — The Dominant Short-Term Driver

Here is something that surprises most people: global trade in goods and services — the buying and selling of actual things and actual services — accounts for roughly 2% of daily forex market volume. The remaining 98% is capital flows: financial investments hopping across borders in search of returns, safety, and opportunity.

This means that in the short run, what moves the rupee is not whether India exports more software or imports more oil. What moves the rupee is whether foreign institutional investors (FIIs) are buying or selling Indian stocks and bonds.

When a pension fund in Norway decides to increase its allocation to “Indian equities,” it buys billions of dollars of Indian stocks. To do so, it converts euros to rupees — billions worth — in a matter of hours. The rupee strengthens. When Indian markets fall and the same pension fund reduces its India allocation, it sells the stocks, converts rupees back to euros, and the rupee weakens.

In 2022, when global interest rates rose and emerging markets faced a “risk-off” environment, foreign investors pulled approximately $50 billion out of Indian equities and bonds in twelve months. The rupee fell from approximately ₹74 to ₹83 per dollar. Not because India’s exports fell or its current account worsened dramatically — but because thousands of foreign portfolio managers simultaneously clicked “sell India” and converted their rupee holdings to dollars.

This capital flow dominance of forex markets is one of the central challenges of modern exchange rate management. It creates the possibility of sudden, volatile currency moves that have little to do with the underlying economic fundamentals — and a great deal to do with the mood of global finance.

Force 6: Political Stability and Institutional Trust

Currencies are ultimately backed by institutional trust — the collective belief that the government, central bank, and legal system of a country will behave predictably. When that trust is present, investors are willing to hold assets in that currency. When it collapses, capital flight happens with stunning speed.

In 1991, India’s gold was quietly loaded onto aircraft and flown to the Bank of England for pledging — because institutional trust in India’s ability to meet its international obligations had evaporated. The rupee had to be devalued. In 1998, political crises in Russia caused a sudden loss of confidence; the ruble collapsed and the Russian government defaulted on its debt. In 2013, when the US Fed merely hinted at reducing its bond-buying programme, currencies from India to Indonesia to Brazil fell sharply — not because their fundamentals had changed, but because investors suddenly began reassessing emerging market risk.

India’s relatively stable democracy and independent central bank give the rupee a degree of institutional credibility that insulates it somewhat from the most extreme capital flight scenarios. This is not a minor point — it is the difference between managed depreciation and currency collapse.

Force 7: Central Bank Interventions

When all other forces have been set, the central bank enters the room. The Reserve Bank of India can, at any moment, buy rupees by selling its foreign exchange reserves (reducing rupee supply, supporting the rupee price) or sell rupees by buying foreign exchange (increasing rupee supply, weakening the rupee). This intervention can smooth excessive volatility and prevent the carry-trade and capital-flow dynamics from producing disorderly markets.

But central bank intervention is a war of attrition. The RBI’s foreign exchange reserves — approximately $620 billion as of early 2024 — are large by historical standards. But the global forex market trades $7.5 trillion per day. If global markets are determined to push the rupee down, the RBI can slow but not permanently stop the decline by selling reserves. Intervention buys time; it cannot buy the wrong exchange rate indefinitely.

CHAPTER 3 — Strong Currency vs. Weak Currency: The Great Economic Paradox

Demolishing the Folk Myth

Ask any middle-class Indian what they think of a “stronger rupee,” and the answer will almost certainly be: “good.” Respectability, purchasing power, national pride. The folk wisdom is that currency strength equals national strength — a strong nation has a strong currency.

This intuition is not wrong in every context. But it is dangerously incomplete, and understanding its incompleteness is essential to understanding almost every major exchange rate story in modern economic history.

The truth is more paradoxical: a strong currency is simultaneously great news for some people in an economy and catastrophic news for others. Which group you belong to depends on whether you import or export, whether you earn in foreign currency or local currency, whether you are a manufacturer or a consumer, and what stage of development your country is at. There is no universally correct currency strength — only the level that is correct for your economy’s current needs and structure.

The Exporter’s World: When Weakness Is Strength

Imagine you run a garment factory in Tiruppur. Your costs — wages, electricity, rent — are all in rupees. Your revenue comes almost entirely from American and European buyers who pay in dollars and euros.

When the rupee is at ₹70/$, your factory produces a shirt for ₹700 (the rupee cost). In dollar terms, that shirt costs $10. An American retailer compares this to a Vietnamese shirt at $8 and a Bangladeshi shirt at $7 — you are not competitive.

When the rupee depreciates to ₹85/$, the same ₹700 shirt now costs only $8.24 in dollar terms — suddenly competitive. Your order book fills up. You hire more workers. You expand.

When the rupee further weakens to ₹90/$, the shirt costs $7.78 — you are price matching Bangladesh. Your factory runs three shifts.

This is the exporter’s exchange rate logic — and it is the economic story behind every Asian manufacturing miracle. Japan in the 1950s, Taiwan in the 1960s, South Korea in the 1970s, China in the 1990s: in every case, a deliberately or incidentally cheap currency was a crucial enabler of export-led growth. The undervalued currency was an implicit subsidy to every exporter, funded by the reduced purchasing power of every consumer.

Currency manipulation: When a government deliberately suppresses its currency below its market value to gain this export advantage, it is called currency manipulation. China’s management of the renminbi from 1994 to approximately 2010 is the most consequential and most debated example. By preventing the renminbi from appreciating to its market value — through capital controls that prevented Chinese citizens from freely converting renminbi to other currencies, combined with central bank purchases of massive quantities of dollar reserves — China’s goods remained artificially cheap in global markets. The US Treasury under multiple administrations has accused China of manipulating its currency; China has argued that it was simply managing volatility. The debate remains live, though China’s trade surplus and currency management have evolved significantly since 2010.

The Importer and Consumer’s World: When Strength Matters

Now stop being the shirt factory owner and become the Indian government’s finance ministry, staring at the imported crude oil bill.

India imports approximately 85% of its crude oil. Oil is priced globally in US dollars. When the rupee is at ₹70/$, a barrel of oil at 80costs₹5, 600.Whentherupeeweakensto₹85/, the same barrel now costs ₹6,800 — a 21% increase in rupee terms, purely from the exchange rate change, before any global price change has occurred. This directly translates into higher petrol and diesel prices, higher freight costs for every good transported by road, and inflationary pressure throughout the economy.

For a country like India — where food inflation is politically explosive and oil import costs are the largest single driver of current account deficits — currency weakness is an inflationary tax on every citizen, falling most heavily on the poorest who spend the highest share of their income on food and fuel.

The strong-currency advantages: - Cheaper imports: Every imported good — oil, machinery, electronics, fertiliser — costs less in rupee terms - Inflation suppression: Cheaper imports reduce cost pressures throughout the economy - Debt management: India’s external debt denominated in dollars and euros is cheaper to service - Consumer purchasing power: Foreign travel, foreign education, imported goods become more affordable - Investor confidence: A stable, strong currency signals macroeconomic discipline and attracts long-term capital

The RBI’s Tightrope

The Reserve Bank of India operates under this permanent tension — and it does so with a sophistication that many outside the building do not fully appreciate.

Too weak a rupee: the oil import bill explodes, inflation rises, the current account deficit widens, the poor are squeezed, and investor confidence may eventually desert a currency on a sustained depreciation path.

Too strong a rupee: IT exports — India’s largest foreign exchange earner — become more expensive; manufacturing ambitions (the PLI scheme investments) face a competitiveness headwind; the tradeable goods sector struggles against cheaper competition.

The RBI’s solution is the managed float: not fixing the rupee at any specific level, not letting it float freely either, but actively intervening when the rupee’s movement becomes disorderly or sharply inconsistent with the fundamentals — while allowing gradual adjustments that reflect India’s core reality (somewhat higher inflation than trading partners → gradual long-term depreciation trend).

It is not an elegant solution. There is no elegant solution. The exchange rate is the economy’s most consequential and least manageable price, and the central bank who claims to have mastered it is either lying or understating the difficulty.

CHAPTER 4 — Exchange Rate Regimes: The Three Great Systems

Every government must choose — explicitly or by default — how to manage its currency’s relationship with other currencies. The choice is not just economic; it is a profound statement about what the state prioritises: price stability, export competitiveness, policy freedom, or international integration. History has produced three fundamental approaches, each with profound strengths and fatal weaknesses.

Regime 1: The Gold Standard — When Metal Was Money

Before the 20th century, most currencies were not promises backed by government credibility. They were certificates for a specific weight of metal — gold or silver. The gold standard in its classic form (dominant from approximately 1870 to 1914) worked like this: the British pound was defined as 7.32 grams of gold; the US dollar as 1.50 grams. The exchange rate between them was therefore fixed — not by government decree, but by mathematics: £1 = 4.86 dollars (7.32 ÷ 1.50). All major currencies were similarly fixed against gold, and therefore fixed against each other.

The genius of the gold standard: the automatic adjustment mechanism, described elegantly by philosopher David Hume in 1752 as the “price-specie flow” model. If Britain imports more than it exports, gold flows out to pay for the excess imports. The British money supply (gold-backed) contracts. Prices in Britain fall. British exports become cheaper. The trade deficit self-corrects. No human decision required; the mechanism was self-regulating.

This discipline was also the gold standard’s beauty and its tyranny. By tying monetary policy to a metal, it made it impossible for governments to print money to fund spending — a powerful constraint on fiscal excess. The period of the classical gold standard (1870–1914) was one of remarkable global trade expansion and monetary stability.

The fatal flaw: no flexibility in a crisis. When the Great Depression struck in 1929, countries needed to stimulate their depressed economies — cut interest rates, inject money, run deficits. The gold standard prevented all of this. To cut interest rates, you needed to let your currency weaken; a gold peg prevented currency weakness. To print money, you needed to suspend gold convertibility. Countries faced a terrible choice: stick to gold and watch the economy collapse, or leave gold and accept the stigma of monetary unreliability.

The countries that left the gold standard earliest — Britain in 1931, the United States in 1933 (domestically; Roosevelt confiscated private gold holdings by executive order) — recovered faster. The countries that clung longest to gold — France until 1936, the United States internationally until 1971 — suffered longer. Barry Eichengreen’s authoritative study Golden Fetters documents this with precision: the gold standard was not a source of stability during the Depression; it was the transmission mechanism by which Depression spread from the United States across the world.

Bretton Woods — the gold standard reincarnated: After World War II, the Allied powers convened at Bretton Woods (Chapter 8 of the Rise and Fall report covered this from the American-power perspective) to design a new monetary order. The system they created maintained the discipline of gold convertibility while allowing more flexibility: the dollar was pegged to gold at $35 per ounce, and all other currencies were pegged to the dollar. The US Federal Reserve committed to selling gold to any foreign central bank at $35 per ounce. Other countries committed to maintaining their currencies within 1% of their dollar peg.

This worked brilliantly during the postwar boom (1945–1965). Dollar-financed Marshall Plan reconstruction created European growth; American manufacturing dominance meant the US ran trade surpluses that provided dollars to the world; the system had genuine credibility.

Then came the Nixon Shock. As American spending on the Vietnam War and Lyndon Johnson’s Great Society social programmes expanded in the late 1960s, the US money supply grew faster than its gold reserves justified. Foreign central banks — particularly France under Charles de Gaulle, who was suspicious of American monetary discipline — began converting their dollar reserves to gold at the promised $35/ounce, watching US gold reserves drain toward suspicious lows.

On August 15, 1971, a Sunday night, President Richard Nixon appeared on national television and announced that the United States was immediately suspending the convertibility of the dollar into gold. No negotiations. No multilateral agreement. A unilateral declaration that ended the Bretton Woods system overnight.

The world woke up the next morning without a monetary anchor. The modern floating exchange rate era had begun — not by design, but by American necessity.

Regime 2: Fixed and Pegged Exchange Rates

After Bretton Woods collapsed, many countries chose to maintain some form of fixed exchange rate — pegging their currency to the dollar or, later, to a basket of currencies — rather than accepting the volatility of a floating rate.

How a peg works: The government declares that its currency will be maintained at, say, $1 = 3 local currency (the Thai baht in the mid-1990s, as we will see). The central bank commits to buying or selling its own currency whenever the market rate threatens to deviate from this declared level. If demand for foreign currency rises (the currency faces depreciation pressure), the central bank sells foreign exchange from its reserves and buys local currency, maintaining the peg. If the currency faces appreciation pressure, the central bank buys foreign exchange and sells local currency.

The peg provides certainty — businesses planning trade and investment know what exchange rate to expect. It imports the monetary credibility of the anchor currency. For a small country with weak institutional credibility, fixing your currency to the dollar or the deutschmark is a powerful signal of commitment to monetary discipline.

Why pegs are dangerous: they are promises. And promises made in financial markets can be tested by anyone with enough capital.

The Impossible Trinity — also known as the Mundell-Fleming Trilemma — is one of the most important ideas in all of international economics, and it explains precisely why pegs are so vulnerable.

The Trilemma states that any country faces three desirable policy goals: 1. A fixed exchange rate (stability, certainty for trade and investment) 2. Free capital flows (investment freedom, financial integration with the world) 3. Independent monetary policy (freedom to set interest rates for domestic economic conditions)

A country can have any two of these three. It cannot have all three simultaneously. This is not a political choice; it is a mathematical constraint.

Picture a triangle. At each corner is one of the three goals. You can occupy any side of the triangle — any two of the three — but never the corner where all three meet, because they are mutually incompatible.

Why you can’t have all three: if you fix the exchange rate and allow free capital flows, your interest rates must match wherever your peg currency’s interest rates are. If your domestic economy needs low rates (recession) but your peg currency’s central bank is raising rates, you must raise rates too — or capital will flee your currency (seeking higher rates in the peg currency), exhausting your reserves and breaking the peg.

This is exactly what happened to Britain in 1992 (Chapter 6) and Thailand in 1997 (Chapter 7). Both maintained fixed exchange rates and free capital flows — and therefore sacrificed independent monetary policy, with catastrophic consequences.

Hong Kong’s survival: Hong Kong has maintained a currency board — an extremely rigid form of peg, where the Hong Kong dollar is fixed to the US dollar at 7.8:1 since 1983 — for over 40 years through multiple crises, including the 1998 attack by Soros and other speculators. It has survived because Hong Kong maintains massive foreign exchange reserves (several times its monetary base), runs a fiscal surplus (no government need to print money), and accepts the Impossible Trinity’s constraint: Hong Kong has absolutely no independent monetary policy, following the US Fed in every rate decision. The discipline is extreme. The reward is institutional credibility.

Argentina’s disaster: Argentina’s 1:1 peso-dollar convertibility (1991–2001) is the most famous peg failure in history. The peg was adopted precisely because Argentina needed to import monetary credibility after hyperinflation destroyed the previous currency. It worked initially beautifully — ending hyperinflation and restoring growth. Then the US dollar strengthened in the late 1990s, dragging the peso (pegged to it) up with it, making Argentine exports devastatingly uncompetitive. A recession began. The government could not cut interest rates (peg constraint). Could not devalue (political commitment to the peg was total — banks held dollar-denominated deposits). Ran deficits financed by borrowing. By 2001, the debt was unpayable, the recession was severe, and the convertibility peg broke in a crisis that froze bank accounts, triggered riots, and caused the worst sovereign default in history at that time. The Argentine middle class watched their dollar savings converted back to pesos at a fraction of the value they had deposited.

Regime 3: Floating Exchange Rates

The pure float: the market determines the exchange rate. Supply and demand. No government commitment to any level. The exchange rate moves freely, like any other asset price.

The theoretical advantages are compelling: automatic adjustment — a trade deficit causes currency depreciation (imports become more expensive, exports more competitive), which naturally corrects the imbalance without requiring any government action. Monetary policy independence — the central bank can set interest rates for domestic conditions without any exchange rate constraint (you are not on the Impossible Trinity — you gave up the fixed rate). No reserve drain — you cannot “run out” of reserves defending a peg you don’t have.

The practical disadvantages are also real: volatility — without any anchor, currencies can swing sharply, creating uncertainty for businesses planning trade and investment. Inflationary spirals — a sharp depreciation raises import prices, which raises inflation, which the central bank responds to with rate hikes, which slow the economy, which reduces investor confidence, which causes further depreciation, which further raises inflation… the feedback loop can be vicious.

Almost no country in practice has a truly free float. Even the US and the European Union intervene occasionally. The realistic choice is between degrees of management:

The managed float (or “dirty float”): the central bank intervenes to smooth excessive volatility and prevent the exchange rate from moving too far from what it considers appropriate levels — but without committing to defend any specific level. This is India’s approach. The RBI regularly buys and sells dollars in the forex market, holds large reserves as ammunition, and sometimes signals its preferences through statements. But the rupee is not pegged; it has depreciated from ₹7/$ in 1971 to ₹85/$ today, reflecting the market’s long-term verdict on India’s inflation differential with the United States.

The managed float is in many ways the most intellectually honest approach: it acknowledges that exchange rates matter and that markets are not always right in the short term, while refusing the false promise of a level that fundamentals cannot sustain.

[Continued in Part 2 — Chapters 5 through 8] ## CHAPTER 5 — The Asian Export Miracle: How Cheap Currencies Built Industrial Empires

The Secret Weapon Hidden in Plain Sight

The great Asian economic miracles — Japan in the 1950s–70s, South Korea and Taiwan in the 1960s–80s, China from the 1990s onwards — have been variously attributed to Confucian work ethic, authoritarian developmental states, high savings rates, and export-led growth strategies. All of these factors were real. But hidden in plain sight, enabling all of them, was a single crucial variable that most popular accounts underemphasise or overlook entirely: a deliberately undervalued exchange rate.

A cheap currency is an implicit export subsidy. It makes every good you sell abroad cheaper in foreign-currency terms without requiring any change in your production costs. It is a generalized, automatic, sector-spanning competitive advantage. And the greatest industrial empires of the 20th century were built, partly, on this advantage — sustained for years and sometimes decades before market forces or political pressure forced an adjustment.

Japan: The 360-Yen Era (1949–1971)

When the Allied occupation authorities set Japan’s currency at 360 yen to the dollar in 1949 — a rate chosen partly for its simplicity (360 degrees in a circle) and partly to be conservative about Japan’s postwar productivity — it created the foundation for an extraordinary competitive advantage.

At 360 yen per dollar, a transistor radio that cost 3,600 yen to produce in Japan sold for just $10 in America. As Japanese productivity rapidly improved through the 1950s and 1960s — the cost fell to 2,000 yen, then 1,500 yen — the dollar price fell to $5.55, then $4.17. Japanese consumer electronics became irresistibly cheap. Sony’s first transistor radio, the TR-55 of 1955, captured American markets at prices that American manufacturers simply could not match. Toyota’s early cars, priced for the American mass market, sold on a price point that Detroit couldn’t touch.

Was this “currency manipulation” in the sense the US Treasury would use decades later with China? Not precisely — Japan maintained the peg through the Bretton Woods system, which all countries participated in. But the yen was arguably undervalued given Japan’s real productivity trajectory, and the peg’s longevity created a sustained export advantage that financed Japan’s industrial buildout.

The Plaza Accord (1985): When the G5 Moved a Currency by Decree

By the early 1980s, the US was running enormous trade deficits with Japan and West Germany. American manufacturing was being devastated by cheap imports. The US dollar had strengthened dramatically (perversely — Reagan’s high interest rates to fight inflation attracted capital, which pushed the dollar up, which made the trade deficit worse). American politicians were furious and the threat of protectionist legislation was growing.

The Reagan administration, under Treasury Secretary James Baker, engineered one of the most consequential and least-celebrated economic events of the 20th century: a secret weekend meeting at the Plaza Hotel in New York on September 22, 1985, with the finance ministers and central bank governors of the G5 (US, UK, France, Germany, Japan).

The Plaza Accord produced a joint agreement to intervene in currency markets — together, coordinated — to push the dollar down and the yen and deutschmark up. The signal was explicit and credible: five of the world’s most powerful governments were unanimous in their direction, and they had the reserves to back it.

The result was dramatic. The yen appreciated from 240 to 150 per dollar within two years — a 40% strengthening in 24 months, unmatched in the history of any major currency in peacetime. Japanese exports, which had been priced at 240 yen per dollar, suddenly needed 150 yen per dollar — 37% more expensive overnight in dollar terms. The comparative advantage the yen peg had provided for a generation evaporated.

The Bank of Japan’s response to the resulting economic shock was to dramatically cut interest rates — from 5% to 2.5% — to stimulate the domestic economy that could no longer rely on export growth. This flood of cheap money inflated the most spectacular asset bubble in history: Tokyo property values reached levels where the Imperial Palace grounds were theoretically worth more than the entire state of California. Stock market prices reached 60 times earnings at their 1989 peak.

When the bubble burst, beginning in 1990, the consequences were the Lost Decades: 30 years of sub-1% average growth, deflation, corporate balance sheet restructuring, and demographic stagnation. The Plaza Accord — a currency agreement between five governments in a New York hotel room on a September Saturday — triggered a chain of events whose economic consequences Japan is still navigating.

The lesson is blunt: exchange rates are geopolitics as much as economics. The decision about what a currency is worth can be made not by markets alone but by governments acting in concert — and the consequences can echo for decades.

South Korea: The Managed Won

South Korea’s industrial strategy under Park Chung-hee was more sophisticated than Japan’s fixed peg, and in many ways more instructive as a model. Rather than maintaining a rigidly fixed exchange rate that eventually required a traumatic Plaza-Accord-style adjustment, South Korea regularly adjusted its won peg — periodically devaluing to keep Korean exports competitive as wages rose, then adjusting again. The exchange rate was a policy tool deployed actively, not a fixed anchor defended religiously.

This approach gave Korean exporters the competitive pricing they needed at each stage of industrial development without creating the kind of massive misalignment that ultimately required the plaza shock to correct. Korea moved from textiles (labour-intensive, sensitive to wage cost) to steel and shipbuilding (capital-intensive, less wage-sensitive) to electronics and semiconductors (technology-intensive) — and at each stage, the won was managed to support the transition.

China: The Most Consequential Currency Decision of the Modern Era

In January 1994, China made a single currency decision that would restructure global manufacturing, hollow out factory towns in the American Midwest, create the most extraordinary export boom in history, and generate a decade of diplomatic tension between Washington and Beijing. China devalued the renminbi by 33% in a single move — from 5.8 to 8.7 per dollar — and then effectively pegged it at approximately that level for over a decade.

At 8.7 renminbi per dollar, Chinese goods were extraordinarily cheap. A factory worker in Shenzhen earned approximately $60 per month. At 8.7 renminbi per dollar, producing a pair of jeans in China cost a US importer perhaps $3 — making it profitable to ship manufacturing 10,000 miles across the Pacific and still land jeans in American retailers at prices domestic manufacturers couldn’t match.

The consequences were world-historic. China became the world’s manufacturer, producing an estimated 40% of global manufacturing output at its peak. US manufacturing employment fell by approximately 5 million jobs between 2000 and 2010 — the “China shock” that MIT economists David Autor, David Dorn, and Gordon Hanson documented with precision, showing that communities that faced the highest Chinese import competition suffered the most dramatic declines in employment and wages.

For China, the cheap renminbi strategy delivered exactly what it was designed to: massive foreign exchange earnings (which were reinvested in US Treasury bonds, funding the very American consumption that bought Chinese goods — a remarkable circular flow), enormous technology transfer as global companies established China operations, and the industrial knowledge accumulation that eventually allowed China to move up the value chain from toys and textiles to smartphones and electric vehicles.

The political price: years of US-China friction over currency “manipulation.” The US Treasury’s semi-annual currency manipulator report became a geopolitical weapon. China gradually allowed the renminbi to appreciate after 2005 — from 8.3 to approximately 6.3 by 2015 — under sustained American pressure. The era of the dramatically undervalued renminbi is largely over; China’s exchange rate today reflects market fundamentals more closely than it did in 2004.

Singapore: The Exchange Rate as Monetary Policy

Singapore’s exchange rate strategy deserves special mention because it is unique in the world: Singapore uses the exchange rate — rather than the interest rate — as its primary monetary policy tool.

Most countries use interest rates to fight inflation: raise rates, attractive capital flows in, currency strengthens, import prices fall, inflation cools. Singapore, as a tiny open economy where imports are an enormous share of consumption, realised that managing the Singapore dollar’s value was more powerful and more direct than managing interest rates. The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) manages a basket of trading-partner currencies and allows the Singapore dollar to move within an undisclosed band — appreciating gradually to offset inflation imported through the price of imports.

This unconventional system has worked extraordinarily well for 40 years: Singapore has combined sustained economic growth with consistently low inflation, managed through exchange rate policy rather than interest rate policy. It is one of the most successful and least-replicated monetary frameworks in the world.

CHAPTER 6 — Soros, the Bank of England, and the Art of the Currency Attack

The Setup: A Promise Britain Couldn’t Keep

September 1990. Britain joined the European Exchange Rate Mechanism — a system of semi-fixed exchange rates linking European currencies together in preparation for eventually replacing them all with a single euro. Britain entered at a rate of £1 = 2.95 deutschmarks — a rate that some economists immediately argued was too high for Britain’s actual economic fundamentals relative to Germany.

The choice of entry rate was political as much as economic. Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher had been dragged reluctantly into the ERM by Chancellor John Major and Foreign Secretary Geoffrey Howe. Major — as Chancellor implementing the entry — chose a level that expressed ambition rather than realism. The deutschmark was the symbol of German monetary virtue; pegging the pound close to it was a statement of intent.

By 1992, the problem had become acute. Britain was in recession — the early 1990s property crash (following the late 1980s credit boom) had left households overindebted and corporate balance sheets damaged. What the British economy needed was what every economy in recession needs: lower interest rates to stimulate borrowing, investment, and spending.

But the ERM prevented this. To maintain the pound at 2.95 deutschmarks, Britain had to match German interest rates — and Germany had its own problem: reunification with the former East Germany was inflationary, and the Bundesbank was raising rates to contain it. Britain, trapped by its exchange rate commitment, had to raise rates in a recession. The conflict between the external commitment and the domestic reality was tearing the economy apart.

The pound was at the bottom of its ERM band, which was supposed to be ±2.25% around the central rate. The Bank of England was defending it — buying pounds, selling foreign currency — but the market could see the arithmetic.

Enter George Soros

George Soros is a Hungarian-American investor and philosopher who made his billions through Quantum Fund, the hedge fund he managed with his chief investment officer Stan Druckenmiller. By 1992, Soros was already legendary in financial circles for a distinctive investment philosophy: find moments where the prevailing consensus in financial markets is wrong, construct a large asymmetric bet, and hold it with conviction.

In the summer of 1992, Druckenmiller identified Britain’s pound-ERM situation as exactly the kind of unsustainable position that Soros’s philosophy was built to exploit. The argument was simple: Britain’s economic fundamentals — high inflation relative to Germany, a large current account deficit, a recession-bound economy — made the pound’s ERM rate fundamentally untenable. The Bank of England would eventually be forced to leave the ERM and devalue. The only question was when.

The trade was elegant: short the pound. Borrow pounds from banks (paying sterling interest rates), immediately sell them for deutschmarks (locking in the rate). If the pound fell — when the pound fell — buy pounds back cheaply to repay the borrowings, and pocket the difference in deutschmarks.

The risk: if the Bank of England succeeded in defending the peg, the pound stayed at 2.95 DM and the trade lost money (the cost of borrowing pounds minus any interest earned). The maximum loss was calculable and bounded.

The potential gain: if the pound crashed — devalued by 15, 20, or 30% — the profit on a large enough position would be enormous.

Druckenmiller ran the numbers and proposed the trade to Soros. Soros’s response was characteristic: “if the trade is as good as you say, why aren’t we doing it bigger?” Quantum Fund committed $10 billion to shorting the pound — leveraged positions in an astonishing size.

Black Wednesday: September 16, 1992

Financial historians record what followed in detail that reads like a battlefield dispatch.

6 AM: The pound is at the bottom of its ERM band. The Bank of England is buying pounds to support it.

7 AM: The Bank of England announces that it will raise interest rates from 10% to 12% — a dramatic move, intended to attract capital flows into sterling by making pound-denominated deposits more attractive.

8 AM: The markets shrug. The pound doesn’t recover. The fundamental problem — Britain cannot sustain these rates in a recession — is more clearly known to everyone than the government acknowledges.

10:30 AM: A second rate rise announced. Rates from 12% to 15%. The highest peacetime interest rate in British history, raised to defend a currency peg.

The measure of how desperate the situation had become: 15% interest rates during a housing market crash and recession, with hundreds of thousands of British homeowners on variable-rate mortgages. Each point of rate increase was devastating to homeowners already stretched thin.

Noon: The pound is still falling. The Bank of England is spending foreign exchange reserves at a rate that cannot be sustained. Chancellor Norman Lamont is on the phone with his German counterpart, begging the Bundesbank to cut German rates — which would reduce the pressure on Britain to raise its own. The Bundesbank refuses.

2 PM: Lamont calls an emergency press conference. He is accompanied by John Major’s press secretary. His face says everything: the game is over.

7 PM: Britain announces it is suspending its membership in the ERM. The pound will float. The 15% rate rise is immediately reversed.

The pound crashes. From 2.95 deutschmarks — the level Britain had spent the day’s reserves defending — it falls to approximately 2.50 over the following weeks. A 15% devaluation.

George Soros’s profit: $1 billion in a single day — the most famous currency trade in history. “The man who broke the Bank of England.”

The Deeper Lesson

Soros did not break anything that wasn’t already broken. This is the crucial insight, and it is easily missed in the drama of the story.

Britain’s pound peg was not sustainable given its economic fundamentals. At some point — next month, next year — Britain would have been forced to leave the ERM. The recession was too deep, the interest rate commitment too painful, the unemployment too high for any democratic government to sustain indefinitely.

Soros didn’t create this unsustainability. He recognised it, bet on it, and made money when it was confirmed. His $10 billion position was the match that lit a fire that was already laid. A fire it would take a while to lay for, but once laid, it would inevitably be lit.

This is the currency attack’s true nature — and it directly explains the 1997 Asian crisis: speculators do not create currency crises. They reveal and accelerate crises that bad policies have already created. The Thai baht, the Malaysian ringgit, the Indonesian rupiah — all fell in 1997 not because Soros attacked healthy currencies but because they were pegs stretched beyond their fundamentals, waiting for the market to notice the cracks.

CHAPTER 7 — The 1997 Asian Currency Crisis: When Pegs Became Death Traps

The Perfect Setup for Disaster

In the early 1990s, Thailand, Indonesia, South Korea, Malaysia, and the Philippines were among the world’s most celebrated economic successes. GDP growth of 7–10% annually, rising middle classes, gleaming new cities, infrastructure being built at remarkable speed. The World Bank called it the “East Asian Miracle” in a 1993 report. Foreign investors were enthusiastic: high growth rates, high interest rates, stable currencies (all were pegged to the dollar). Capital poured in.

Beneath the celebration, the vulnerabilities were accumulating:

Overvalued pegs: As countries maintained fixed exchange rates through the 1990s while their domestic inflation ran somewhat higher than the US, their currencies became gradually overvalued in real terms. Exports became gradually less competitive. Current account deficits widened.

The double mismatch: Banks and corporations were borrowing in US dollars (cheap, low-interest) and lending in local currencies at higher rates (profitable). This “currency mismatch” — your debts are in a foreign currency but your revenues are in local currency — is benign as long as the exchange rate holds. It is catastrophic when the peg breaks.

Simultaneously, they were borrowing short-term (international banks rolled over short-term credit facilities) to finance long-term investments (office buildings, infrastructure, factories). This “maturity mismatch” meant that if the short-term lenders wanted their money back suddenly, there was no liquid asset to sell.

Interconnected banking systems: The capital inflows funded an explosion of bank lending. Asset prices — property especially — rose dramatically. Banks lent against rising property values; rising property values made more lending seem prudent; the cycle inflated a credit boom with no productive foundation.

Thailand: The First Domino (July 1997)

The Thai baht had been pegged at approximately 25 baht per dollar since the 1980s. By 1996, Thailand’s current account deficit had reached 8% of GDP — clearly unsustainable. Foreign investors began pulling money out. The Bank of Thailand defended the peg by selling dollar reserves.

By early 1997, the Bank of Thailand had spent most of its visible reserves — but the full picture was worse: it had committed most of its remaining reserves through forward contracts to currency traders, obligations that wouldn’t appear on the balance sheet but would come due when the peg broke. When international investors and hedge funds learned (through careful analysis of forward market positions and reserve data) that Thailand’s reserves were nearly exhausted, the attack became overwhelming.

On July 2, 1997, Thailand abandoned the peg. The baht fell from 25 to 56 per dollar within weeks — a 56% depreciation.

Now apply what this means to a Thai company — not an abstraction but a business employing hundreds of people, run by a family that had invested everything:

The company had borrowed 10millionatUSratesin1995.Atthepeggedrateof25baht/, this was 250 million baht in debt — manageable for a factory generating 40 million baht per year in revenue.

After the devaluation, $10 million = 560 million baht. The debt just grew 124% in local currency terms, overnight. The company’s revenue didn’t change — still 40 million baht per year. The debt service just became impossible. The company defaults. Its bank takes the hit. The bank’s capital is wiped out. The bank stops lending. The economy contracts.

Multiply this by thousands of companies, hundreds of banks, and you have Thailand 1997–1998: GDP fell 10.5% in one year.

The Contagion: How Crisis Spreads

One of the most important features of the 1997 crisis — and one of the most intellectually important things about currency crises generally — is that the contagion was not irrational. When Thailand fell, global investors didn’t blindly panic and sell all Asian currencies. They thought analytically: which other countries look like Thailand?

The diagnosis was a checklist: large current account deficit. Currency peg to the dollar. Heavy short-term foreign borrowing. Rapid credit expansion. Asset price inflation. Dollar-denominated corporate debt.

Indonesia: check. South Korea: check. Malaysia: largely check. Philippines: partial.

The investors who had studied the Thai situation carefully were now two steps ahead. They knew the template. They attacked the next most vulnerable currency before the crisis arrived there organically. Indonesia’s rupiah collapsed. South Korea’s won collapsed (South Korea, the world’s eleventh-largest economy, needed an IMF bailout — a moment of national humiliation that has never been forgotten). By year-end 1997, the crisis had consumed five economies and wiped out decades of accumulated development.

The IMF Response: Orthodoxy and Its Discontents

The International Monetary Fund’s crisis response packages — to Thailand, Indonesia, and South Korea — required their recipients to accept conditions: raise interest rates (to defend the currency and attract capital), cut government spending (fiscal austerity), and close insolvent banks.

The intellectual case for each prescription was defensible. High interest rates would make the currency more attractive. Fiscal discipline would reduce the government’s borrowing needs. Closing insolvent banks would prevent zombie lending.

The practical result, particularly in Indonesia, was economic catastrophe verging on social breakdown. GDP fell 13.5% in 1998. Unemployment tripled. The IMF’s demand that Indonesia close 16 banks simultaneously triggered a bank run as depositors couldn’t tell which banks were safe — the announcement designed to stop the crisis deepened it. Nobel Prize-winning economist Joseph Stiglitz — then Chief Economist at the World Bank — publicly condemned the IMF’s prescriptions as precisely the wrong medicine: raising interest rates during a collapsing demand environment deepened the recession that was causing the currency crisis.

The counterargument: South Korea followed the IMF programme more faithfully and recovered faster than Indonesia. The discipline, while painful, prevented a deeper fiscal crisis.

The truth is that neither the “IMF was right” nor “IMF was wrong” position fully captures the evidence. The orthodox prescriptions were founded on models designed for different kinds of crisis (government fiscal excess, like Latin America in the 1980s) and applied to a different kind of crisis (private-sector balance sheet crisis from currency mismatch). The fit was imperfect, and people suffered for it.

Malaysia: The Road Not Taken

In September 1998, as Indonesia and Thailand were following IMF prescriptions with painful results, Malaysia’s Prime Minister Mahathir Mohamad made a radical unilateral decision: capital controls.

Mahathir fixed the ringgit at 3.8 per dollar — ending the currency’s painful slide. He banned offshore trading of the ringgit (making it impossible for foreign investors to short it). He imposed a one-year moratorium on capital outflows — foreign investors who wanted to take their money out of Malaysia had to wait a year.

Every Western economist and the IMF predicted disaster. Capital would never return to a country that had imprisoned investors’ money. The controls would create black markets, currency distortions, and long-term investment deterrence.

The prediction was wrong. Malaysia recovered faster than Thailand. Foreign investment returned within two years, faster than the pundits had predicted. The controls gave Malaysia the time and space to restructure its banking system and corporate balance sheets without the pressure of ongoing currency crisis. By controlling the valve in the crisis tube, Malaysia prevented the worst-case hyper-depreciation while accepting some long-term costs to its reputation for capital openness.

Mahathir’s success was partial, conditional, and has generated decades of academic debate. Capital controls that are credibly temporary, implemented by a government with genuine policy capacity, and aimed at buying time for fundamental restructuring can work. Capital controls as a permanent substitute for structural adjustment do not work. The Malaysia case illustrates both the limitations of IMF orthodoxy and the conditions under which heterodox policies can succeed.

CHAPTER 8 — Currency Wars: When Nations Compete by Weakening Their Money

Beggar Thy Neighbour

The phrase “beggar-thy-neighbour” entered economic discourse in the 1930s and has never left. It describes a policy logic that is individually rational and collectively ruinous: if I weaken my currency, my exports become cheaper, my factories fill with orders, my workers stay employed. The cost is borne by my trading partners, whose exports become less competitive.

The problem: everyone’s trading partners have the same government with the same political pressures. If I devalue, they devalue. If they devalue, I devalue further. The result is a devaluation arms race in which everyone’s relative competitive position ends up roughly where it began — but everyone has inflated their money supply and destabilised their financial system in the process. The beggar eventually beggars the self.

The 1930s Currency War: How Competitive Devaluation Deepened the Depression

The 1930s is the definitive case study. After the gold standard constrained monetary adjustment and turned a serious recession into the Great Depression, countries faced an impossible situation: their economies were collapsing, but the gold peg prevented the monetary stimulus that might have stopped the collapse.

Britain left gold in September 1931 — not by design but by Market necessity (reserves ran out). The immediate result was a 25% sterling devaluation that made British exports cheaper and boosted British industry. The countries left on gold — whose currencies had just appreciated against sterling — saw their exports become less competitive. They resented it.

The United States left gold domestically in 1933 under Roosevelt — confiscating citizens’ gold (at $20.67/ounce) and then revaluing it at $35/ounce, a 70% devaluation against gold and a substantial depreciation against gold-linked currencies. American exports became cheaper. European competitors resented it.

France held on to gold until 1936, maintaining what economists bitterly called the “Gold Bloc” — a coalition of countries that chose gold standard discipline over economic recovery. French unemployment and economic contraction worsened relative to countries that had devalued. When France finally left gold in 1936, it was after years of unnecessary suffering.

The net result of three years of competitive devaluation: global trade collapsed by 65% between 1929 and 1932 — one of the sharpest contractions in the history of international commerce. The devaluations did not, in aggregate, help anyone. They merely shifted the pain from one country to another, round by round, while the volume of trade they were competing over contracted until there was barely enough to fight over.

The Modern Currency War (2010–2015)

Brazilian Finance Minister Guido Mantega used the phrase “currency war” in September 2010 to describe what he saw in global markets: every major central bank in the developed world was devaluing. The US Federal Reserve was engaged in “quantitative easing” — creating new dollars to buy bonds, inflating the money supply and weakening the dollar. The Bank of Japan was doing similar. The Bank of England.

The transmission mechanism to emerging markets worked like this: when the Fed prints dollars and buys bonds, US interest rates fall. The interest rate differential between the US and high-yielding emerging markets like Brazil widens. Carry trade capital floods into Brazil, buying reals to invest in Brazilian bonds. The real strengthens — sharply, from 2.7 per dollar in 2008 to 1.55 per dollar in 2011. Brazilian exports become 40% more expensive relative to competitors who haven’t had carry-trade appreciation. Brazilian manufacturing suffers.

Mantega’s accusation had merit: developed-country central banks, in pursuing their domestic recovery mandates, were creating exchange rate dislocations that damaged emerging market exports. But the developed-country central banks were not primarily trying to weaken their currencies to gain export advantage; they were trying to prevent deflation and stimulate their own recoveries. The currency effects were a side consequence, not the primary objective.

The distinction matters but doesn’t change the real-world impact for a Brazilian shoe factory pricing against Chinese competitors.

China’s Renminbi Strategy: The $3 Trillion Question

China maintained a pegged or tightly managed renminbi from 1994 through approximately 2015, resisting appreciation pressure for a decade during which its current account surplus reached approximately 10% of GDP (2007). At that surplus level, almost every economist — including economists at the IMF and World Bank — argued that the renminbi was dramatically undervalued.

The US position: the undervalued renminbi was an unfair trade subsidy, artificially pricing Chinese goods below their true cost and exporting unemployment to American manufacturing workers. This view had bipartisan support in the US Congress and was reflected in the Treasury’s semi-annual currency report.

China’s counter-position: the renminbi required managed appreciation to prevent currency volatility that would disrupt the enormous export economy employing hundreds of millions. Rapid appreciation would cause factory closures, unemployment, and social instability. China was appreciating the currency — just slowly and on its own schedule, not America’s.

The geopolitical dimension was equally important: China used its enormous dollar reserves — accumulated through years of current account surpluses and capital controls that prevented renminbi outflows — as a form of geopolitical leverage. As the largest foreign holder of US Treasury bonds (approximately $1.1 trillion at peak), China had the theoretical ability to destabilise US bond markets by selling — the “nuclear option” that Chinese officials occasionally alluded to when US-China tensions rose.

The digital renminbi (e-CNY): China’s long-term currency strategy includes the development of a Central Bank Digital Currency — the e-CNY or digital yuan — that could allow China to conduct international trade in renminbi through a digital railroad that bypasses the SWIFT interbank messaging system on which dollar-based international transactions depend. If China can convince Belt and Road Initiative partner countries to accept payment in digital renminbi, it reduces their need for dollars, incrementally eroding the dollar’s reserve currency status. This is one dimension of the US-China rivalry that is economic, geopolitical, and technological simultaneously.

The Dollar’s Exorbitant Privilege — and the Challenge to It

French Finance Minister Valéry Giscard d’Estaing coined “exorbitant privilege” in the 1960s to describe America’s unique advantage: because the dollar is the world’s reserve currency, the United States can borrow in its own currency at artificially low rates, can run trade deficits indefinitely (because the world needs dollars), and can impose financial sanctions that cripple other countries’ access to the global financial system because the global financial system runs on dollars.

The privilege is real and large. Estimates suggest the US saves approximately $100 billion per year in interest costs from the reserve currency premium — effectively a tax on every country that holds dollars. The ability to impose financial sanctions (on Russia in 2022, on Iran, on Venezuela) is the most powerful non-military instrument of American foreign policy. No country can replicate it without reserve currency status.

The challenge to dollar dominance has several dimensions:

De-dollarization in bilateral trade: Russia and China now conduct a majority of their bilateral trade in non-dollar currencies. India and Russia have traded oil in rupees. Saudi Arabia has accepted renminbi for some oil sales to China. These are individually small; cumulatively, they represent a slow attrition of dollar trade invoicing.

BRICS alternatives: The BRICS grouping (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa, and now six additional members) has discussed creating a common settlement currency for intra-BRICS trade. Progress has been slow — India and China have different interests, India does not want to surrender to renminbi dominance, and creating a genuinely new reserve currency is vastly harder than discussing one.

The dollar’s structural support: Despite all challenges, dollar alternatives face overwhelming structural obstacles. No other currency has the depth of liquid financial markets (US Treasury market is the world’s largest and most liquid bond market), the institutional credibility, or the network effects (everyone holds dollars because everyone else holds dollars) required for reserve currency status. The euro lacks a fiscal union. The renminbi lacks capital account convertibility and political trust. Bitcoin and cryptocurrencies lack stability.

The dollar’s privilege may erode slowly over decades — as the pound’s eroded between 1900 and 1956 — but there is no credible near-term replacement. The dollar’s era is not ending; it is beginning a very long decline.

The Ultimate Exchange Rate Failure

Everything in this report so far has been about exchange rates within the recognisable range of human economic experience: currencies that weaken 15% or 20% or even 50%, recoveries and crises that are painful but survivable. This chapter examines the extreme end of the spectrum — when a currency doesn’t weaken but ceases to function as money at all. When the exchange rate moves not in percentages but in multiples of millions.

Hyperinflation is not simply “very high inflation.” It is a qualitatively different phenomenon: a complete collapse of trust in a currency as a store of value, a medium of exchange, and a unit of account. When hyperinflation arrives, the currency — the basic technology that enables economic cooperation in complex societies — fails. What replaces it is barter, foreign currency, and the primitive economics of survival.

Weimar Germany (1921–1923): The Original Horror

The German mark’s collapse between 1921 and 1923 is the foundational hyperinflation story — the case that every subsequent instance is measured against, the event that shaped Germany’s pathological commitment to monetary stability for a century afterward, and the indirect precursor to the political environment that brought Hitler to power.

The cause was fiscal: Germany in 1921 faced crushing World War I reparations payments under the Treaty of Versailles — approximately 132 billion gold marks, an amount so large that economists then and now have debated whether Germany could ever actually have paid it. When France and Belgium occupied the Ruhr industrial region in January 1923 to extract reparations payments in kind, the German government encouraged workers to strike — passive resistance against the occupiers. The government financed the strikers by printing money.

The result: the exchange rate against the dollar went from 4.2 marks per dollar in 1914 to 160 marks per dollar in January 1923. By July 1923: 353,000 marks per dollar. By September: 98.8 million marks per dollar. By November 1923: 4.2 trillion marks per dollar — exactly one trillion times the 1914 rate.

The human texture of hyperinflation: workers demanded — and received — wages paid twice daily, because the value of morning wages would halve by afternoon. Restaurants changed their prices while customers were still eating. A wheelbarrow of cash was needed to buy a loaf of bread — and then the wheelbarrow was worth more than the cash in it. Savings accumulated over lifetimes evaporated. A person who had been frugal and responsible their entire life, who had saved in marks, found their savings worth nothing — while those who had borrowed in marks found their debts worth nothing. The moral economy of prudence and thrift was inverted.

The deeper consequence: Germany’s middle class — the doctors, professors, civil servants, shopkeepers who had saved in government bonds and bank deposits — was financially destroyed. This class had been the social backbone of German democracy. Its destruction radicalized a generation. The hyperinflation of 1923 is not the only cause of Hitler’s rise; but the economic devastation it caused to the very people who would ordinarily have anchored a functioning democracy created the social conditions without which Nazism’s appeal would have been far more limited.

This is the exchange rate’s ultimate lesson: a currency collapse is not just an economic event. It is a civilizational trauma that destroys social trust, corrupts the moral economy, and leaves political space for demagogues.

Zimbabwe (2007–2009): The 100 Trillion Dollar Note

Robert Mugabe’s Zimbabwe inherited one of Africa’s strongest economies in 1980 — productive commercial farmland, a diversified manufacturing base, decent infrastructure. By 2007, the Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe was issuing 100 trillion dollar notes that could not buy a bus ticket.

The path of destruction was the exchange rate story of governmental failure in compressed time. Mugabe’s government launched a land reform programme in 2000 that dispossessed commercial farmers (predominantly white) in a chaotic, violent process that destroyed the agricultural base. Lacking tax revenues from a collapsing economy, the government printed money to pay its bills — including the salaries of soldiers fighting in the Democratic Republic of Congo. The money supply expanded faster than any economist had previously documented.

Official inflation statistics were eventually abandoned — the statisticians ran out of meaningful ways to measure prices doubling every few hours. Independent economists estimated peak monthly inflation of 89.7 septillion percent in November 2008 (that is 89 followed by 21 zeros). The exchange rate — once a meaningful number — became a joke updated by the hour.

The human consequences: a nurse’s monthly salary could not buy an egg. Teachers and doctors emigrated by the thousands. Hospital wards ran out of basic supplies because no supplier would accept payment in a currency that would be worthless before the cheque cleared. The informal economy — barter and foreign currency — became the real economy. In 2009, Zimbabwe abandoned its own currency entirely and adopted the US dollar, the South African rand, and a basket of foreign currencies. The Zimbabwean dollar ceased to exist.

A currency is ultimately social trust made material. When that trust is destroyed — as Zimbabwe’s government destroyed it through systemic misgovernance — the currency becomes worthless paper. Restoring it requires rebuilding not just monetary institutions but the underlying institutional trust that makes a currency believable.

Venezuela (2016–Present): Socialism, Oil, and Currency Collapse

Venezuela is the 21st century’s most complete economic collapse — a country with the world’s largest proven oil reserves, which used those revenues to build one of Latin America’s most comprehensive social programmes under Hugo Chávez, and which then imploded when the oil revenues fell and the government chose to print money rather than reform.

The exchange rate story is relatively simple. As long as oil was above $80 per barrel, the revolutionary bolivar could be pegged at an official rate that bore some relationship to economic reality. Under the surface, distortions accumulated: price controls created shortages (the Diocletian effect, Chapter 2); nationalised companies were mismanaged; private sector investment collapsed as property rights became insecure; the oil company PDVSA was staffed with political loyalists rather than petroleum engineers.

When oil prices fell sharply in 2014–2015 and Chávez’s successor Nicolás Maduro refused structural adjustment, the government printed bolivars to maintain public spending. Inflation in 2018 reached an estimated 1,000,000% (one million percent). The bolivar was redesigned, with six zeros removed, creating the “sovereign bolivar” — which then hyperinflated again, requiring another six zeros removed to create the “digital bolivar” in 2021. The country experienced the progressive denominations of every historical hyperinflation, replaying the script with grim fidelity.

The human cost: approximately 7.7 million Venezuelans — over 20% of the population — emigrated between 2015 and 2023, the largest peacetime refugee crisis in Latin American history. Doctors who had earned middle-class salaries emigrated to Peru or Colombia to work in restaurants — because restaurant wages in foreign currency exceeded their Venezuelan salaries at any realistic exchange rate.

Turkey (2021–2023): When Presidents Overrule Economics

Turkey’s lira crisis of 2021–2023 is the most instructive contemporary case study in how political interference in monetary policy destroys currency value — because it is a middle-income country (not a failed state), with sophisticated financial markets, in which the mechanism of destruction is unusually clear and documented.

Recep Tayyip Erdoğan holds an unorthodox view of inflation: he believes that high interest rates cause inflation rather than cure it. This view is inconsistent with all mainstream economic theory and empirical evidence, but Erdoğan is the President of Turkey and he controls the appointments of central bank governors.

In 2021, with Turkish inflation rising to 20%+, the orthodox response — which economists of every ideological stripe endorsed — was to raise interest rates. Erdoğan fired three central bank governors in two years for the crime of raising rates. He appointed governors who cut rates as inflation soared.

The market’s response was immediate and unforgiving: if the central bank will not raise rates to fight inflation, then real interest rates (interest rate minus inflation) go deeply negative. Real negative rates are a powerful incentive to sell the currency — why hold an asset in a currency losing value faster than it pays interest? Foreign investors sold lira assets; domestic Turks converted savings to dollars and gold; the lira collapsed.

From approximately 8 lira per dollar in early 2021 to 19 lira per dollar by the end of 2021 — a 58% depreciation in one year. By late 2023, the lira exceeded 30 per dollar — a total loss of over 70% against the dollar in two years. Turkish inflation peaked at approximately 85% in late 2022.

The lesson is unavoidable and uncomfortable in an era of political pressure on central banks: central bank independence is not an academic nicety. It is the institutional mechanism that makes a currency credible. When it is compromised — when the person setting interest rates is accountable to the head of government rather than to the inflation target — the currency loses credibility and the damage is borne by ordinary citizens through rising prices, collapsing savings, and economic contraction.

India, which has faced episodic political pressure on the RBI but has maintained reasonable monetary institutional independence, is a counterexample of some importance here.

CHAPTER 10 — Central Banks: The Guardians of Currency Value

The Most Powerful Economic Institutions in the World

Central banks are among the least understood and most consequential institutions in modern economic life. They control the price of money (interest rates), manage the supply of money (monetary policy), act as lender of last resort to the banking system (financial stability), and — centrally for this report — manage the exchange rate (foreign exchange policy).

They operate independently of elected governments in most countries — shielded from day-to-day political pressure so that long-term price stability can be prioritised over short-term political gain. This independence is not undemocratic — central bank mandates are set by Parliament, governors are appointed by governments, and Parliament can change the mandate. But within the mandate, the central bank operates without requiring ministerial approval. This distinction — between mandate-setting (democratic) and policy-execution (independent) — is the institutional design that makes monetary credibility possible.

The Fed Effect: How One Institution Shapes the World

The US Federal Reserve’s monetary policy decisions ripple through every currency on Earth — and most of the world’s people have no vote in those decisions and no representation in Room around the FOMC (Federal Open Market Committee) table when they are made.

The mechanism: when the Fed raises interest rates, US Treasury bonds pay more interest. Global investors — sovereign wealth funds, pension funds, insurance companies managing trillions in assets — rebalance toward US Treasuries. They sell emerging market assets (Indian stocks, Brazilian bonds, Indonesian property) and buy US assets. To do so, they convert rupees, reals, and rupiah into dollars. Emerging market currencies weaken. The dollar strengthens.

For an emerging market country with dollar-denominated debt, the double blow is severe: not only does the local currency weaken (making imports more expensive and inflation worse), but the debt itself becomes more expensive to service (more local currency needed to buy the same number of dollars). Countries that borrowed heavily in dollars during the cheap-money era of 2010–2021 found themselves in exactly this trap during the 2022–2023 Fed tightening cycle.

Sri Lanka — which ran out of foreign exchange entirely in 2022, unable to pay for food and fuel imports — is the dramatic instance of this mechanism at work. The combination of COVID’s impact on tourism revenue (its largest foreign currency earner), high dollar debt, and the Fed tightening cycle that reduced the capital flows that had previously financed Sri Lanka’s current account deficit — all converged to produce a genuine foreign exchange crisis that caused food and medicine shortages and the resignation of the President amid popular protests.

Pakistan faced similar pressures in 2022–2023. Zambia defaulted. Ghana went to the IMF. Ghana’s Kwame Nkrumah once said that Africa needed the US to keep its domestic affairs in order; today, it is the Federal Reserve’s interest rate decisions that determine the financial breathing room of dozens of developing nations.

India’s RBI: An Institutional Achievement

The Reserve Bank of India was established in 1935 under British colonial rule and nationalized in 1949. It is one of the oldest central banks in the developing world, with sophisticated institutional traditions, high-quality research, and — crucially — genuine, if imperfect, independence from political pressure.

The RBI operates a formal inflation targeting framework: its primary mandate is to maintain Consumer Price Inflation in India within a target range of 4% ±2%. The Monetary Policy Committee that sets interest rates has external members as well as RBI officials, reducing the risk of groupthink and providing mechanisms for transparent deliberation.

The RBI’s approach to exchange rate management reflects the managed float described in Chapter 4: it does not target a specific rupee level, but it intervenes when the rupee’s movement appears disorderly or significantly misaligned from fundamentals. It has built India’s foreign exchange reserves from near-zero in 1991 to over $620 billion by 2024 — equivalent to approximately 10 months of import cover, providing significant insurance against crisis.

Its one persistent limitation: political messages from the Finance Ministry about exchange rate levels occasionally complicate the RBI’s communication strategy — not structurally different from what central banks face in most democracies, but a reminder that institutional independence is maintained through constant vigilance rather than constitutionally guaranteed.

Foreign Exchange Reserves: Why Countries Hoard Dollars

After the 1997 Asian crisis — in which countries’ inability to defend currency pegs led to catastrophic depreciations — emerging market governments collectively decided to buy insurance. The insurance policy: accumulate large stockpiles of foreign currencies (primarily dollars), which can be used to buy your own currency in the forex market if it faces attack.

This reserve buildup has been extraordinary in scale. China went from approximately $150 billion in reserves in 1997 to over $3.2 trillion by 2024 — partly a currency management tool, partly geopolitical savings. India went from effectively zero (necessitating the gold pledge of 1991) to over $620 billion. South Korea, Japan, Singapore, and others similarly built enormous reserve cushions.

The reserve provides genuine protection: India can withstand capital outflow shocks that would have caused crisis in 1991, because the RBI can sell dollars to support the rupee, buying time for the external shock to pass or the underlying situation to adjust.

The cost: reserves earn low returns (they are invested primarily in US Treasury notes and other liquid safe assets, which yield 4–5% at best) while the country’s domestic investment needs might earn 8–12%. The opportunity cost of holding $620 billion in low-yield US assets rather than deploying it in Indian infrastructure or education is real — though offset by the insurance value of crisis prevention.

CHAPTER 11 — The Rupee’s Story: India’s Exchange Rate Journey

The Colonial Rupee: Money as an Extraction Tool

India’s rupee has a history that predates Britain’s pound sterling in its current form. The silver rupee was a sophisticated monetary instrument of the Mughal Empire, widely used in trade from the Ottoman Empire to Southeast Asia. Its purchasing power was real and its geographic reach was substantial.

Britain’s colonization of India transformed the rupee from India’s own monetary instrument into a tool of colonial extraction. The colonial monetary system was designed to serve British interests: the exchange rate between the rupee and the pound was set to make Indian exports competitively priced (encouraging exports of cotton, indigo, and opium that provided raw materials for British industry), while making British manufactured goods competitive in India (discouraging Indian industrialization). The monetary system was one of the invisible mechanisms of the “drain of wealth” — the systematic transfer of economic value from India to Britain — alongside more visible mechanisms of direct tax revenue and resource extraction.

When the East India Company’s debts to shareholders were eventually transferred to the British Crown, Indian taxpayers funded the transition. The colonial railways — a genuine piece of infrastructure — were financed partly by guaranteed returns to British investors, payable in pounds by the Indian government, whether the railways were profitable or not. Every rupee of gold and silver flows went to London. The monetary system was structured for extraction.

Post-Independence: The Overvalued Rupee and the License Raj Connection

At Independence in 1947, India chose a fixed exchange rate — ₹4.76 to £1 (the pound sterling link was maintained from the colonial period). This rate was appreciated — overvalued given India’s actual economic fundamentals, which included significantly higher inflation than Britain and lower productivity.

An overvalued rupee had a specific political-economic logic in the Nehruvian development framework: it made imports affordable for the capital-goods industries that the licence raj was prioritising. A factory that needed German machine tools or British turbines benefited from an overvalued rupee that made those imports cheap. Conversely, exporters — textile mills, handicraft producers, agricultural producers — faced a consistent competitive disadvantage. Their products were priced out of global markets by a currency that overvalued India’s relative productiviy.

This was a choice — as all exchange rate regimes are choices — with distinct winners and losers: capital-goods importers won, exporters lost. The License Raj sustained overvalued-rupee imports by rationing who could access the cheap imports through import licenses, creating a system where the exchange rate policy and the import-control policy were mutually reinforcing.

The 1966 Devaluation: Indira’s Bitter Pill

India’s foreign exchange crisis of the mid-1960s — caused by two wars (China 1962, Pakistan 1965), droughts, and rising import costs — forced the first major exchange rate adjustment. In June 1966, Prime Minister Indira Gandhi devalued the rupee by 36.5% — from ₹4.76 per dollar to ₹7.50 per dollar — under pressure from the US government and the IMF, who made economic assistance conditional on the devaluation.

The political backlash was ferocious. Congress party colleagues accused Gandhi of capitulating to foreign pressure. The devaluation was portrayed as a national humiliation — India’s monetary sovereignty surrendered to Washington. The expected economic benefits — export boost, foreign exchange stability — were slow to materialise, partly because the 1966 monsoon failed and a severe drought dominated the economic picture.

The devaluation trauma of 1966 created a deep political sensitivity around exchange rate policy that persisted for decades. Indian politicians became allergic to the word “devaluation” — it had the political valence of a four-letter word. This sensitivity partly explains why the chronic overvaluation of the rupee continued for so long, imposing costs on India’s export competitiveness in the name of avoiding another humiliation. Structural overvaluation is not politically visible the way a discrete devaluation is — which makes it politically survivable even when economically damaging.

1991: The Gold Airlift and the Exchange Rate Liberation

The 1991 crisis — documented in Chapter 5 of the India report — was, from the exchange rate perspective, the moment the chickens of chronic overvaluation finally came home to roost. India’s foreign exchange reserves fell to $1.1 billion — barely two weeks of import cover. The rupee was unsustainable.

The devaluation of 1991 was executed in two tranches to soften the political impact: a 9% devaluation on July 1, followed by a further 11% on July 3 — a cumulative 22% adjustment. The political language was careful: Indian officials referred to it as an “exchange rate adjustment” rather than a “devaluation,” but this was linguistic self-preservation, not economic accuracy.

The economic consequences were precisely what the 1966 devaluation had failed to deliver, partly because the exchange rate adjustment was part of a comprehensive reform package under Manmohan Singh. A more competitive rupee made India’s exports competitively priced for the first time in decades. Foreign investment, attracted by liberalisation and a properly priced currency, began to arrive. The current account deficit narrowed. The crisis resolved.

The rupee’s subsequent regime — the managed float — allowed gradual depreciation that reflected India’s higher inflation relative to the United States. From ₹7.50 to the dollar in 1966, to ₹17 in 1991 at the outset of the crisis, the rupee is at approximately ₹85 in 2024. This long depreciation is not a failure — it is the arithmetic of India’s inflation differential with the United States accumulated over decades. An Indian who held rupees rather than dollars over this period lost real value relative to dollar holders; an Indian who built a business, educated herself, and invested in Indian assets outperformed the exchange rate depreciation through productivity gains that the currency depreciation partially offsets.

IT Exports and the Rupee: When Weakness Becomes Profit

One of the most elegant features of the Indian IT sector’s business model — which emerged serendipitously from the coincidence of rupee weakness and dollar-denominated revenues — is the natural exchange rate hedge it provides:

When the rupee weakens from ₹75 to ₹85 per dollar, an Indian IT company that earns $100 million in annual revenue sees its rupee revenues jump from ₹7,500 crore to ₹8,500 crore — a 13.3% revenue increase in rupee terms, with no additional business won. But its costs — programmer salaries, office rents, electricity — remain in rupees and don’t automatically change. The rupee depreciation creates windfall profit.

This is why Indian IT companies’ quarterly earnings reports pay enormous attention to the rupee-dollar rate. Infosys, TCS, Wipro, and HCL Technologies are among the most currency-sensitive large corporations in the world — their profit margins can swing by full percentage points based on exchange rate movements. An IT CFO who doesn’t hedge currency risk is essentially betting the company’s profitability on the rupee-dollar rate.

The irony is that the same currency weakness that creates IT profits makes imported electronics (the computers and servers that Indian IT companies need) more expensive, makes foreign university education more expensive for Indian students, and makes India’s oil import bill costlier. The exchange rate distributes gains and losses throughout the economy simultaneously.

Rupee Internationalisation: The Long Road to Global Currency Status

India has announced its ambition to settle more trade in rupees — to internationalise the rupee and reduce India’s dependence on the dollar. The ambition is genuine; progress is slow; the structural prerequisites are demanding.

Currency internationalisation requires: deep, liquid financial markets in the currency (so that foreign holders can invest in rupee assets with confidence of exit); capital account convertibility — the ability to freely convert rupees to other currencies and vice versa without restriction; institutional credibility that makes foreign entities comfortable holding rupees as reserves; and — crucially — scale: there must be enough India-related trade and investment that using rupees is efficient for a critical mass of participants.

India trades oil with Russia in rupees (partially — the accumulated rupee balances Russia holds have created a bilateral imbalance that is itself a problem). India-UAE trade in rupees is growing. These are genuine steps, but the dollar’s network effects — every commodity is priced in dollars, most international contracts are denominated in dollars, most foreign exchange reserves are held in dollars — are formidable barriers.

The realistic near-term goal is not rupee reserve currency status but reducing the rupee’s vulnerability to dollar-shocks: reducing dollar-denominated debt, building deeper local-currency bond markets, and gradually expanding the share of trade settled in rupees. These are 15–20 year projects, not policy announcements.

CHAPTER 12 — The Future of Exchange Rates: Digital Money, De-Dollarization, and the New World

The Digital Yuan: Money as Surveillance and Geopolitics

China’s central bank digital currency — the e-CNY or digital yuan — is the most ambitious monetary experiment since Bretton Woods. It is also the most misunderstood.

The e-CNY is not a cryptocurrency in the Bitcoin sense — it is not decentralised, not mined, and not scarce by algorithmic design. It is a liability of the People’s Bank of China, digitally issued, programmable, and traceable to an unprecedented degree. The Chinese government can see, in real time, every e-CNY transaction made by every Chinese user. It can program expiry dates (use it within 30 days or lose it) that force spending during economic slowdowns. It can restrict what the money is spent on. It is money as an instrument of granular economic management — and social control.

For international purposes, the strategic potential is different and more consequential for the dollar: the e-CNY creates the possibility of a payment rail that is completely outside the SWIFT interbank messaging system, which is how virtually all international dollar transactions are processed. If China can convince its Belt and Road partners — Pakistan, Kenya, Indonesia, the UAE — to accept e-CNY payment for trade, those transactions bypass SWIFT entirely. The US loses the visibility into financial flows that SWIFT provides and the ability to impose financial sanctions that SWIFT access makes possible.

The e-CNY pilot has been extensive domestically — hundreds of billions of yuan distributed in digital form in trial cities. Internationally, it remains nascent. But its trajectory is deliberate and its geopolitical logic is undeniable: China wants a payment mechanism that cannot be cut off by Washington.

Cryptocurrency: The Decentralization Dream and Its Limits

Bitcoin was born in 2009 with an elegant promise: a currency outside the control of any government, immune to debasement, transparent in supply (capped at 21 million bitcoins), and transferable globally without a bank. For people in Venezuela, Zimbabwe, or Turkey watching their savings destroyed by government monetary incompetence, the appeal is visceral and rational.

Has it delivered? As an investment asset — spectacularly, volatilely, controversially. Bitcoin has appreciated from essentially zero to approximately $60,000–70,000 by 2024, creating enormous wealth for early holders and destroying enormous wealth for late-cycle buyers who purchased at peaks. As a currency — as a medium of exchange for everyday transactions — it has largely failed. The price volatility (swinging 20% in a week) makes it impractical for pricing goods and services. The transaction speed and cost have not been solved despite years of development. The energy consumption required for Bitcoin mining (roughly equal to Argentina’s entire electricity use) creates environmental costs that are increasingly unacceptable.

Stablecoins — cryptocurrencies pegged to the dollar — tell a more interesting story. Dollar-pegged stablecoins (USDT, USDC) have become enormously used in developing countries as a practical way to hold dollar value on a smartphone without a US bank account. In countries with hyperinflation or currency controls, a Nigerian or Argentine who holds USDT effectively holds dollars without navigating a banking system that may not allow them. This is genuine financial innovation — but it has the ironic effect of strengthening dollar dominance rather than challenging it. People reaching for a stable digital currency reach for dollar stablecoins, not bitcoin or e-CNY.

Climate Change and the Petrodollar’s Future

One of the least discussed but most structurally significant exchange rate stories of the next 30 years involves the energy transition.

The “petrodollar” system — in which oil is priced and traded globally in US dollars — has been a pillar of dollar dominance since 1974, when the Nixon administration negotiated with Saudi Arabia to price oil sales in dollars in exchange for US military protection. The arrangement worked brilliantly for 50 years: every country that needed oil needed dollars, which meant every country maintained dollar reserves, which funded the US Treasury market at low interest rates.

As the world transitions away from fossil fuels toward renewable energy — solar, wind, batteries — the oil trade will shrink. Countries that currently hold large dollar reserves primarily to hedge oil price risk and fund oil imports will need fewer dollars. The petrodollar’s structural support for dollar dominance will erode. This is a genuinely long-term process — the energy transition will take decades — but its exchange rate implications are already beginning to appear in the conversations of finance ministers and central bank governors.

Additionally: the countries that benefit most from a renewable energy world are those with the greatest solar and wind resources. India ranks among the world’s largest renewable energy resource nations — abundant sunshine, significant wind corridors, and a government genuinely committed to the transition. A world powered by renewable electricity is a world where India’s energy security no longer depends so critically on dollar-priced imported oil. The exchange rate implication: a reduction in India’s structurally large oil-import-driven current account deficit, less rupee selling pressure from oil payments, and potentially a structurally stronger rupee over the long run.

De-Dollarization: The Trend That Is Real but Slow

The dollar’s share of global foreign exchange reserves has fallen from approximately 71% in 2000 to approximately 59% today. This is statistically significant and directionally clear: a slow erosion of dollar dominance over two decades. It would take another half-century at this rate to reduce the dollar’s reserve share to the euro’s current approximately 20%.

The factors driving slow de-dollarization: - The weaponisation of the dollar through sanctions (Russia’s $300 billion in reserves frozen in 2022 after the Ukraine invasion sent a global signal: dollar reserves can be confiscated by the US government) - China’s effort to internationalise the renminbi through bilateral agreements and the e-CNY - The natural diversification impulse of central banks seeking to reduce concentration risk - Gradual deepening of non-dollar financial markets

The factors maintaining dollar dominance: - No credible alternative reserve currency exists - US bond markets remain the world’s most liquid safe asset - The dollar’s network effects are self-reinforcing (everyone uses it because everyone else uses it) - The renminbi lacks convertibility; the euro lacks a fiscal union; cryptocurrency lacks stability

The realistic scenario for the next 20 years: a gradual multipolar currency world in which the dollar’s share falls to perhaps 45–50%, with the euro, renminbi, yen, pound, and possibly a BRICS settlement mechanism each taking meaningful but not dominant shares. This would be less advantageous for the United States but would not represent a “fall of the dollar.” More like the decline of an absolute monarchy to a constitutional monarchy — still powerful, just less absolute.

The Ultimate Synthesis: The Exchange Rate as Institutional Mirror

Return one final time to the Chennai airport currency counter. You slide ₹8,500 across. You receive $100.

That transaction is embedded in a system that processes $7.5 trillion per day. It reflects interest rate decisions made in Washington, inflation data released in Mumbai, geopolitical tensions over Taiwan, the Fed’s minutes, the decisions of three thousand foreign fund managers reading screens in Singapore and London and New York. It embodies the accumulated verdict of markets on the relative institutional credibility, monetary discipline, productivity trajectory, and political stability of two countries.

The exchange rate is not just a price. It is:

A thermometer: measuring the economic health of a nation — rising when growth and investment confidence are strong, falling when they weaken.

A weapon: deployed by governments to gain export advantage (China’s administered renminbi), adjusted through international agreements (the Plaza Accord), and attacked by speculators (Soros’s Black Wednesday), who in targeting the exchange rate are targeting something more fundamental — the sustainability of a policy commitment.

A mirror: reflecting institutional credibility back at governments that have chosen to inflate, capture central banks, or commit to unsustainable pegs. Currencies don’t lie for long. They eventually tell the truth about the economy beneath them — as Venezuela’s bolivar told the truth about Chavismo’s contradictions, as the Turkish lira told the truth about Erdoğan’s monetary heterodoxy, as the Indian rupee in 1991 told the truth about four decades of overvalued management reaching its limits.

A vehicle for geopolitical ambition: the dollar’s reserve currency status is America’s greatest financial strategic asset; China’s digital yuan is a bid to create an alternative; the BRICS payment discussions are a coalition of the dissatisfied building institutional alternatives.

A teacher: the most instructive economic history of the past century runs through exchange rates. The Great Depression’s depth was determined by how long countries stayed on gold. The Asian miracle was built on cheap currencies. The Lost Decades were triggered by the forced yen appreciation. The 1997 Asian crisis was an exchange rate crisis. The 2008 global crisis displaced the currency system temporarily but currency management returned as the primary recovery tool. Every great economic story of the modern era has exchange rates at its heart.

To understand exchange rates is to hold in your hands one of the most powerful lenses for understanding how the world actually works — how monetary decisions in Washington reverberate in Chennai, how a single hedge fund’s analysis of British monetary policy can move a billion-pound profit in a single day, how the price of petrol at your local pump in Bengaluru is ultimately set by a combination of dollar-priced crude, a rupee-dollar exchange rate managed by the RBI, and geopolitical decisions made in Riyadh.

You started this report at a currency exchange counter at Chennai airport, handing over ₹8,500 for $100. You understand now that the number on that counter is not arbitrary, not bureaucratic, and not simple. It is the distillation of everything this report has described: the supply and demand of currencies that encode the entire flow of international trade and investment, the interest rate policies of central banks on four continents, the inflation differentials between two economies, the accumulated credibility of their institutions, and the market’s collective, non-stop, real-time judgment on the economic health and policy wisdom of every nation on Earth.

Use that number. Watch it. Understand what moves it. And when a currency collapses — anywhere in the world — you now know where to look for the cause. Not in the markets. In the institutions, the policies, and the decisions that the markets merely reveal.
