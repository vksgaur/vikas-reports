🐉 The Dragon Awakes: How China Became the World’s Manufacturing Superpower

The Most Astonishing Economic Transformation in Human History

Consider what China did in 40 years. In 1978, China’s GDP per capita was roughly $200 — less than that of Chad or Bangladesh. By 2023, it was $13,000. The absolute size of the Chinese economy grew from $149 billion to $18 trillion. China went from making almost nothing the world wanted to making almost everything the world uses: 80% of the world’s air conditioners, 70% of its smartphones, 60% of its shoes, 50% of its steel. In those same 40 years, China lifted 850 million people out of extreme poverty — more people than existed on Earth in 1750, rescued from destitution in a single human lifetime. No country has ever grown this fast, for this long, from so low a starting point, at this scale. Not the British Industrial Revolution, which took 150 years. Not Japan’s postwar miracle, which involved a country of 100 million. Not the American 20th century, which began from a position of extraordinary advantage. What China did between 1978 and 2023 is the most remarkable economic achievement in the history of human civilization. Understanding how it happened is not merely interesting — it is essential for anyone who wants to understand the world.

CHAPTER 1 — The Sleeping Dragon: China Before Modernization (Pre-1949)

Life in the Middle Kingdom: A Timeless World

To understand the magnitude of China’s transformation, you must first understand where it began.

Walk into a village in Hunan province in 1900. The scene has changed remarkably little in the previous 500 years: the same mud-brick farmhouses with thatched or tile roofs, the same water buffalo pulling wooden ploughs through rice paddies, the same rhythms of planting and harvest governing every dimension of life. Chen Dashan — our imaginary peasant — rises before dawn to tend to the rice that has fed his family and paid the landlord for generations. He cannot read. His wife cannot read. Their children will likely not read either, unless they show extraordinary promise and a wealthy sponsor takes interest. Their world extends perhaps 20 kilometres in any direction; the empire’s capital is as distant and abstract as the moon.

The Chinese peasant’s life was not without dignity or complexity. Villages had their own social lives, festivals, religious practices, and mutual aid networks. The Confucian social order — with its precise hierarchies of emperor, officials, gentry, farmers, artisans, and merchants — had organized Chinese civilization with remarkable stability for millennia. The examination system (keju) offered, in theory, a path from peasantry to officialdom through scholarly achievement — a meritocratic ideal that gave Chinese civilization a distinctive character of social mobility aspiration even within profound inequality.

But the material life was precarious to a degree difficult to comprehend from a modern vantage point. Famines were routine: China experienced hundreds of localized famines between 1000 CE and 1900 CE. Life expectancy at birth hovered around 35 years — not because most people died young but because infant and child mortality was catastrophic. A Chinese peasant family in 1900 might have 5–6 children, of whom 2–3 would survive to adulthood. The surplus agricultural output above bare subsistence flowed to the landlord class (typically owning 70–80% of the land while comprising 10% of the population), to local officials, to imperial taxes, and eventually — in the Qing dynasty’s later years — to the humiliating payment of war indemnities to foreign powers.

China’s Lost Century of Superiority

The Chinese civilization that entered modern history had reason for its extraordinary self-confidence.

At the height of the Song Dynasty (960–1279 CE), China was, by most measures, the most technologically advanced society on Earth. Its astronomers had the most sophisticated celestial charts. Its ironmongers were producing cast iron centuries before Europe. Its farmers cultivated with techniques — crop rotation, sophisticated irrigation, wet rice cultivation — that Europeans would not develop for centuries. The Grand Canal, stretching 1,776 kilometres from Beijing to Hangzhou, was the world’s longest artificial waterway and a logistical marvel that knit the empire together commercially. Song Dynasty China had paper money — jiaozi — six centuries before European banks issued notes. It had gunpowder, the compass, block printing, and porcelain: the four great inventions that Chinese historians have rightly celebrated as world-historical contributions.

The maritime silk roads carried Chinese ceramics, silk, and spices to Southeast Asia, India, the Persian Gulf, and the East African coast. When the Yongle Emperor sent the Muslim eunuch admiral Zheng He on his great voyages (1405–1433) — with fleets of over 300 ships and 27,000 men, dwarfing Columbus’s three ships and 90 sailors by a factor that staggers the imagination — Chinese civilization was projecting power and commerce across half the world’s oceans.

Then came the pivot that changed everything. In 1433, the Yongle Emperor’s successors abruptly ended the voyages, burned the ships, banned ocean-going vessels, and turned inward. This retreat — driven by Confucian court officials who viewed maritime trade as beneath the dignity of the empire and foreign commerce as destabilizing — was one of history’s more consequential decisions. While China looked inward, Europe looked outward. While China perfected its internal civilization, Portugal sailed around Africa, Spain crossed the Atlantic, and British industrialists learned to harness steam.

The Industrial Revolution — beginning in Britain in the 1760s, spreading to Europe and North America through the 19th century — was the civilizational divergence that put China permanently on the back foot. In 1820, China still produced approximately 33% of global GDP — the largest single economy on Earth. By 1952, amid civil war, foreign invasion, and dynastic collapse, that share had fallen to 5%. The most powerful civilization in the world had, in 150 years, become one of its most impoverished.

The Century of Humiliation: Shame as Rocket Fuel

September 4, 1839: Commissioner Lin Zexu ordered the destruction of 2.376 million pounds of British opium on the beaches of Humen, Guangdong — 20,000 chests of the drug that the British East India Company had been systematically flooding into China to reverse its trade deficit (China sold tea, silk, and porcelain to Britain; Britain had nothing China wanted, so it created a want). Britain’s response to the opium destruction was war.

The First Opium War (1839–1842) ended with the Treaty of Nanking — China’s first “unequal treaty” and the opening humiliation of what Chinese historians call the Bǎi Nián Guóchǐ (百年国耻): the Century of Humiliation. The treaty ceded Hong Kong to Britain in perpetuity, opened five treaty ports to British trade, gave British traders extraterritorial rights on Chinese soil (British citizens who committed crimes in China would be tried by British courts, not Chinese ones — a sovereign violation so complete it still generates rage), and imposed an indemnity of 21 million silver dollars.

It was the beginning of a process that would dismember Chinese sovereignty over the following century through a succession of imposed humiliations:

The Second Opium War (1856–1860): Britain and France, seeking to expand trade privileges, defeated China again. The Old Summer Palace (Yuanmingyuan) — a complex of extraordinary architectural beauty, China’s most magnificent imperial garden — was looted and burned by British and French troops. Lord Elgin, who ordered the burning (his father had stripped the Parthenon marbles from Greece), considered it a “teachable moment” for the Qing dynasty.

The First Sino-Japanese War (1894–1895): China, which had considered Japan a smaller, inferior civilization for millennia, was defeated by the recently modernized Meiji state. The Treaty of Shimonoseki transferred Taiwan to Japan and imposed another massive indemnity — 200 million silver taels — that the Japanese used partly to build their modern steel industry. The psychological shock was enormous: if even Japan could defeat China, what had China become?

The Boxer Rebellion and its aftermath (1900–1901): When Chinese nationalists (the “Boxers”) rose against foreign exploitation, the Eight-Nation Alliance (Britain, France, Germany, Russia, Japan, the United States, Austria-Hungary, and Italy) invaded, occupied Beijing, and imposed the Boxer Protocol: the largest war indemnity in history at the time — 450 million taels of silver, to be paid over 39 years with interest. The Qing government was compelled to allow foreign troops to be permanently stationed in Beijing. The dynasty was finished; it formally ended in 1912.

The Japanese invasion (1931–1945): Japan’s occupation of Manchuria in 1931, full invasion in 1937, and subsequent occupation of China’s most productive regions — accompanied by atrocities of extraordinary brutality (the Nanjing Massacre of 1937, in which Japanese troops killed an estimated 200,000–300,000 Chinese civilians and prisoners of war) — was the final wound of the century of humiliation.

This 110-year sequence of defeats, extractions, and humiliations did something to the Chinese national psyche that is impossible to understand China’s subsequent history without acknowledging: it created a burning determination for restoration that has been the most powerful motivating force in Chinese politics since 1949. Every Chinese leader from Mao to Xi has operated within the framework of national rejuvenation — the repudiation of humiliation, the restoration of the place China believes it deserves. The extreme poverty that China endured was not experienced merely as poverty; it was experienced as evidence of national failure, as a wound to civilizational pride that demanded healing.

Three Failed Industrializations and What They Left

Between 1861 and 1949, China attempted modernization three times, and three times failed:

The Self-Strengthening Movement (1861–1895): After the Second Opium War’s humiliation, Qing officials like Li Hongzhang and Zuo Zongtang launched a program of selective modernization: arsenals, shipyards, telegraph lines, and a modern navy, all built with Western technology purchased or licensed from foreign firms. The philosophy: acquire Western weapons while preserving Chinese essence (Zhongxue wei ti, Xixue wei yong — “Chinese learning as the foundation, Western learning for practical application”). The fatal flaw: self-strengthening was confined to military modernization without reforming the economic, educational, or political institutions that made Western industry possible. The 1895 defeat by Japan — a nation that had undertaken much deeper modernization through the Meiji Restoration — ended the movement’s credibility.

The Reform of 1898 and Republican Revolution (1895–1912): The young Emperor Guangxu, inspired by Kang Youwei’s radical reform proposals, launched the Hundred Days’ Reform — 103 days of extraordinary edicts that proposed modernizing every dimension of Chinese government and society. The Empress Dowager Cixi staged a coup, imprisoned the Emperor, and executed the reform leaders. The dynasty’s inability to reform itself from within led to its overthrow in 1912 — Sun Yat-sen’s Republic of China replaced the imperial system. But the Republican period (1912–1949) was dominated by warlordism, civil war between the KMT and Communists, and Japanese invasion. No sustained industrialization could take root.

The Nationalist (KMT) Decade (1927–1937): Chiang Kai-shek’s Nationalists briefly achieved something approaching national unity and began genuine industrial development in the 1930s. Shanghai, with its foreign concessions and compradors, was becoming Asia’s most dynamic industrial and commercial city. But the Japanese invasion of 1937 obliterated this decade’s gains and began eight years of devastation. The subsequent civil war (1946–1949) between KMT and Communists finished whatever remained.

China in 1949: Devastated But Determined

When Mao Zedong stood on Tiananmen Gate on October 1, 1949, and announced the founding of the People’s Republic, he was declaring victory over a century of humiliation from a position of extraordinary weakness:

  • GDP per capita: approximately $23 — less than almost anywhere in the world
  • Life expectancy: 35 years — comparable to medieval Europe
  • Literacy rate: approximately 20% — 80% of the population could not read
  • Industrial capacity: essentially zero — virtually no modern industry survived the war decade
  • Infrastructure: destroyed — railways bombed, ports ruined, roads barely existing
  • Rural population: approximately 90% — a country of farmers with no industrial base

But there was also something else Mao inherited: a unified nation for the first time in decades, a people who had survived extraordinary suffering, and a burning, disciplined, furious determination that the century of humiliation would never be repeated.

CHAPTER 2 — Mao’s Paradox: Destruction and Foundation (1949–1976)

The Most Consequential Figure of the 20th Century — and the Most Controversial

Mao Zedong is one of those figures — like Napoleon, like Stalin, like Churchill — whose legacy is so enormous and so contradictory that every generation must assess him anew, and every assessment reveals as much about the assessor as the assessed.

He was born in 1893 in Shaoshan, Hunan — the son of a modestly prosperous peasant who had risen from poverty and had the contradictory instincts toward his own son of wanting education (which Mao received) while resisting any elevation above his own authority (which Mao resisted fiercely, eventually leaving home to pursue revolution). The Oedipal dynamic of Mao’s upbringing — the father who was simultaneously an obstacle and a model — runs through his political psychology: he simultaneously revered and despised authority.

He discovered Marxism in Beijing in 1919, helped found the Chinese Communist Party in 1921, survived the catastrophic defeat of the 1927 Shanghai massacre (when Chiang Kai-shek had thousands of Communists killed in a single night), led the Long March (1934–1935) — the legendary 8,000-kilometre retreat that became the founding myth of Chinese Communist identity — and eventually led his party to total victory in the civil war in 1949.

As a strategist and military leader, he was a genius. As an economic policymaker, he was a catastrophe. As a politician, he was a master of the brutal art — and as a poet, genuinely accomplished. He was all of these things simultaneously, and Chinese history cannot be understood without holding the full complexity.

The Collectivization That Killed Individual Incentive

Mao’s first major economic policy — the collectivization of Chinese agriculture — was driven by genuine ideological conviction and a strategic reading of how industrial capital could be extracted from an agricultural economy.

The logic: China had no foreign capital, no external financing, and insufficient internal commercial capital to fund industrialization. The only available source of investment capital was the agricultural surplus — the rice, wheat, and cotton that China’s 500 million peasants produced. To capture that surplus for the state (which would then invest it in industry), agriculture needed to be collectivized: private plots eliminated, production organized through People’s Communes, and surplus delivered to the state at state-set prices below market value.

The land reform of 1950–1953 was, in itself, remarkably popular: it took land from the landlords who owned 70% of it (approximately 3 million landlords were killed in the process, in violence Mao explicitly welcomed) and distributed it to the peasants who had worked it. For the first time in Chinese history, the rural majority owned the land they farmed.

Then came the collectivization that took it back: first into cooperatives (1953–1955), then into People’s Communes (1958), in which private ownership was abolished, communal kitchens replaced private cooking, and production decisions were made by state-appointed managers rather than individual farming families.

The economic consequence was predictable and devastating: when farmers don’t own the output of their labor, and when their daily meals come from a communal kitchen regardless of how hard (or little) they work, the incentive to work hard disappears. Crop yields fell. Animals were slaughtered rather than surrendered to the collective. Small-scale rural industry was nationalized and often destroyed in the process. The communal kitchens initially served abundant meals — eating down the community’s grain reserves — before the reserves ran out.

The Great Leap Forward: The Deadliest Development Policy in History

In 1958, Mao launched the Great Leap Forward (Dàyuèjìn) — one of the most ambitious, most catastrophically conceived, and most disastrously executed economic programmes in human history.

The ambition: China would simultaneously industrialize and collectivize, transforming itself from an agrarian society to an industrial one within fifteen years. Steel production would overtake Britain’s within fifteen years. The agricultural communes would release labour for industry. China would leap from feudalism to communism, bypassing capitalism entirely.

The programme: Mao announced that steel production must be massively increased. Since China had few modern steel mills, the solution was the backyard steel furnace campaign — every village, every commune, every factory would build small steel furnaces and contribute to the national target. In the summer and autumn of 1958, an estimated 90 million Chinese spent their energies melting scrap metal — woks, door hinges, agricultural tools, anything metal — into small ingots of steel in backyard furnaces.

The problem: the steel produced was almost entirely worthless. Backyard furnaces cannot achieve the temperatures required for quality steel production; the result was pig iron of such poor quality it could not be used for any industrial purpose. But more catastrophically: the 90 million people melting scrap metal were not farming. At the precise moment when China’s crops needed to be harvested, hundreds of millions of agricultural workers were being redirected to the steel furnaces, to the construction of irrigation projects, to political meetings — and the crops were left in the fields to rot.

The Great Leap Forward produced a famine of extraordinary scale. The estimates — disputed among historians — range from 15 million to 45 million deaths between 1959 and 1962, with the most carefully researched studies (Yang Jisheng’s Tombstone, Frank Dikötter’s Mao’s Great Famine) converging on figures around 35–45 million. Whatever the precise number — and the imprecision itself is a product of deliberate data suppression by the state — the Great Leap Forward famine was one of the deadliest man-made disasters in recorded human history.

The cruelty was compounded by the political system’s response: local officials who reported food shortages were accused of “rightism” and punished. Grain continued to be exported to the Soviet Union and to demonstrate Chinese agricultural success even as people starved. The information distortion of the planned economy — in which officials reported what their superiors wanted to hear rather than what was actually happening — cost millions of lives. A market economy’s price signals would have communicated the food shortage immediately and triggered responses; the planned economy’s political signals created silence until the catastrophe was irreversible.

The Cultural Revolution: Destroying the People Who Could Build

If the Great Leap Forward was economic catastrophe, the Cultural Revolution (1966–1976) was institutional catastrophe — an attack on the very people and structures that any development process requires.

Mao, politically weakened after the Great Leap Forward’s disaster and increasingly paranoid about challengers within the Party, launched the Cultural Revolution as a mechanism for eliminating his rivals and re-establishing his absolute authority. He mobilized China’s youth — the Red Guards, teenagers and young adults burning with revolutionary fervor — to attack “bourgeois” elements in Chinese society.

The targets: teachers, professors, scientists, engineers, doctors, writers, artists — anyone with education, expertise, or independent authority that might constitute an alternative source of legitimacy to Mao’s personal authority. Universities were closed for four years (1966–1970). Professors were beaten by their own students, forced to wear dunce caps, marched through streets in humiliation, and sent to “re-education” camps to work as farmers. An estimated 1–2 million people were killed; tens of millions were persecuted.

The development cost of destroying China’s educated class for a decade cannot be overstated. A generation of scientists, engineers, doctors, and teachers had their careers interrupted or ended. The scientific infrastructure built during the 1950s — laboratories, research institutions, universities — was damaged or destroyed. China’s human capital — the most important ingredient for any knowledge-intensive economic development — was deliberately attacked at precisely the moment when the country needed it most.

Deng Xiaoping was among those purged: China’s second-most-important figure of the 20th century was sent to work in a tractor factory in Jiangxi province during the Cultural Revolution. When he eventually returned to power and rebuilt China, he carried with him a visceral, personally experienced understanding of what happens when ideology substitutes for pragmatism. His reformist instincts were not abstract; they were written in the memory of years doing manual labor while China’s development stagnated.

Mao’s Hidden Foundation: What the Brutality Built

Yet — and this is the most uncomfortable truth about China’s miracle — the Maoist period also laid several foundations without which the subsequent transformation might have been impossible:

National unity: Mao ended the warlordism, fractional politics, and foreign exploitation that had prevented any sustained development for a century. For all its authoritarian brutality, the People’s Republic created a unified political entity with effective central control for the first time in generations. Without this unity, Deng’s coordinated economic reforms would have been impossible to implement at national scale.

Literacy and basic education: Mao’s campaigns dramatically increased China’s literacy rate — from approximately 20% in 1949 to approximately 65% by 1976. The simplification of written Chinese characters (jiǎntǐzì) — reducing the number of strokes required, making learning easier — was a Maoist reform with lasting developmental benefits. A literate workforce could operate machinery, follow written instructions, and eventually participate in a modern economy.

Infrastructure in interior regions: The Maoist state built railways, roads, dams, and irrigation systems — often brutally, using forced labor — into regions that had no modern infrastructure. These were economically inefficient by market standards; they connected strategically important interior regions and proved valuable in the reform era’s development of China’s interior provinces.

Basic public health: Mao’s “barefoot doctors” programme extended basic medical care into rural villages, dramatically reducing mortality from infectious disease. Life expectancy in China rose from 35 years in 1949 to 65 years in 1976 — one of the fastest life expectancy gains in history, achieved primarily through public health rather than economic growth.

Elimination of feudalism: The “feudal” land system — an extreme concentration of land in a small landlord class, with the great majority of rural people as tenants or laborers — was eliminated through land reform, however brutal the process. When Deng Xiaoping later gave land back to peasant families under the Household Responsibility System, he was giving back newly created family plots that the Maoist land reform had created from the rubble of the landlord system.

The counterfactual is unresolvable: would China’s development have been faster without Mao? Almost certainly yes. Would it have been possible at all, starting from the chaos of 1949, without something like the unifying brutality of the Maoist state? Many historians argue: possibly not. This is not a defence of Mao’s crimes. It is an attempt to understand a historical sequence in which the same system that destroyed millions of lives also created some of the preconditions for the lives of hundreds of millions to be transformed for the better.

CHAPTER 3 — The Great Pivot: Deng Xiaoping and the Reform Revolution (1978–1989)

The Man Who Survived Everything

Deng Xiaoping’s biography is one of the more extraordinary political survival stories of the 20th century — a man who was purged twice, rehabilitated twice, survived the Cultural Revolution’s destruction of everything he had built, and emerged at age 73 to become the most consequential reformer in the history of economic development.

He was born in 1904 in Sichuan province, trained in France (where he worked in a Renault factory and became a communist organizer), returned to China to join the revolutionary movement, and rose through decades of party struggle to become China’s second-most-powerful figure by the 1950s. His relationship with Mao was always complex: he was loyal enough to survive the early years, pragmatic enough to attract the enmity of Maoist hardliners, and politically shrewd enough to be rehabilitated after each fall.

His first purge came during the Cultural Revolution. Denounced as a “capitalist roader” by Red Guards, stripped of all positions, and sent to work in a factory in Jiangxi — the famous Deng Xiaoping tractor factory period — he spent years in political exile, reading, thinking, and presumably drawing conclusions about what ideological excess had cost China. His son Deng Pufang was thrown from a window by Red Guards during a struggle session and was permanently paralyzed — a personal cost that makes Deng’s subsequent restraint in seeking vengeance against his political enemies even more remarkable.

His second purge came in 1976, when Mao’s associates blamed him for the Tiananmen Square demonstrations of that year (the demonstrations honoring the recently deceased Premier Zhou Enlai, broadly seen as the moderate face of the Party). Deng was again stripped of positions and put under house arrest.

When Mao died on September 9, 1976, and the radical Gang of Four — Mao’s wife Jiang Qing and her three associates who had overseen the Cultural Revolution’s worst excesses — was arrested within weeks, Deng’s rehabilitation became possible. He had outlived his enemies, survived the system that had tried to destroy him, and was now positioned to rebuild what he had watched be wrecked.

His cat metaphor — “It doesn’t matter whether the cat is black or white, as long as it catches mice” — was not a throwaway line. It was a philosophy of governance expressed with characteristic economy: ideology is means, not end. What matters is results. If capitalism catches mice better than communism in a given context, use capitalism. The pragmatism was radical because in a one-party Marxist state, ideology is legitimacy; to dismiss ideology as mere means rather than sacred end was to threaten the very basis of Party rule. Deng did it anyway — with consequences that continue to shape the world.

The Meeting That Changed the World: December 1978

The Third Plenum of the 11th Central Committee of the Chinese Communist Party, held in Beijing from December 15–22, 1978, is, by any measure, one of the most consequential meetings in modern history. More decisions affecting more human lives were made in those eight days than in almost any other single gathering of the 20th century.

The meeting formally announced the shift from class struggle to economic modernization as China’s central task — ending the Maoist orthodoxy that political-ideological purity mattered more than economic results. It endorsed agricultural reforms that were already beginning in Anhui and Sichuan provinces. It committed to opening China to foreign investment and technology. And it established Deng Xiaoping — not as formal head of state, but as paramount leader — as the architect of what would follow.

The shift was expressed with characteristic communist-party circumspection: there were no dramatic denunciations, no explicit repudiation of Mao (Mao was declared “70% right, 30% wrong” — a diplomatic formula that preserved public reverence while enabling policy change). But the direction was unmistakeable to everyone in the room and, within months, to everyone in China.

Reform 1: The Household Responsibility System — Giving Land Back to Life

The reform that most immediately transformed China’s agricultural economy began not with a government announcement but with a secret agreement among 18 families in Xiaogang village, Anhui province, in November 1978.

These 18 families — exhausted by the communal farming system that had left them chronically hungry — gathered secretly and signed a document in which they agreed to divide the commune’s land among individual families, each family to be responsible for its own production and to keep (after meeting the state quota) whatever it produced. The agreement was secret because what they were doing was illegal — private farming had been banned since the collectivization of the 1950s. They included a provision that if any of them were sent to prison for this agreement, the other families would raise the imprisoned members’ children. It was that serious a risk.

The Xiaogang experiment — which produced a harvest in 1979 several times larger than any previous year — was quietly endorsed rather than condemned by Deng’s government, which was simultaneously receiving similar reports from experimental reforms in Sichuan province. By 1982, the Household Responsibility System was national policy: land remained formally state-owned, but farming families received long-term leases and, crucially, could keep the surplus after meeting the state’s quota.

The results were immediate and extraordinary. Chinese agricultural production grew by approximately 7.1% per year between 1978 and 1984 — a rate comparable to the most successful Green Revolution experiences anywhere in the world. Grain output rose by a third. Incomes in rural areas doubled. And something equally important happened: the rural labor force, no longer required to collectively maintain large agricultural operations, became available for industrial employment. This was the first wave of the great migration.

The Xiaogang story is essential because it illustrates something important about how China’s reforms actually worked: from the bottom up as much as from the top down. The state set direction; but the innovations often came from people who were incentivized to find solutions and who had enough political space (barely, in Xiaogang’s case) to try them, with the state ratifying successful experiments at scale. This “crossing the river by feeling for stones” approach — Deng’s metaphor — allowed China to learn from experiment rather than committing to untested blueprints.

Reform 2: Township and Village Enterprises — The Factory in the Fields

The Household Responsibility System freed agricultural workers; the Township and Village Enterprises (TVEs) employed them.

TVEs — factories and processing plants run by village and township governments, officially collective enterprises rather than private businesses (a legal distinction that mattered enormously in a state still formally committed to socialism) — were the first wave of Chinese industrial development. They occupied a curious legal grey zone: collective, therefore compatible with socialist ideology; yet market-oriented, competing for customers and profits, with workers paid by results.

They were extraordinarily dynamic. Rural entrepreneurs, often former commune officials or people with some technical skill, set up factories making furniture, textiles, bricks, machine parts, food products, and thousands of other items. They hired their neighbors, paid wages above farm income, and competed for customers in domestic markets that state enterprises served poorly or not at all. TVE employment rose from 28 million in 1978 to 128 million by 1993 — one of the fastest industrialization waves in history.

The TVE model solved two problems simultaneously: it absorbed surplus rural labor (people leaving agriculture could find industrial work without migrating to cities, which the hukou household registration system made difficult) and it generated the domestic manufacturing capacity that could supply an urbanizing economy with consumer goods.

Reform 3: Special Economic Zones — Capitalism’s Laboratories in a Communist Laboratory

The most visibly dramatic of Deng’s reforms — and the one that would eventually transform China’s relationship with the global economy — was the creation of Special Economic Zones (SEZs).

In 1980, four zones were designated: Shenzhen (adjacent to Hong Kong), Zhuhai (adjacent to Macao), Shantou, and Xiamen (facing Taiwan). The choice of locations was deliberate: each was positioned to attract investment from the Overseas Chinese communities of Hong Kong, Macao, and Taiwan — people with capital and manufacturing know-how who shared cultural and linguistic connections with mainland China.

Within the SEZs, the rules were different: foreign companies could invest, employ workers, generate profits, and repatriate earnings. Import duties were reduced. Labour regulations were more flexible. Land leases were available. Capitalist economic rules applied within zones that were, literally and metaphorically, laboratories for capitalism inside a communist body — sealed-off experimental chambers where the economic biology worked differently than in the surrounding organism.

What happened in Shenzhen in the following decade was one of history’s more astonishing transformations. In 1980, Shenzhen was a fishing village of approximately 30,000 people. By 2000, it was a city of 7 million, with gleaming factories, highways, and apartment blocks where rice paddies had stood two decades earlier. By 2023, greater Shenzhen had a population of approximately 18 million and a GDP of over $550 billion — comparable to a medium-sized European country.

The SEZs attracted investment from Hong Kong manufacturers who could no longer afford Hong Kong’s rising wages, from Taiwanese electronics companies looking for a cost-competitive production base, from Japanese firms seeking to reduce production costs, and eventually from every major multinational that wanted to serve the growing Chinese domestic market or to source manufactured goods for global distribution.

Reform 4: Dual-Track Pricing — Why China Avoided the Soviet Collapse

One of the China model’s least celebrated but most important innovations was the dual-track pricing system — the mechanism that allowed China to liberalize its economy gradually without the chaos that destroyed the Soviet Union when it tried to liberalize rapidly.

The Soviet Union’s shock therapy — the sudden elimination of price controls across the entire economy in January 1992 — produced hyperinflation, economic collapse, and a contraction of GDP that exceeded the Great Depression in severity. The logic of shock therapy was economically sound (distorted prices must be corrected) but politically catastrophic (the immediate disruption destroyed the government’s legitimacy and created conditions for oligarchic capture).

China’s dual-track approach: state enterprises continued to operate at planned prices (receiving inputs and selling outputs at state-set prices); while simultaneously, production above the state plan quota could be sold on the market at market prices. Over time, as market-price production grew and planned-price production shrank as a share of the total, the economy gradually transitioned from planned to market prices — without ever imposing a sudden shock on the state enterprise sector that would have created catastrophic disruption.

This was a masterclass in what economists call “sequencing reform”: changing price signals gradually, protecting existing stakeholders from immediate losses (reducing political opposition), while creating space for market mechanisms to develop. It was not ideologically pure, and it created significant arbitrage opportunities (for the politically connected officials who could buy at plan prices and sell at market prices — the basis of much 1980s corruption). But it kept the reform process moving without destroying it.

Reform 5: Opening to Foreign Direct Investment

The reforms described above changed China internally. The opening to Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) connected China to the global economy — and this connection would ultimately prove the most transformative of all.

When Deng opened China to foreign investment, he was making an implicit bargain: foreign companies could access China’s cheap labor and its growing market in exchange for bringing capital, technology, and management knowledge that China lacked. The foreign company got a manufacturing base; China got an industrial education.

The quality of that education was substantial. When a Taiwanese electronics manufacturer set up a factory in the Pearl River Delta in 1985, it brought:

  • Production processes developed and refined over decades
  • Quality management systems built on Japanese TQM principles
  • Supply chain management practices from working with global brands
  • Engineering standards for electronics assembly
  • Management training for local supervisors and technicians

Chinese workers who learned these skills became the instructors for the next generation of Chinese factories. The knowledge spread through the industrial ecosystem — through workers who left foreign factories to start their own companies, through reverse engineering of foreign products, through engineers trained at foreign firms who later built Chinese competitors. This knowledge transfer was not accidentally donated; the foreign companies were trying to protect their technology as much as possible. But in the dense industrial clusters that developed around the SEZs, the knowledge leaked anyway — through human movement, through supplier relationships, through the ecosystem dynamics that make clusters so productive.

Tiananmen 1989: The Line Deng Drew

On June 4, 1989, the Chinese People’s Liberation Army moved on Tiananmen Square in Beijing, killing an unknown number of protesters (estimates range from hundreds to thousands) who had been demonstrating for political reform — democracy, freedom of the press, an end to corruption — for seven weeks. The images of a lone man facing a column of tanks became the defining image of political repression in the late 20th century.

For Deng Xiaoping — who almost certainly approved the crackdown — the decision reflected a clear and explicit choice: economic liberalization yes, political liberalization no. Deng believed, with a coherence one can reject but must understand, that China’s development required political stability above all, and that political stability in a country of China’s scale and history required one-party rule. He had watched the Soviet Union’s political liberalization under Gorbachev lead to the Party’s loss of control and, in his view, chaos. He was not willing to follow.

The Tiananmen decision set the template for the China we see today: an economy increasingly integrated into global capitalism, and a political system that remains a single-party state. Whether this combination is stable over the long term — whether economic development inevitably generates political liberalization pressures that cannot be indefinitely contained — is the central unresolved question about China’s 21st-century future.

What Tiananmen definitely did in the short term: it cast an international shadow over China’s reform trajectory, froze foreign investment for several years, and stalled the reform momentum that Deng had created in the 1980s. By 1991, the reform project appeared partially in retreat.

And that is when Deng Xiaoping — 87 years old, nearly deaf, partially paralyzed, no longer holding any formal office — made one last extraordinary intervention.

The 88-Year-Old Who Restarted History

By 1991, three years after Tiananmen, China’s reform momentum had stalled. Conservative Party elders, emboldened by the Soviet Union’s apparent collapse (which they attributed to Gorbachev’s political liberalization), were arguing that China had gone too far, too fast with market reforms. The SEZs were attacked as hotbeds of “spiritual pollution.” Inflation in the late 1980s had been blamed on reforms. The reformist tide was turning.

In January 1992, Deng Xiaoping — 88 years old, effectively retired from formal positions, nearly deaf, walking only with assistance — boarded a train for Guangdong province and undertook what became known as the Nanxun (南巡): the Southern Tour.

For 35 days, Deng traveled to Shenzhen, Zhuhai, Shanghai, and other reform showpieces. And he talked — to local officials and businesspeople, in speeches designed to be heard by the entire Party through the informal networks that the Party’s communication system both suppressed and depended on. What he said was unambiguous:

“Anyone who doesn’t reform should step down.”

“Development is the last word. Development is absolute truth.”

“Whoever does not advance with reform and opening is derelict in their duty.”

Standing in Shenzhen — the fishing village that had become a gleaming city of millions in a decade — Deng was presenting physical evidence for his argument. Not abstract economic theory: a city. Look at this, he was saying. This is what reforms can do. Now imagine doing it everywhere.

The Southern Tour was a political masterstroke. By making his case publicly and in person, in locations whose success was undeniable, Deng created facts on the ground that conservative critics could not refute. Economic reform — which had been under intellectual attack since Tiananmen — was dramatically reinvigorated. The 14th Party Congress of October 1992 formally endorsed the “socialist market economy” as China’s official economic model — the first formal ideological acknowledgment that markets could coexist with socialism in China’s particular version of both.

The tour was also Deng’s final major public political act. He would live until 1997, long enough to reclaim Hong Kong from Britain, but the Southern Tour was his last great intervention in Chinese economic policy. He had, in 35 days, reset the trajectory of the world’s largest developing country. Not bad for a retired 88-year-old.

Zhu Rongji: The Most Important Economist You’ve Never Heard Of

If Deng Xiaoping was China’s reformist philosopher-king, Zhu Rongji was his most consequential implementer — the economic surgeon who performed the most painful and most necessary operations on China’s body politic with surgical precision and complete indifference to either popularity or political comfort.

Zhu became Vice Premier in 1991 and Premier in 1998. He was, by any assessment, among the most remarkable economic policymakers of the 20th century: intellectually brilliant, personally incorruptible (in a system saturated with corruption, this was remarkable), administratively ferocious, and willing to take decisions that he knew would make him deeply unpopular.

His biggest surgery: the State-Owned Enterprise (SOE) reform.

When Zhu took over as Premier, China’s state enterprise sector was, economically, a disaster. China had approximately 100,000 state enterprises — factories, mines, construction companies, stores — that had been built during the Mao era to fulfil production plans rather than to earn profits. Most were hopelessly inefficient by any market standard: they produced goods nobody wanted at costs that made them permanently loss-making; they maintained massive workforces as a form of social welfare (the “iron rice bowl” — the guaranteed job with housing and healthcare that SOEs provided); and they survived only because state banks were required to lend them money regardless of creditworthiness.

Zhu’s decision: the SOEs had to be reformed, merged, privatized, or closed. The state would retain its position in “strategic” industries (defense, energy, telecoms, banking) while releasing everything else. The inevitable result: 40–50 million SOE workers were laid off between 1995 and 2002 — the largest single wave of industrial unemployment in history.

The social consequences were real and severe: communities built around single factory complexes were devastated; workers in their 40s and 50s who had known only the iron rice bowl found themselves unemployable in the market economy that had replaced it. The Northeast of China — the old industrial heartland of Mao-era heavy industry — became China’s rust belt, scarred by factory closures and unemployment in ways that the gleaming prosperity of coastal cities did not reflect.

But the surgery saved the patient. The SOEs that survived the reform process were leaner, better managed, and eventually profitable. The banking system, cleaned of the bad loans that SOE lending had produced (the state recapitalized the banks and transferred the bad loans to asset management companies — a solution that deferred rather than eliminated the problem, but deferred it long enough for growth to absorb the cost), was put on a more sustainable financial foundation. The private sector — freed from competing with permanently subsidized state enterprises for credit, land, and labour — could now grow.

Zhu’s second surgery: WTO accession.

When Zhu Rongji flew to Washington in April 1999 to negotiate China’s entry into the World Trade Organization, he made, on his own authority, concessions that surprised American negotiators and infuriated Chinese conservatives: opening China’s agriculture to American imports, allowing foreign companies into Chinese financial services, committing to the intellectual property standards the US trade agenda required. The concessions were real and substantial.

They were also strategic: Zhu understood that WTO rules would bind the hands of protectionist and interventionist elements inside China as much as they bound China’s trade partners. If the WTO required China to allow foreign financial services competition, it would be much harder for domestic interest groups to block it. External commitments, accepted publicly, could force the internal reforms that domestic politics might otherwise prevent. It was external credibility as internal reform catalyst — a strategy that worked.

China formally acceded to the WTO on December 11, 2001 — the date that unleashed the most explosive manufacturing expansion of the modern era.

WTO Accession: The Starter Pistol for the World’s Factory

The economic effects of WTO accession were immediate and enormous. Chinese manufacturers could now export to the United States, Europe, and the rest of the WTO’s 140+ member countries without the uncertainty of annual congressional reviews (the US had previously had to renew China’s most-favored-nation trade status annually, creating investment uncertainty). Global multinationals could now invest in China with greater legal certainty about repatriation of profits and dispute resolution.

The result was a tsunami of foreign direct investment. Foreign invested enterprises in China grew from $11 billion in 1990 to $60 billion in 2004 to over $140 billion by 2013. Factories opened across the Pearl River Delta, the Yangtze River Delta, and eventually into the interior provinces. Every major global brand — Apple, Nike, Gap, Dell, Sony, Samsung — either set up its own manufacturing operations in China or contracted Chinese manufacturers to supply its goods.

In 2000, China’s share of global manufacturing was approximately 6%. By 2020, it was approximately 28.7% — meaning that more than one in four manufactured goods in the world was made in China. For context: at its postwar industrial peak, the United States’ share of global manufacturing was approximately 28%. China achieved the same manufacturing dominance — starting from near zero — in approximately 25 years. And it did so at a scale that encompasses a population 4 times larger than the United States.

CHAPTER 5 — The World’s Factory: How China Conquered Global Manufacturing (2001–2010)

The Perfect Storm of Comparative Advantage

Economics has a concept called comparative advantage: countries gain by specializing in what they can produce relatively more efficiently, trading for everything else. China in 2001 had comparative advantage in manufacturing of a breadth and depth that no country in history had possessed:

Labour cost advantage: An agricultural worker in rural Sichuan or Hunan province could earn perhaps 2,000–3,000 yuan per year from farming. A factory wage in Guangdong province, even at the lowest levels, was 8,000–15,000 yuan per year — a difference substantial enough to represent a genuine, life-changing improvement. This meant China had an effectively inexhaustible supply of workers willing to migrate to factory cities for wages that, by global standards, were extraordinarily low, but by their own previous experience, were transformational.

Scale: China’s pool of available rural labour was, at its peak, approximately 800 million people — more than the entire population of Europe and North America combined. No other country could offer supply chains the scale certainty that China could: “Can you make 10 million units? Can you scale to 50 million units?” The answer in China was invariably yes.

Infrastructure investment: The Chinese state had, unlike most developing countries, the political capacity and the fiscal resources to build the infrastructure that manufacturing requires: deep-water ports to handle container ships, highways connecting factory zones to ports, electricity generation to power factories, and eventually the world’s most extensive high-speed rail network. This infrastructure was often built before it was needed — what the Chinese call “building roads before people use them” — rather than after.

Industrial clustering: Once manufacturing concentration began in specific regions, it created self-reinforcing gravitational fields. The Pearl River Delta became an electronics cluster because electronics manufacturers needed component suppliers near them; the supplier concentration attracted more manufacturers; which attracted more suppliers. Eventually, you could source virtually any component within 50 kilometres of any Pearl River Delta factory — a supply chain ecosystem that had no equivalent anywhere in the world.

The Greatest Migration in Human History

Between approximately 1990 and 2010, an estimated 250–300 million people moved from China’s rural interior to its coastal and urban manufacturing zones — the largest migration of people in human history, accomplished within a single generation, without precedent in scale or speed.

To understand what this meant: 300 million people is the entire population of the United States. Imagine every single person in the US relocating across the country in 20 years. In China, this happened: rural farmers from Sichuan, Henan, Hunan, and Anhui provinces packed their belongings and traveled — by train, by bus, by any available means — to the factory cities of Guangdong, Zhejiang, Jiangsu, and Fujian.

The annual Spring Festival migration — when tens of millions of migrant workers make the journey home for the Chinese New Year holiday — became the largest annual human movement on Earth, with the railway system carrying hundreds of millions of passengers in a two-week period. The images: packed platforms, overnight train carriages with every square centimeter occupied, people carrying enormous sacks of possessions, journeys of 30+ hours.

These migrants — called nónmíngōng (農民工), literally “farmer workers” — were the human foundation of China’s manufacturing miracle. They lived, often, in dormitories within or adjacent to factory compounds: 8–12 people to a room, shared bathrooms, communal canteens. They worked 12-hour shifts, 6–7 days per week, in conditions that ranged (depending on the factory and period) from adequate to deeply uncomfortable to genuinely dangerous. They sent remittances home that built new houses in their home villages and paid for their children’s education.

Their sacrifice was real. But so was their choice: they came because the factory wage was better than the farm, and because the alternative was rural poverty with no visible exit. This is the essential paradox of industrialization’s early stages everywhere in history — from the British cotton mills of the 1790s to the textile factories of South Korea in the 1960s to China’s electronics assembly lines in the 2000s. The conditions are objectively difficult; the alternative was considered worse by the people making the choice.

Apple, Nike, and Walmart: How Global Supply Chains Rebuilt Around China

The WTO accession and the migration gave China the workforce. What gave that workforce global-scale demand was the restructuring of supply chains by the world’s most powerful companies.

Apple is the most famous example. When Steve Jobs returned to Apple in 1997 and began developing the iPod (2001), iPhone (2007), and iPad (2010), he made a fundamental decision: Apple would design products in California and manufacture them in China. The manufacturing partner he chose — Foxconn, the Taiwanese-owned contract manufacturer with its massive plants in Shenzhen and elsewhere — could do something no American manufacturer could: assemble a million units of a new product within weeks of final design approval, with the workforce flexibility to run three shifts 24 hours a day and the supply chain density to source virtually every component from nearby factories.

The iPhone’s components illustrate China’s supply chain integration: the display from South Korea (Samsung, LG) or Japan (Sharp), the processor designed in California but fabricated in Taiwan (TSMC), the memory from South Korea or China, the camera modules from Japan, but assembled at Foxconn’s Zhengzhou plant in Henan province — where 200,000 workers, living in company dormitories, assembled up to 500,000 iPhones per day at peak production. Every other major electronics brand operated through similar structures.

Walmart’s US supply chain transformation is equally illuminating. In 1990, Walmart sourced approximately 6% of its goods from China. By 2005, the estimate was 70–80% of its general merchandise. Walmart’s low-price model — its entire competitive advantage — was dependent on Chinese manufacturing costs. The company effectively acted as a demand aggregator, translating American consumer demand for cheap goods into Chinese manufacturing orders at extraordinary scale. University of California researchers estimated that Walmart alone accounted for approximately $27 billion per year in Chinese exports — if it were a country, it would have been China’s 8th largest trading partner.

The Infrastructure Blitz That Made It All Possible

The manufacturing miracle required infrastructure, and the Chinese state delivered infrastructure at a speed and scale that had no precedent in economic history.

Ports: Containerized shipping — first developed in the 1950s, transformative for global trade from the 1970s — is the physical mechanism by which Chinese factories supply global markets. China built or expanded ports at extraordinary pace: Shanghai became the world’s largest container port in 2005 and has maintained that position every year since. Shenzhen’s Yantian terminal, Guangzhou’s Nansha, Ningbo-Zhoushan — China has seven of the world’s top ten container ports, a concentration that reflects not just the scale of Chinese manufacturing but the deliberate state investment in the infrastructure of global trade.

The highway network: In 1990, China had virtually no expressways — modern, limited-access highways comparable to Germany’s Autobahn or America’s Interstate system. By 2020, China had 150,000 kilometres of expressway — the world’s largest expressway network, larger than the US Interstate system that took 60 years to build. China built it in 25 years, using state financing, eminent domain acquisition, and a construction industry that mobilized millions of workers simultaneously.

Electricity generation: Manufacturing requires electricity. Between 2000 and 2020, China added more electricity generation capacity than the United States had built in its entire history. By 2020, China’s total installed electricity capacity exceeded 2,200 gigawatts — more than the US and European Union combined. The speed was staggering: commissioning a coal-fired power plant in China took approximately 18–24 months; in the US, it takes 6–10 years.

The India comparison: India in 2001 had a similar population to China’s manufacturing workforce, similar wage levels, and similar potential labour surplus. What it lacked was precisely what China had: the state capacity to build infrastructure ahead of demand, the land acquisition authority to assemble large industrial zones quickly, and the political focus on manufacturing as the primary development strategy. India’s electricity blackouts continued; its ports remained congested; its roads were inadequate. The infrastructure gap between the two nations in 2001 was probably the single most important reason why India did not become the world’s factory — and China did.

The 2008 Financial Crisis: China’s Moment and China’s Gift

When the US financial system collapsed in 2008, triggering the worst global recession since the Great Depression, a question with no obvious answer arose: without American consumer demand, who would buy Chinese factories’ output?

The Chinese government’s answer was swift, decisive, and economically controversial: a stimulus package of 4 trillion yuan ($586 billion) — approximately 13% of China’s GDP — deployed within months of the crisis. The package went primarily into infrastructure: an extraordinary acceleration of high-speed rail construction, urban subway systems, highways, airports, and social housing.

The high-speed rail story is remarkable in itself. China’s first high-speed rail line opened in 2008 (Beijing-Tianjin). By 2023, China had approximately 42,000 kilometres of high-speed rail — more than the rest of the world combined. A network that would connect every major Chinese city with journey times competitive with flying (city center to city center, when airport transit time is considered) was built in 15 years. The construction employed hundreds of thousands of workers, created demand for Chinese steel, cement, and engineering, and provided infrastructure that will be productive for generations.

The stimulus worked, in the narrow sense: China’s growth barely dipped below 8% even during the global recession, providing demand that cushioned the global economy’s collapse. But it also planted the seeds of the debt problems — the overextended real estate sector, the indebted local governments, the excess industrial capacity — that would haunt China’s economy in subsequent decades.

CHAPTER 6 — The Technology Leap: From “Made in China” to “Invented in China” (2010–Present)

The Graduation Problem

For the first 30 years of China’s manufacturing boom, the dominant critique was simple: China made things, but it didn’t invent things. It assembled the iPhone, but Apple designed it in California. It produced Nikes, but Nike designed them in Oregon. Its factories manufactured the world’s goods; its engineers and scientists were, largely, implementing other people’s innovations.

This was, by the standards of historical industrialization, exactly right for this stage: Britain in the 18th century imported French textile techniques, American manufacturers in the 19th century imported British industrial technology, Japan in the 1950s and 1960s licensed American and European technology. Technology transfer and imitation are the natural first phase of catch-up industrialization. The question is: what comes next? Can you move from imitator to innovator?

China’s answer, under Xi Jinping, who came to power in 2012 and consolidated authority more completely than any Chinese leader since Mao, was an explicit industrial policy of extraordinary ambition: “Made in China 2025” — a 10-year plan to make China dominant in ten “next economy” sectors: artificial intelligence, semiconductors, electric vehicles, aerospace, robotics, biopharmaceuticals, new materials, power equipment, agricultural equipment, and marine engineering.

The plan was remarkable for its candor: it explicitly targeted specific market share goals for Chinese companies in each sector, both domestically and globally. China would capture 70% of its domestic market in ten core high-tech sectors by 2025. The plan was so explicit about China’s ambitions that, when foreign governments discovered it (the English translation circulated widely only after 2018), it caused considerable alarm — because it made obvious what China’s state was attempting to do.

The New Chinese Tech Champions

Huawei is the most dramatic case study. Founded in 1987 by Ren Zhengfei — a former People’s Liberation Army engineer — with 21,000 yuan ($5,000) in startup capital in the living room of an apartment in Shenzhen, Huawei has grown into the world’s largest telecommunications equipment company and one of the most consequential technology companies on Earth.

Huawei’s rise tracks China’s technological development with unusual precision: it began by reverse-engineering foreign telecommunications equipment and selling it at lower prices to rural Chinese telecoms companies that the foreign firms considered too small and too difficult. It invested heavily in R&D — far more, as a percentage of revenue, than most of its Western competitors — and over two decades built genuine engineering capabilities. By the 2010s, it was competing directly with Ericsson and Nokia in the global market for telecoms infrastructure, winning contracts in Europe, Africa, Asia, and Latin America on the basis of competitive pricing and genuine technical quality.

Its 5G infrastructure capability — the technology for next-generation mobile networks that will underpin everything from autonomous vehicles to smart factories — became the central flashpoint of the US-China technology war. The US government, concluding that Huawei’s products posed espionage risks (Huawei disputed this evidence vigorously), imposed a sweeping ban on sales of US-designed semiconductor components to Huawei in 2020. The ban was devastating: Huawei’s premium smartphone business essentially collapsed because it could no longer access the chips its phones required.

But Huawei’s response — doubling its investment in chip design and working with Chinese chip foundries to develop domestic alternatives — illustrates the broader pattern of US technology sanctions inadvertently accelerating China’s technology self-sufficiency agenda. Every component blocked by US export controls created a Chinese government mandate (and substantial state subsidies) to develop a domestic alternative.

CATL (Contemporary Amperex Technology): founded in 2011 by Zeng Yuqun in Ningde, Fujian province, CATL has become the world’s largest electric vehicle battery manufacturer — supplying Tesla, BMW, Volkswagen, Toyota, and dozens of other automakers globally as well as Chinese brands. Its market share in global EV batteries was approximately 37% in 2023. CATL’s success represents the most significant case of China moving from fast-follower to genuine global leader in a strategically critical technology — one that will determine the economics of transportation for the rest of the 21st century.

BYD (Build Your Dream): Warren Buffett’s Berkshire Hathaway bought a 10% stake in BYD in 2008 — when it was a Chinese automaker nobody outside China had heard of. By 2023, BYD had overtaken Tesla in quarterly global electric vehicle sales. The company — founded by Wan Gang in 1995 as a rechargeable battery manufacturer — made the strategic pivot to electric vehicles in the early 2000s and has executed it with engineering discipline and manufacturing efficiency that the global automotive industry is still processing. BYD makes everything in-house: batteries, electric motors, chips, body panels. Its vertical integration gives it a cost advantage that is proving deeply disruptive to established automotive companies.

DJI (Da-Jiang Innovations): Founded by Frank Wang in 2006 in Shenzhen, DJI controls approximately 70% of the global consumer drone market — in a product category it essentially created. Wang, a Hong Kong University of Science and Technology student who was obsessed with remote-controlled helicopters, built a company whose physical product is the most visible example in everyday life of Chinese technological leadership. Military, industrial, and consumer drone technology is now one of the areas where China’s technological edge over the United States is not questioned by serious analysts.

Belt and Road: China Exports Its Industrial Model

The Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), announced by Xi Jinping in 2013, is the largest infrastructure investment programme in history — China financing and often building railways, ports, roads, power plants, and telecommunications networks across 140+ countries, primarily in Asia, Africa, and Latin America.

The BRI is genuinely complex and genuinely controversial. On one reading, it is a mechanism for China to: - Secure raw material supply chains (African mineral resources, Middle Eastern oil, Central Asian gas) - Create export markets for Chinese construction companies and manufacturing - Build strategic port positions across global shipping lanes (the “String of Pearls” concern in the Indian Ocean) - Export China’s model of infrastructure-led development to the developing world

On another reading, it is exactly what it claims to be: a development finance initiative that provides infrastructure that developing countries cannot finance themselves, addressing a global infrastructure gap that the World Bank and IMF have chronically underfunded.

Both readings are partially correct. The BRI has built real infrastructure (the railway from Mombasa to Nairobi, the Colombo port, the Gwadar port in Pakistan, the Laos-China railway) that has genuine economic value. It has also created debt situations in countries (Sri Lanka, Zambia, Pakistan) where the terms proved more burdensome than initially understood. The Hambantota port in Sri Lanka — leased to a Chinese company for 99 years after Sri Lanka could not service its debt — has become the defining Western cautionary tale about BRI’s strategic agenda.

The Chip War: The Battle for Technological Destiny

In October 2022, the Biden administration imposed the most sweeping export restrictions on semiconductor technology in American history — effectively cutting off China’s access to: - Advanced semiconductor manufacturing equipment from American companies (Applied Materials, KLA, Lam Research) - The software tools used to design chips (Synopsys, Cadence, Mentor) - Chips made anywhere in the world using American technology - American-trained or -nationality semiconductor engineers from working in China

The stated rationale: preventing China from developing the advanced AI chips that the US believed would provide military and economic advantages. The unstated calculation: if China cannot access the most advanced chips, it cannot develop the most advanced AI, and the US can maintain technological leadership.

China’s response: a state-funded crash programme to develop domestic alternatives at every level of the semiconductor supply chain — chip design tools (EDA), equipment for chip fabrication, and chip fabrication itself. The investments are enormous (estimates of $50–100 billion in state chip subsidies over 5 years), the timeline is compressed, and the ceiling imposed by physics and fundamental engineering constraints on how far this strategy can advance in the short term is real. Advanced chip fabrication — the kind required for AI training and the most sophisticated electronics — requires equipment (specifically the extreme ultraviolet lithography machines made exclusively by ASML in the Netherlands) that China cannot currently reproduce.

The chip war represents the most important strategic contest in the global economy’s immediate future: whether China’s manufacturing-to-innovation transition can cross the semiconductor barrier, or whether US technology controls can maintain a technological ceiling above which China’s industrial ambitions cannot rise.

CHAPTER 7 — The Human Cost: What the Miracle Demanded

The Things the Growth Statistics Don’t Measure

GDP per capita grew from $200 to $13,000. 850 million people were lifted out of extreme poverty. Life expectancy rose from 65 to 78. These are extraordinary achievements. They are also incomplete descriptions of what China’s transformation cost — and who paid that cost.

An honest account of China’s manufacturing miracle requires acknowledging, without euphemism, the price that was paid in environmental destruction, labor exploitation, social displacement, and political repression.

The Environmental Catastrophe

China’s industrialization was powered predominantly by coal — cheap, domestically abundant, and climatically catastrophic. At its 2013 peak, China was burning approximately 4 billion tonnes of coal per year — more than the rest of the world combined. The result: air quality in China’s industrial cities became, in the worst years, genuinely dangerous to health. The PM2.5 particulate pollution in Beijing routinely reached levels 50–100 times the World Health Organization’s recommended maximum. The Chinese government coined the term “airpocalypse” without apparent irony. Schools were closed on high-pollution days. Residents of Beijing bought imported air purifiers and kept children indoors.

The water pollution was equally severe. A 2010 survey found that approximately 60% of China’s underground water and a significant proportion of its surface water were contaminated beyond usable levels — by industrial runoff from factories, by heavy metals from mining and smelting operations, by agricultural chemicals, and by inadequately treated sewage. Villages along China’s rivers developed what the Chinese medical literature clinically called “cancer clusters” — communities where cancer rates were measurably elevated by contaminated water. Their residents called them 癌症村 (àizhèng cūn): cancer villages.

China’s farmland — the ultimate basis of any agricultural civilization’s survival — was contaminated at alarming scale. A 2014 government survey found that approximately 19% of China’s farmland was contaminated with heavy metals, primarily cadmium (from mining and smelting), arsenic (from pesticides and mining), and lead (from manufacturing). The contamination was concentrated in exactly the regions — around manufacturing zones and mining areas — where economic activity had been most intense.

The environmental reckoning began in earnest around 2015, when Xi Jinping’s government declared a “war on pollution” that was genuinely serious in its implementation: factory closures, coal plant shutdowns, emission standards that were actually enforced, and the largest single investment in renewable energy in history. China’s air quality measurably improved after 2015 — Beijing’s PM2.5 fell by approximately 50% between 2013 and 2020. Its investment in solar and wind power went from marginal to dominant: China installs more solar capacity each year than the entire cumulative US solar base. But the environmental debt of three decades of prioritizing growth over environmental protection will take generations to repay.

The Labor Story China Preferred Not to Tell

Standing at a Foxconn factory gate at 6 AM to watch the shift change is one of the more striking experiences available in modern China: hundreds of young men and women, many barely older than 18, streaming out of an enormous building after 12 hours of repetitive assembly work, heading back to dormitories to sleep before the next shift. Many had come from provinces a thousand kilometres away. Many were doing work that required them to perform the same motion — inserting a component, pressing a button, placing a screen — thousands of times per shift.

The work was physically unglamorous and economically rational. But the conditions raised real questions.

Working hours: 60–72 hours per week was common in China’s electronics and garment factories — hours that would be illegal in any developed country and that create genuine health and safety risks. The productivity demanded was often extreme: during iPhone launch periods, Foxconn workers were required to produce 3,000+ assembled units per shift.

Dormitory living: The factory dormitory is China manufacturing’s distinctive social institution — a system of housing workers within or adjacent to factory compounds that simultaneously solves the workers’ housing problem (they have accommodation without the cost or difficulty of finding housing in unfamiliar cities) and gives the factory total control over workers’ time and movement. The migration experience in China has, for millions of workers, meant not moving to a city but moving to a factory — a complete world of work, sleep, and limited recreation within a company-managed compound.

The Foxconn suicide cluster: In 2010, fourteen workers at Foxconn’s Shenzhen campus died by suicide — jumping from the factory dormitory buildings. The suicides attracted global attention to factory conditions in ways that press releases about working conditions and CSR reports never had. Foxconn’s response — installing anti-suicide nets on its buildings’ ledges, raising wages 30%, creating psychological counselling services — was simultaneously a genuine response to a crisis and a reminder that the global supply chain’s cost optimization had created conditions of psychological pressure that were, for some workers, intolerable.

The absence of independent unions: China’s only legal trade union is the All-China Federation of Trade Unions (ACFTU) — a state-controlled body that is explicitly not an independent worker organization but rather a transmission mechanism for Party policy to the workplace. Independent union organizing is illegal in China. Workers who attempt to organize without ACFTU authorization risk detention. This prevents the collective bargaining that, historically, has been the primary mechanism through which industrial workers in other countries converted productivity gains into wage increases and improved conditions. China’s manufacturing workers are more productive than workers in almost any country on Earth; whether they have received an adequate share of the value they create is an ongoing question without a clear answer in a system that prohibits independent worker organization.

The Hukou System: Migration Without Belonging

The hukou (户口) system — China’s household registration system, inherited from the 1950s — is in some ways the single institutional feature most responsible for China’s distinctive form of inequality.

Every Chinese citizen has a hukou registered in a specific location, typically their birthplace. Access to social services — public schools, healthcare through the local health insurance system, pension contributions — is tied to the hukou location. A migrant worker from Sichuan who moves to Shenzhen for factory work retains a Sichuan hukou; she can work in Shenzhen, but her children cannot attend Shenzhen’s public schools (because school places are allocated to Shenzhen hukou holders), she cannot access Shenzhen’s health insurance system, and she cannot receive a Shenzhen pension. She contributes tax revenue to Shenzhen’s government through her work; she receives social services from Sichuan’s government, which she no longer lives in.

The consequences for China’s 280 million internal migrants: a system of partial citizenship in the cities where they actually live and work. Their children either stay in the sending province (cared for by grandparents — China’s estimated 60–80 million “left-behind children” who grow up without their parents), or accompany their parents and attend overcrowded and under-resourced migrant schools, or return to the sending province for secondary education when urban school access proves impossible.

The hukou system served a purpose — controlling the pace of urbanization, preventing the massive informal settlement that accompanied industrialization in South Asia and Latin America — but at the cost of creating a two-tier citizenship in which the people whose labor built China’s coastal cities were systematically excluded from the full benefits of the urban prosperity they created.

The Poverty Reduction That Was Real — and Extraordinary

And yet: the 850 million people lifted out of extreme poverty between 1978 and 2020, as measured by the World Bank’s $1.90/day threshold (adjusted for purchasing power parity), is a fact that cannot be diminished by any recitation of the costs. No event in human history has improved the material welfare of so many people so rapidly.

The rural family in Sichuan that was too poor to buy shoes in 1975 lives, in 2023, in a house with running water, a refrigerator, a smartphone, and a child in university. This is not a hypothesis or an average — it is the lived experience of hundreds of millions of real people whose lives were genuinely transformed by China’s growth. The infant mortality rate fell from 50 per 1,000 births in 1978 to 5 per 1,000 in 2023. Child malnutrition — which stunted the physical and cognitive development of millions of Chinese children in the 1960s and 1970s — has been essentially eliminated.

The World Bank, which has studied China’s poverty reduction comprehensively, describes it without qualification as “the greatest poverty reduction in the history of poverty reduction.” This is not Chinese government propaganda; it is the assessment of international development economists who have spent their careers trying to understand why poverty reduction succeeds and fails.

The human costs of China’s miracle were real. So was the miracle.

MAO ZEDONG (1893–1976): The Founding Trauma and the Iron Foundation

Mao Zedong is impossible. He is too large to fit in any characterization that does justice to both his achievement and his crimes, and too central to China’s story to be avoided.

He united a country that had been torn apart by 110 years of humiliation, civil war, warlordism, and foreign invasion — an achievement that required extraordinary will, military genius, political cunning, and a genuine capacity to inspire. The Long March (1934–35), in which the Communist forces retreated 8,000 kilometres over some of the most difficult terrain in Asia while under attack by Nationalist forces, losing perhaps 90% of their starting numbers but emerging with a cadre of battle-hardened survivors and a mythology of heroic endurance that still echoes in Chinese political culture, was an achievement of genuine leadership.

He also presided over one of the worst man-made famines in history (the Great Leap Forward), launched a political campaign (the Cultural Revolution) that destroyed the careers, freedom, and often lives of millions of China’s best-educated people, and maintained a cult of personality that would have made Stalin uncomfortable. The official Chinese judgment — 70% right, 30% wrong — is obviously a political formula rather than a historical assessment, but the distribution of credit and error is less important than understanding the nature of each.

What Mao left behind: a unified nation, a national narrative of humiliation and restoration that provided the emotional fuel for everything that followed, the PLA (People’s Liberation Army) as an institution of national unification, basic literacy at scale, an industrial base (however inefficient), and a political system whose authoritarianism created both the pathologies of the Mao era and the capacity for the Deng era’s top-down reform direction. Both things are simultaneously true, which is what makes Mao so impossible.

DENG XIAOPING (1904–1997): The Pragmatic Revolutionary

Deng survived more than most people could: two political purges, years of factory exile, the Cultural Revolution’s destruction of his life’s work, his son’s permanent disability from Red Guard violence. He emerged from all of it not embittered (or not only embittered) but possessed of a clarity about what mattered — economic results — and a ruthlessness about what didn’t — ideological consistency.

His management style was unusual for a communist leader: he preferred to work through others rather than fronting everything himself, he tolerated policy disagreement within the permissible range, and he had an almost American instinct for letting experiments run before scaling them. He watched Xiaogang village’s private farming produce extraordinary results before declaring the Household Responsibility System national policy. He watched Shenzhen’s transformation before calling critics of the SEZs wrong. He crossed rivers by feeling for stones.

His contradictions: the man who gave China’s farmers autonomy over their land also ordered tanks into Tiananmen Square. The man who told China “to get rich is glorious” also maintained an authoritarian party system that prohibited political contestation. His resolution of this contradiction — that economic freedom and political control could coexist indefinitely — has been China’s defining institutional experiment. Whether it will prove sustainable in the long run is the most important unanswered question in contemporary political science.

His death in 1997, at 92, came in time for him to see Hong Kong returned to China (July 1, 1997), fulfilling the reversal of what he considered the most symbolically important of the colonial humiliations. He died, arguably, as the most consequential human being of the 20th century in terms of the number of lives transformed by his decisions — a case that can be made even against Churchill, FDR, or Einstein.

ZHAO ZIYANG (1919–2005): The Road Not Taken

Zhao Ziyang is China’s great political tragedy — the reformer whose vision of political liberalization accompanying economic liberalization was defeated by Tiananmen and who spent the last 16 years of his life under house arrest in Beijing, producing secret memoirs by recording his thoughts on cassette tapes that were smuggled out of China after his death and published as Prisoner of the State.

Zhao was Deng’s most trusted economic reformer: as Premier from 1980 to 1987 and then as General Secretary from 1987 to 1989, he oversaw the most dynamic years of China’s early reform period, championed the TVEs and price liberalization, and managed the difficult politics of rapid economic change within the party system. His instinct was consistently toward greater market freedom, greater transparency, and eventually (by 1989) toward political reform that might accompany and sustain economic reform.

His fatal moment came on May 19, 1989, when he visited the hunger-striking students in Tiananmen Square. Visibly moved, his voice breaking, he told them: “We have come too late.” He was the last senior Chinese Communist Party leader to appear in Tiananmen Square before the crackdown. He opposed the imposition of martial law; Deng overruled him. He was stripped of all positions and placed under house arrest, where he remained until his death.

Zhao’s secret memoirs argue that China’s development required political reform as its complement — that without democratizing elements, the market economy would generate corruption, inequality, and instability that would eventually undermine the entire project. The subsequent decades of China’s experience — the Petrobras-scale corruption of Chinese officials, the extraordinary inequality, the political tensions — give his argument some retrospective force. But Beijing’s official verdict remains that he made “serious mistakes” in 1989. His name cannot be searched on China’s internet.

ZHU RONGJI (1928–): The Iron Economic Surgeon

On his last day as Premier, at the conclusion of a press conference in March 2003, Zhu Rongji was asked what regrets he had. He paused, then said: “I did not accomplish enough, and I made mistakes.” His eyes filled with tears.

It was characteristically direct — Zhu was famous for a bluntness that was extraordinary by the standards of Communist Party leaders, who typically speak only in carefully formulated official language. “I prepared a hundred coffins,” he said when he launched his anti-corruption drive in the late 1990s, “ninety-nine for officials, and one for myself.” He meant it. His anti-corruption efforts imprisoned or executed dozens of senior officials, including some with political connections that might have protected them from less determined prosecutors.

The SOE reform he implemented between 1995 and 2001 — restructuring, merging, or closing thousands of state enterprises, releasing tens of millions of workers from the iron rice bowl into a market economy for which many were completely unprepared — was economically necessary and humanely difficult. The workers who lost their guaranteed jobs often had nowhere to go. Zhu understood this; he simultaneously expanded social safety net provisions as he dismantled the factory-based welfare system. It was not adequate. It was more than nothing.

His WTO negotiating concessions — made on his own authority, without full cabinet consultation, knowing they would be attacked domestically — reflected a strategic clarity about where China needed to go that was ahead of the political consensus. He was right; China’s subsequent export boom validated every concession he made.

He is one of the few Chinese political figures of the reform era who commands genuine admiration across political lines — admired by liberals for his economic management and anti-corruption commitment, respected by conservatives for his patriotism and policy results. His memoir, published in Chinese, reveals a man who cared deeply about doing the job well and was willing to accept the political costs of hard decisions.

JIANG ZEMIN (1926–2022): The Consolidator

Jiang Zemin was the man nobody expected to lead China. He was appointed General Secretary in 1989, in the immediate aftermath of Tiananmen, as a compromise choice — relatively moderate, not associated with either the hardliners or the reformers. He spent his 13 years in power (1989–2002) surprising everyone by being more effective than his un-charismatic exterior suggested.

His most important ideological innovation: the “Three Represents” doctrine, which asserted that the Communist Party represented not just (as Mao had insisted) the working class and peasantry, but also “advanced productive forces” (meaning entrepreneurs and business people) and “advanced culture.” This formulation — which would have been ideologically incoherent in Mao’s taxonomy — was the explicit opening of the Party to the capitalist class that China’s economic development had created. Jiang was formalizing what Deng’s pragmatism had already produced: a party that governed a capitalist economy without officially acknowledging it was governing a capitalist economy.

He also oversaw the return of Hong Kong (1997) and Macao (1999) — two of the most symbolically significant moments of national restoration in the Communist era — and China’s accession to the WTO. His era was not without problems (corruption under his state capitalism model was extensive, inequality grew rapidly), but it produced extraordinary growth rates and solidified China’s position in the global economy.

HU JINTAO (1942–): The Quiet Steward

Hu Jintao governed China from 2002 to 2012 — the decade of China’s most explosive export-led growth, the WTO boom years, the 2008 Olympics, the financial crisis stimulus, and China surpassing Japan as the world’s second-largest economy in 2010.

He was, by the standards of his predecessors and successor, unremarkable. His political philosophy — the “scientific outlook on development” and the “harmonious society” doctrine — had the quality of committee-drafted slogans rather than genuine intellectual content. He gave few interviews, showed little personal charisma, and was widely described by foreign dignitaries who met him as wooden and formulaic in conversation.

What he was: an administrator of considerable competence who kept the machine running during years when the machine ran very well because the external conditions (the global commodity boom, the WTO-enabled export surge, the infrastructure investment multiplier) were favorable. He also presided over a significant expansion of social spending — the expansion of rural health insurance, the New Cooperative Medical Scheme — that brought basic healthcare to hundreds of millions of rural Chinese who had previously had no coverage.

His failure: governance, particularly corruption and spatial inequality. Under Hu, the nexus of local government official, state enterprise, and real estate developer that produced China’s extraordinary urban construction boom also produced extraordinary corruption and the credit expansion that seeded the subsequent real estate crisis. Hu’s era of “stability” maintained growth by deferring the institutional reforms that accumulated problems would eventually require.

XI JINPING (1953–): The Centralizer

Xi Jinping came to power in 2012 with a biography that, within the logic of Chinese Communist Party elite politics, was close to impeccable: princeling (his father Xi Zhongxun was a veteran revolutionary and senior Party official), survivor of the Cultural Revolution (sent to rural Shaanxi for seven years of “re-education,” where he reportedly read by candlelight and practiced public speaking to villagers), veteran of multiple provincial governorships, and patient ascender through the party hierarchy. He had waited his turn and had reached the summit with more broad-based support among the party elite than any leader since Deng.

What he has done with that power is the most consequential set of decisions in Chinese governance since Deng’s 1978 reforms — and the most contested. His elimination of term limits in 2018 (which would have required him to step down in 2022 after two five-year terms) breached what had been the most important institutional innovation of the post-Mao era, establishing the principle that party leadership was bounded and would rotate. His consolidation of power over the security services, military, economy, and ideology into his personal authority creates a concentration that, many political scientists argue, will reduce the quality of decision-making by eliminating the pluralism through which previous Chinese leaders received accurate information about the economy and society.

His specific economic interventions: the crackdown on Alibaba and tech companies generally (2020–2021), the campaign against private tutoring companies, the “common prosperity” doctrine that seems to signal discomfort with the private wealth accumulation that Deng had encouraged, the assertion of security concerns over efficiency concerns in economic policymaking. Each individual intervention has some defensible rationale; collectively, they signal a rebalancing in the party’s implicit contract with private sector entrepreneurs that introduces uncertainty into China’s economic future.

His response to COVID-19 — initially zero-COVID suppression (which was effective health policy) and eventually a sudden reversal in December 2022 under public pressure (which was poorly managed) — demonstrated both the capacity and the limitation of the Chinese system under centralized authority: capable of implementing extraordinary lock-downs but resistant to course-correction until public pressure forced a chaotic reversal.

Whether Xi’s centralization will ultimately constrain China’s innovative capacity — whether the entrepreneurs, scientists, and engineers whose creativity is driving China’s technology transition will operate less freely, less confidently, with less tolerance for unconventional thinking under a system asserting Party control ever more directly over civil and economic life — is the most important open question about China’s economic future.

JACK MA (1964–): The Symbol and the Cautionary Tale

Jack Ma — Mǎ Yún — is the most globally recognizable face of Chinese capitalism, and his recent experiences have made him the most instructive cautionary tale about the relationship between private wealth and Party power in Xi Jinping’s China.

He was born in Hangzhou, the son of musical performers, failed the university entrance exam twice, was rejected by Harvard ten times (he tells this story himself with cheerful precision), and was among the first wave of Chinese entrepreneurs who built companies using the internet — a technology that China’s state simultaneously tolerated, monitored, and eventually attempted to control.

Alibaba, which he founded in 1999 in his Hangzhou apartment, became China’s largest e-commerce platform and one of the world’s most valuable companies, making Jack Ma by 2014 the richest person in China. Ant Group, Alibaba’s financial services spinoff, had developed the payment system Alipay into something that served over 1 billion users — one of the world’s most impressive financial platforms, processing more payments than Visa and Mastercard combined.

In October 2020, Ma gave a speech at a financial conference in Shanghai in which he criticized China’s banking regulators as a “pawnshop mentality” that stifled innovation — and implicitly compared China’s regulatory system unfavorably to the innovation-friendly environment he saw as necessary for financial services. It was a speech that, in most market economies, would have registered as standard libertarian tech entrepreneur rhetoric.

In China, it was catastrophic timing. Ant Group’s IPO — which had been valued at potentially $34 billion, the world’s largest — was suspended within days. Regulatory investigations into Alibaba were launched. Ant Group was required to convert from the loosely regulated fintech model that had made it profitable into a tightly regulated financial holding company. Ma himself disappeared from public view for three months, appearing briefly in January 2021 in a carefully managed online appearance with rural teachers. He has since spent most of his time outside China, reportedly in Tokyo and elsewhere.

The message his case sent to China’s entrepreneur class was clear and clearly received: economic success does not confer political standing; private wealth is contingent on continuing party approval; and speaking truth to power is not a protected activity, whatever your net worth. The implications for China’s innovation ecosystem — whether the creative risk-taking that technological advancement requires can flourish in an environment where political orthodoxy constrains intellectual freedom — are being debated with increasing anxiety.

REN ZHENGFEI (1944–): Building Under Siege

Ren Zhengfei is the most important Chinese businessman you have probably never heard of. The founder of Huawei — currently the world’s single most contested company, the subject of trade wars, espionage accusations, and civilizational conflict between the US and China — is, in person, a quietly spoken engineer from Guizhou province who spent his early career in the PLA’s engineering corps before founding a company that now defines the global battle for technological leadership.

Ren served in the PLA for 20 years as a military engineer — building fixed-line communications infrastructure in construction corps units — before retiring and, at age 43, founding Huawei in 1987 with 21,000 yuan (roughly $5,000 in 1987 terms) scraped together from a handful of engineers and friends. The founding story is one of the great entrepreneurial origin myths: a middle-aged military engineer, no business experience, no connections in the telecom industry, starting a company to sell telecom switches licensed from a Hong Kong firm to rural Chinese phone companies that larger suppliers ignored.

What made Huawei is two things: ferocious R&D investment (Huawei has consistently spent 10–15% of annual revenue on R&D — a rate that equals or exceeds most Western technology companies) and organizational culture modeled partly on military discipline and partly on Ren’s own obsessive focus on long-term mission over short-term profit. Huawei is not publicly listed; it has no stock price to chase; its employee-shareholders (every senior employee holds shares) have long investment horizons. These structural features gave Huawei the ability to invest in technologies — 5G, cloud computing, AI chips — for timeframes that publicly listed competitors, managed quarter-to-quarter, found difficult to match.

His management of Huawei under the US siege since 2018 is the most watched case study in corporate resilience and adaptation in the contemporary technology industry. The company lost significant revenue from its device business when cut off from Android and American chips; it pivoted aggressively into enterprise services, cloud computing, solar inverters for energy systems, and the domestic Chinese market where US companies cannot operate. It has produced its own operating system, its own mapping software, its own chips (within the constraints of the manufacturing capabilities available to it). Whether this adaptation succeeds in making Huawei genuinely competitive with its global peers without access to the most advanced semiconductor technology is still being determined.

CHAPTER 9 — Lessons from China: What the World Can Learn

Eight Principles for Extraordinary Development

Distilling 45 years of the most complex economic transformation in history into lessons for other nations requires both intellectual honesty about what was replicable and humility about what was specific to China’s particular moment, scale, and institutional context.

Lesson 1: Pragmatism Over Ideology

Deng’s “black cat, white cat” principle is the foundational lesson: the measure of economic policy is results, not theoretical consistency. Countries that cling to ideological models — whether socialist production planning or pure free-market dogma — against empirical evidence of their failure are sacrificing development on the altar of intellectual consistency. China’s dual-track price liberalization was theoretically incoherent; it was practically brilliant. China’s SEZs were ideologically contradictory (capitalist enclaves in a communist state); they were practically transformative.

Lesson 2: Sequence Reforms to Protect Reform Momentum

The contrast between China’s dual-track sequencing and the Soviet Union’s shock therapy is the most important natural experiment in modern economic policy. China’s sequenced approach — protecting existing stakeholders from sudden disruption while creating space for market mechanisms to develop alongside the planned system — maintained political stability through the reform transition. Shock therapy’s sudden disruption destroyed both the economy and the political conditions for further reform.

Lesson 3: Infrastructure Investment Ahead of Demand

China built ports, highways, railways, and electricity generation before they were fully needed, creating the productive capacity that attracted manufacturing investment. The lesson for developing countries: infrastructure must precede development, not follow it. Countries that wait for private investment to demonstrate demand before building public infrastructure wait forever, because private investment waits for the infrastructure.

Lesson 4: Export Discipline as Industrial Learning

China’s integration into global supply chains did not merely generate employment. It generated rents from the knowledge embedded in global supply chains — quality management, logistics, product standards, engineering techniques. Every foreign factory that trained Chinese workers and engineers was an industrial school. The lesson: export markets impose quality and efficiency standards that domestic monopolies do not; competitive exposure, even when initially uncomfortable, builds industrial capability.

Lesson 5: Protect Some Sectors, Open Others — and Know the Difference

China maintained state dominance in finance, energy, and telecoms while opening manufacturing to foreign competition. This industrial policy — protecting sectors where foreign dominance would compromise national security or development capacity, while opening sectors where competitive exposure would accelerate learning — is the opposite of the “everything open” IMF model that produced poor results in many developing countries. The trick is knowing which is which — and China’s MITI-equivalent (the National Development and Reform Commission) made better calls than most.

Lesson 6: Human Capital Investment Compounds

Mao’s literacy campaigns, combined with the expansion of technical and university education across the reform era (China today has more engineering graduates per year than any country in the world), created the human capital foundation for successively more sophisticated industrial activity. Countries that neglect basic and technical education find that comparative advantage in low-wage assembly work is a ceiling rather than a starting point.

Lesson 7: Tolerate and Learn from Corruption at Low Levels; Destroy It at High Levels

China’s reform era was characterised by extremely high levels of lower-level corruption — local officials accepting bribes, officials doing business with the companies they regulated. This was tolerated (partly because it was impossible to stamp out given the political system, partly because it aligned local officials’ interests with economic growth). High-level corruption — the kind that extracted state resources for private enrichment at the national level — has been more vigorously prosecuted, particularly under Xi. The lesson is uncomfortable: some tolerance of entrepreneurial grey-area activity may be necessary at an early stage of market development; but corruption at scale destroys the investment environment and public trust.

Lesson 8: The Demonstration Effect Is the Most Powerful Argument

Deng’s Southern Tour worked because he pointed to Shenzhen and said: look. The physical reality of what development had achieved offered an argument no ideology could refute. Development programmes that show early, visible results in specific places create the political support for broader reform replication. The SEZ model — experiment in a zone, prove results, scale nationwide — is applicable far beyond China’s specific context.

China vs. the Asian Tigers: What Was Shared and What Was Unique

China’s development model shares important features with the earlier Asian success stories — Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore — while differing in ways that are both significant and instructive:

Shared features: Export-led growth, using global market integration and comparative advantage in manufacturing as the primary engine; state-guided industrial policy, with government taking an active role in directing credit, protecting infant industries, and selecting sectors for development; high savings rates and investment rates; investment in education as a national priority; Confucian cultural foundations that emphasize education, deferred gratification, and collective identity over individual consumption.

What made China unique: Scale — China’s economy is larger than Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and Singapore combined. The scale creates both advantages (domestic market large enough to support world-scale industries) and complications (coordinating policy across a country of 1.4 billion people requires a different governance approach than managing Singapore’s 6 million). Historical moment — China’s integration into global trade happened at the precise moment when container shipping had made manufacturing globally footloose, when American retailers and tech companies needed low-cost manufacturing partners, and when the post-Cold War “peace dividend” created an unusually favorable environment for developing country integration into global supply chains. Earlier developers (Japan, Korea) faced more restricted market access; later developers (India, Vietnam, Bangladesh) face a China-saturated supply chain environment where carving out a similar manufacturing role is much harder. Authoritarian state capacity — China could build infrastructure, relocate populations, redirect industrial investment, and implement policy changes at a speed that democratic systems (with their procedural requirements for consultation, environmental review, legal challenge) cannot match.

The Middle-Income Trap: China’s Next Great Challenge

The middle-income trap — the tendency for countries to stall their development after reaching middle-income status (roughly $5,000–$15,000 per capita GDP) because the low-wage, export-assembly model that drove early growth no longer functions while the innovation-led model that characterizes rich countries hasn’t yet developed — is the test that separates successful from unsuccessful late developers.

Of the 101 countries that were middle-income in 1960, only 13 reached high-income status by 2008 (according to World Bank analysis). Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan are the most prominent successes; most Latin American countries (Brazil) are the most prominent failures.

China’s challenge: its wages have risen dramatically — average manufacturing wages in China are now 5–8 times higher than in Vietnam or Bangladesh, meaning that the most labour-intensive assembly work is migrating to lower-wage competitors. Its innovation capacity, while expanding rapidly (patent filings, R&D investment, semiconductor capabilities), faces technology governance constraints (US chip sanctions) and political constraints (Xi’s reassertion of party control over technology companies) that create uncertainty about the innovation ecosystem.

The honest assessment: China will probably avoid the middle-income trap, but not easily. Its scale gives it a domestic market large enough to sustain sophisticated industries even without global market access. Its state capacity to direct investment into strategic sectors gives it more options for technological upgrading than most countries have. But the political centralization under Xi creates innovation-hostile conditions that historical evidence consistently suggests matter: breakthrough innovation requires intellectual freedom, tolerance of failure, competitive markets for ideas, and protection for unconventional thinkers — all of which sit uncomfortably with the current political direction.

CHAPTER 10 — The Future Dragon: Challenges and the Road Ahead

The Demographic Time Bomb That China Built Itself

In 1978, facing apparently inexorable population growth (China’s population had doubled between 1949 and 1979, adding another 500 million people to be fed, educated, and employed), Deng’s government implemented the One Child Policy — a restriction on family size, enforced with a combination of financial penalties, social pressure, and (particularly in rural and minority communities) coercive measures including forced sterilizations and abortions, that remained in force until 2015.

The demographic outcome: China’s total fertility rate (the average number of children born per woman) fell from approximately 5.0 in 1970 to 1.5 by 2000 — and has continued to fall, reaching approximately 1.0 in the most recent years, well below the 2.1 replacement rate. China’s population peaked at approximately 1.41 billion in 2021 and has begun declining — the first population decline since the Great Leap Forward famine of 1959–1962.

The economic consequences, as they manifest over the coming decades, will be profound:

The shrinking workforce: China’s working-age population (15–64) peaked around 2010 and is already declining. Between 2012 and 2022, the working-age population shrank by approximately 40 million. This demographic headwind will intensify: by 2050, China is projected to have fewer than 800 million working-age people, down from over 1 billion in 2011. Every manufacturing firm, every technology company, every infrastructure project will be competing for a smaller pool of workers — with rising wages as the inevitable result.

The aging burden: The old-age dependency ratio — the number of retired people relative to working people who must support them through pensions and healthcare — will rise dramatically. Currently approximately 20 (20 retired people per 100 working people), it is projected to reach 44 by 2050. This means that China’s pension and healthcare systems, already strained, face structural funding crises of growing severity. The “4-2-1” family structure (one child supporting two parents and four grandparents) creates a social care burden with no historical precedent.

China’s government has responded to this crisis with policy reversals of considerable irony: the One Child Policy became a Two Child Policy in 2015, a Three Child Policy in 2021, and then effectively unlimited in 2023. It has offered baby bonuses, tax benefits for parents, and extended maternity leave. None of these measures show signs of increasing the birth rate: Chinese women who were educated and urbanized under a one-child cultural norm have generally internalized small-family preferences that financial incentives alone cannot reverse. The demographic trajectory appears locked in for at least the next two to three decades.

The Real Estate Crisis: When Growth’s Fuel Becomes Its Fire

For two decades, China’s real estate sector was the engine of economic growth: construction companies built apartments, cities expanded, local governments sold land to developers (land sales were the primary revenue source for local governments, which had limited taxing authority), and ordinary Chinese families accumulated most of their wealth in the apartments they owned. Real estate and related industries accounted for approximately 25–30% of China’s GDP — a concentration far exceeding any other major economy.

Evergrande — which had grown, through 20 years of debt-fueled expansion, into the world’s most indebted company with liabilities of over $300 billion — defaulted on its international bond obligations in December 2021, triggering a cascade of developer failures across China’s property sector. The crisis exposed structural features that had been building for years: developers had collected full payment for apartments not yet built (pre-sales were standard practice) and had used those payments to fund land purchases for future developments rather than construction of contracted apartments. When the music stopped, millions of Chinese homebuyers had paid for apartments that might not be completed.

The macroeconomic stakes: China’s property sector, at its 2020 peak, was valued at approximately $60 trillion — equal to the GDP of the United States, European Union, and Japan combined. Even a partial deflation of this valuation removes an enormous amount of household wealth and local government revenue simultaneously. The Chinese government’s response — engineering a controlled deflation by directing which developers survive (state-linked ones) and allowing others to fail — has been, by international standards, remarkably orderly. But the deflation of Chinese household wealth in real estate, and the reduction of construction activity (which has been one of the primary employment sources for China’s migrant worker population), will drag on growth for years.

The Taiwan Wildcard: The Question That Changes Everything

Taiwan is the most dangerous geopolitical flash-point in the contemporary world — and the one with the most extraordinary economic stakes.

Taiwan is home to TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company) — the company that fabricates approximately 92% of the world’s most advanced semiconductors, the chips without which modern electronics, artificial intelligence, defense systems, automobiles, and virtually every other technology cannot function. TSMC’s advanced fabs represent the most complex manufacturing infrastructure in human history: each factory takes 5+ years and $15–20 billion to build, requires thousands of specialized engineers with decades of accumulated knowledge, and depends on equipment (ASML’s extreme ultraviolet lithography machines, available alone from ASML in the Netherlands) that cannot be quickly reproduced.

A military conflict over Taiwan — whether China’s People’s Liberation Army were to invade or a confrontation were to interrupt semiconductor production — would paralyze the global technology economy simultaneously. The supply chain disruption from COVID (which demonstrated what happens when a single factory region experiences production interruption) would be modest by comparison. This is what analysts mean when they describe Taiwan’s semiconductor industry as a “silicon shield” — a deterrent that makes conflict over Taiwan economically catastrophic for every participant, including China.

Xi Jinping has been explicit that “reunification” with Taiwan is a central national priority — one he considers a historical imperative of the national restoration project. Taiwan’s government and people have been equally explicit that they do not seek formal independence (which would certainly trigger conflict) but that they consider themselves a separate, self-governing democratic society and reject the PRC’s authority over them. The US has maintained “strategic ambiguity” — not formally committing to defend Taiwan militarily while making clear that it would “help” in undefined ways.

Whether this situation produces conflict, continued unstable equilibrium, or some form of negotiated arrangement in the next decade is the single most consequential unknown in the global economy’s future.

Xi’s Centralization: The Innovation Paradox

Xi Jinping’s China confronts what might be called the innovation paradox: the political system that delivered extraordinary manufacturing success — centralized authority, disciplined implementation, state capacity to direct investment at scale — may be poorly suited to the innovation-led economy that China’s next developmental stage requires.

Manufacturing success requires discipline: executing proven processes more efficiently, absorbing known technologies, implementing quality standards. Innovation success requires something different: the tolerance of failure (most innovative experiments fail), the protection of unconventional thinking (breakthroughs come from people who question established assumptions), the diversity of approaches (competition among ideas, not just among products), and the safety to pursue lines of research without knowing where they will lead.

These qualities sit uncomfortably with a political system that punishes public dissent, requires ideological conformity in universities and research institutions, monitors scientists’ communications for politically sensitive content, and has recently demonstrated (through Jack Ma’s experience and the regulatory crackdowns on technology companies) that private sector success does not protect against political vulnerability.

The counterargument: China’s state direction has produced genuine innovation in specific sectors where the state has committed resources — electric vehicles, renewable energy, high-speed rail — suggesting that directed innovation is at least partially possible. And Chinese private companies, when they have been allowed to operate, have demonstrated genuine innovative capacity (Alibaba’s payment systems, DJI’s drones, ByteDance’s recommendation algorithms). The question is not whether Chinese people or companies can innovate; they demonstrably can. The question is whether the current political environment will allow the breadth and depth of innovative activity that the next developmental stage requires.

The Philosophical Reflection: Model, Warning, or Historical Singularity?

What is China’s rise, finally?

It is not a model, in the sense of a development programme with exportable components that other countries can adopt and expect similar results. China’s specific combination — the scale that gives it an unassailable domestic market, the historical moment of globalization that made its manufacturing competitive advantage so extraordinarily valuable, the Confucian institutional inheritance that channels individual ambition through collective discipline, the state capacity that 70+ years of single-party government has built, and the national motivation of the Century of Humiliation — is a historical singularity. No other country in the 21st century will face China’s particular combination of advantages and circumstances.

It is not merely a warning, though it contains many warnings: about the environmental cost of growth-at-all-costs, about the political risks of indefinite authoritarianism, about the demographic consequences of extreme fertility management, about the instability that extreme inequality produces in societies that simultaneously preach egalitarianism. These lessons are real and important.

It is most accurately understood as a demonstration that the assumptions of development economics were drastically wrong in important ways: wrong that democracy was a necessary condition for development (China proved otherwise), wrong that the Washington Consensus (privatize everything, liberalize everything, stabilize fiscally) was the universal development recipe (China violated virtually every one of its prescriptions), wrong that development must be gradual (China moved hundreds of millions from poverty in decades), and wrong that scale is an obstacle to development (China’s scale proved an advantage in important ways).

China’s rise is a challenge to every orthodoxy about how economies develop. It demands that development economists and policymakers think more carefully about sequencing, about the relationship between political systems and economic performance, about the state’s role in directing industrial transformation, and about what “development” actually means when it comes packaged with environmental destruction, political repression, and extraordinary inequality alongside the greatest poverty reduction in history.

The dragon has awakened. Its ascent has changed the global economy in ways that will reverberate for generations — in the supply chains restructured around Chinese manufacturing, in the technologies Chinese companies are building, in the geopolitical competition China’s power has produced, and in the 850 million lives whose transformation from poverty is China’s most enduring and most humane achievement.

What comes next — whether China’s political system can sustain the innovation its economic ambitions require, whether it can manage its demographic and real estate challenges without a “hard landing,” whether it can navigate the Taiwan question without catastrophic conflict, and whether Xi Jinping’s centralization will prove to be the factor that limits a story that, until recently, seemed to have no limit — is a story that is still being written.

In the most important sense, it is being written by 1.4 billion people who have lived through the most astonishing collective transformation in human history, and who carry within them expectations of continued progress that their country’s leadership will find extremely difficult to disappoint.
