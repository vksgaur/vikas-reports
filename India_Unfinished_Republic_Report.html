🇮🇳 The Unfinished Republic: India’s Journey from Colonial Poverty to Economic Superpower

A Long-Form Report on the Most Complex, Contradictory, and Inspiring Economic Story of Our Time

In 1700, India accounted for approximately 25% of global GDP. It was the world’s largest economy — a civilization of astonishing textile artisans, master mathematicians, maritime merchants, and philosophical depth that had no parallel on Earth.

In 1947, when that civilization reclaimed its political sovereignty after 190 years of colonial rule, its per capita income was approximately $64. Life expectancy was 32 years. Literacy stood at 16%. Hundreds of millions of people had never eaten a full meal in their lives. The subcontinent had just been split in blood, with 14 million people uprooted and 1–2 million killed in weeks of communal carnage.

The distance between those two realities — the India of 1700 and the India of 1947 — is not simply a story of colonial exploitation, though that is a large part of it. It is also a story of what happens when a civilization is systematically stripped of the right to make its own decisions, over generations long enough that the stripping begins to feel like nature.

What happened next — the 77-year attempt to rebuild, reform, argue, fail, recover, and grow — is the subject of this report. It is not a clean miracle, like Singapore’s. It is not a ruthless sprint, like South Korea’s. It is something harder to categorize and ultimately more interesting: a civilization arguing with itself, at full democratic volume, about what it wants to become.

CHAPTER 1 — The Weight of Civilisation: India Before Independence

The World’s Largest Economy

To understand India’s economic journey, you must begin with a fact that most Indians know intellectually but has not settled fully into the national psyche: India was, for most of recorded human history, enormously wealthy.

In 1700, India’s share of global GDP stood at approximately 24–25% — rivalled only by China, and vastly exceeding the combined output of all of Europe. The Mughal Empire under Aurangzeb was, in terms of absolute economic output, the largest empire in the world. Indian textiles — the fine muslins of Dhaka so light they were called woven air, the printed cottons of Gujarat, the silk weaves of Varanasi — were globally coveted luxury goods, traded across the Indian Ocean world that Indian merchants had dominated for centuries. The subcontinent’s mathematical and astronomical traditions had already given humanity the numeral system it uses today, the concept of zero, and sophisticated algebraic frameworks.

This was not a static or frozen civilization. It was dynamic, commercially sophisticated, and deeply integrated into global trade networks — before the word “globalization” existed, India was its leading practitioner.

Then the British came. Not all at once, and not with a clear plan for destruction. The East India Company arrived in 1608 seeking trade. It left — or rather, was replaced by the Crown — in 1858, after having systematically transformed the world’s largest manufacturing economy into a supplier of raw materials for British industry.

The Colonial Deindustrialisation

The mechanism of this transformation was both brutal and banal. As British industrial production grew through the 18th and early 19th centuries, Indian woven goods became a competitive threat. The British response was elegant in its ruthlessness: impose high tariffs on Indian textiles entering Britain (sometimes 70–80%), while forcing open Indian markets to British machine-made cloth at low or zero tariffs. British factories produced cotton goods at mechanised scale; Indian artisans, no longer able to compete on price in global markets or even in their own, abandoned their looms.

The result was what economic historians call deindustrialisation: a deliberate unravelling of the world’s most sophisticated pre-industrial manufacturing ecosystem. Dhaka’s muslin industry, which had employed hundreds of thousands of weavers, collapsed within decades of British rule consolidating. The city’s population fell from 150,000 in 1800 to 30,000 by 1840. Similar processes repeated across Bengal, Gujarat, and the Deccan.

Simultaneously, India was converted into a supplier of raw materials — cotton, indigo, jute, opium — for British industry and imperial needs. Railroads were built, yes — but built to move goods from Indian interior to British ports, not to knit the Indian economy together. Economist Utsa Patnaik’s calculation — based on the mechanism of the “drain of wealth” through the trade surplus India was forced to run without receiving payment — estimates the total extraction at approximately $45 trillion over 200 years. Even if you halve the figure, it represents one of the largest sustained wealth transfers in human history.

The Manufactured Famines

The clearest evidence that colonial rule was actively destructive of Indian welfare is the series of catastrophic famines that punctuated British governance. The Bengal Famine of 1770 killed an estimated 10 million people — one-third of Bengal’s population — partly as a direct consequence of the East India Company’s extraction of revenue even as agricultural distress mounted. The famines of 1876–1879 killed 5–10 million people across peninsular India, during which the British viceroy Lord Lytton hosted a lavish Imperial Assemblage while exporting record quantities of Indian grain to Britain.

The Bengal Famine of 1943 killed 2–3 million people in the midst of World War II. Winston Churchill’s War Cabinet was informed. Churchill personally rejected appeals to divert shipping for food imports for Bengal, diverting Bengal’s rice exports to European and Australian stockpiles instead. The Nobel laureate Amartya Sen’s foundational research demonstrated that no famine has ever occurred in a functioning democracy. Each Indian famine under British rule was a political event enabled by the absence of Indian political power.

What the British Built

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging what the British also constructed. The railways — 67,000 kilometres of track by 1947, the world’s fourth-largest network — gave the subcontinent a physical spine it had lacked. The British codified a legal system that provided a framework for contract and commercial dispute resolution still operative today. English-language education — introduced explicitly to create a class of clerks for colonial administration — produced, as its unintended consequence, the lawyers, scientists, and eventually software engineers who would build independent India. The IIT graduates who built India’s IT revolution were educated in English. The coloniser’s administrative convenience became the colonised’s competitive advantage.

August 15, 1947

When Nehru delivered his “Tryst with Destiny” speech at the stroke of midnight, the subcontinent was simultaneously celebrating and bleeding. Partition had created two nations from one civilization — divided on lines drawn by Cyril Radcliffe, a British lawyer who had never visited India before arriving six weeks earlier to divide it. Perhaps 14 million people were displaced. 1–2 million killed in communal violence. The India that emerged had a per capita income of roughly $64, life expectancy of 32 years, 84% illiteracy, and 500-odd princely states to be persuaded into joining the new nation.

The operating question — never spoken aloud in the celebratory moment — was whether democracy and development could coexist. No one had done it before at India’s scale. The historical precedents for development in Asia involved colonialism or authoritarianism. India was choosing neither.

CHAPTER 2 — Nehru’s Vision: The Socialist Temple and Its Foundations (1947–1964)

The Man Behind the Blueprint

Jawaharlal Nehru (1889–1964) was, in ways both admirable and consequential, the most literary figure ever to lead a major nation. He wrote The Discovery of India while imprisoned in Ahmednagar Fort — a book of astonishing breadth, tracing the arc of Indian civilization with the tenderness of a man in love with a country. He was a romantic and an intellectual who had absorbed the Fabian socialism of Cambridge alongside the anti-colonial nationalism of Gandhi, and who genuinely believed that science, planning, and the rational organization of the state could deliver his people from the poverty that colonialism had manufactured.

His errors were the errors of his gifts: the same idealism that made him visionary made him unable to see the License Raj’s dysfunction until it was too deeply embedded to fix.

The Nehruvian Settlement

Nehru’s economic model was a “middle path” — neither American capitalism (associated with the colonial commercial relationships that had produced India’s ruin) nor Soviet communism (admired for industrialization but unacceptable politically). He called it democratic socialism: a mixed economy in which the state controlled the “commanding heights” — steel, coal, power, heavy machinery — while consumer industries remained in private hands.

The Five-Year Plans directed public investment into industrial development. The logic was sound: India had no indigenous capital class capable of funding the scale of investment needed. The market could not build a steel industry from scratch. The state had to lead.

The institutions he built were real and consequential. Steel plants at Bhilai, Rourkela, and Durgapur are still running. The five IITs established between 1951 and 1961 produced the engineering talent that built India’s IT industry. The CSIR research laboratories conducted the scientific research that would later underpin India’s pharmaceutical capability. Nehru called the great dams and factories “the temples of modern India” — and they genuinely were, in the sense that they were built with the faith and sacrifice of a nation investing in its future.

The License Raj: A Good Idea’s Kafkaesque Shadow

Alongside the achievements ran a system that strangled Indian economic dynamism for four decades. The Industrial Development and Regulation Act of 1951 required any business seeking to establish, expand, or change its product mix to obtain a government license. The Foreign Exchange Regulation Act controlled all foreign currency transactions. The MRTP Act restricted large companies from expanding without permission.

The intent was coherent: prevent monopolistic concentration, direct investment into priority sectors. The reality, accumulated over years, was a labyrinthine system of permissions in which delay was endemic, corruption was inevitable, and the most important business skill was not innovation but the management of relationships in New Delhi.

Indian industry adapted — in the wrong direction. Companies learned to work around the License Raj rather than compete within it. Dhirubhai Ambani — perhaps the greatest Indian businessman of the 20th century — was a genius not primarily of manufacturing or technology but of government relationship management: understanding which politician to cultivate, which bureaucratic lever to pull, which license to lock up before a competitor could. These were real skills in the License Raj economy. They were not the skills that built South Korea.

The License Raj was idealistic, not cynical — and that made it harder to reform. Nehru genuinely believed industrial licensing protected the poor against exploitation by capital. He could not have imagined that by the 1980s, getting permission to manufacture a new drug would take 9 years, or that a truck crossing from Maharashtra to Karnataka would require 26 separate documents at the state border.

A nation of sprinters, forced to run in bureaucratic ankle chains. The talent was always there. The system was the problem.

The 1962 Humiliation and Its Legacy

On October 20, 1962, Chinese forces launched coordinated attacks across India’s disputed Himalayan borders. The Indian army — underprepared, operating on political rather than military logic — collapsed in some sectors with startling speed. By November, China had declared a unilateral ceasefire, but the damage was done. Nehru, who had championed Hindi-Chini bhai-bhai as a foreign policy pillar and never believed war was coming, was shattered. He died on May 27, 1964, diminished — and India inherited a security trauma and an inward-looking psychology that would shape its economic thinking for the next three decades.

His legacy is genuinely complex. The IITs he built produce the engineers who drive India’s 21st-century economy. The steel plants are still running. The license raj he created cost India two decades of growth. Both things are equally true.

CHAPTER 3 — Indira Gandhi: Nationaliser, Emergency, and the Green Revolution (1966–1984)

Steel and Paranoia

Indira Gandhi (1917–1984) was steely and suspicious in the way that powerful women in patriarchal systems often become: armoured by necessity, precise in political calculation, trusting very few. She was not an ideological economist; she was a political operator who used economic decisions instrumentally, as tools of power consolidation more than development strategy.

Her landmark economic move — the nationalisation of India’s 14 largest commercial banks in July 1969 — was announced without prior cabinet discussion, in the manner of a military operation. Banks mandated to open rural branches and extend agricultural credit to small borrowers. The Garibi Hatao (Abolish Poverty) slogan gave Indira a political language that spoke directly to India’s majority.

What did bank nationalisation achieve? Rural bank branch networks expanded from 8,200 branches in 1969 to over 60,000 by the late 1980s. Agricultural credit reached farmers who had previously depended entirely on moneylenders charging 80% annual interest. These were genuine gains. But the nationalised banks also became instruments of political patronage — loans directed by political instruction rather than commercial logic. The seeds of India’s chronic Non-Performing Asset crisis — which haunts the banking system today — were first planted in 1969.

The 1971 Triumph

In November–December 1971, Indira Gandhi presided over the most decisive military victory in Indian history: the two-week war that created Bangladesh. Pakistani forces surrendered; Indira emerged as the most powerful figure in South Asian politics, hailed domestically as Durga. The political capital was enormous. She spent little of it on economic reform.

The Emergency: India’s Darkest Democratic Chapter

On June 25, 1975, citing national security threats and having been found guilty of electoral malpractice by the Allahabad High Court, Indira Gandhi declared a State of Emergency. For 21 months, democratic India was suspended. The press was censored — physically, by cutting electricity to newspaper printing presses on the first night. Opposition politicians were imprisoned. Courts were intimidated. Parliament was made irrelevant.

Her son Sanjay Gandhi ran a parallel, unelected power centre that drove brutal programmes: a forced sterilisation campaign that coerced vasectomies from poor men rounded up by officials; demolition of Delhi slums displacing hundreds of thousands. The Emergency’s economic track record is debated — inflation fell, trains ran punctually, industrial discipline improved production metrics. Some still argue the Emergency “worked economically.” This argument — that efficiency justifies the abolition of rights — is technically credible and morally revealing. India’s voters exercised clear judgment in 1977, routing Indira in the first post-Emergency election.

The Green Revolution: India’s First Great Development Success

While political drama consumed attention, one of the 20th century’s great development interventions was quietly transforming Indian agriculture.

In the 1960s, India was in chronic food emergency — the phrase “ship-to-mouth” described a bleak reality: American PL-480 food aid was arriving monthly to prevent starvation. M.S. Swaminathan, working with Norman Borlaug’s high-yielding wheat varieties and with Indira Gandhi’s political support, concentrated the Green Revolution initially on Punjab and Haryana — states with good water access and farmer entrepreneurialism. The results were extraordinary. Wheat production went from 11 million tonnes in 1966 to 17 million in 1968 — in two years. By 1971, India had food surpluses. By the 1980s, India had moved from food import dependency to food export capability.

The Green Revolution was not without costs: chemical inputs degraded soil health, groundwater depletion in Punjab became severe, and benefits accrued primarily to larger farmers. Swaminathan himself, in later years, became the Green Revolution’s most honest critic. But the primary achievement stands: the Green Revolution fed a billion Indians and gave India the food security without which no other development would have been possible.

October 31, 1984

Indira Gandhi was shot by two of her own Sikh bodyguards, acting in retaliation for Operation Blue Star — the army’s assault on the Golden Temple in Amritsar. The anti-Sikh pogrom that followed — approximately 3,000–8,000 Sikhs killed in Delhi and other cities, with Congress politicians directing mobs and police standing aside — revealed the communal tinder beneath India’s democratic surface with searing clarity.

CHAPTER 4 — Rajiv Gandhi: The Accidental Reformer (1984–1989)

The Pilot Who Inherited a Subcontinent

Rajiv Gandhi had not planned to enter politics. He was an airline pilot who had watched his brother Sanjay’s political career with something close to relief. When Sanjay died in a plane crash in 1980, family expectation fell on Rajiv. Four years later, he was Prime Minister of 800 million people, swept to power by a sympathy wave after his mother’s assassination, with the largest parliamentary majority in Indian electoral history.

He was 40 years old, with no ministerial experience, fundamentally decent, modernising in his instincts, and thoroughly unprepared. What he had was an almost naïve conviction that technology could transform India — and a friend named Sam Pitroda who shared it with evangelical intensity.

The Computer Policy: The Hidden Seed of Bangalore

In 1984, Rajiv’s government issued a New Computer Policy that dramatically reduced import duties on hardware and software, liberalised computer manufacturing, and signalled that India would embrace the digital revolution rather than fear it. It was one of the most consequential policy acts in Indian economic history — though its consequences would not be visible for nearly a decade.

The Software Technology Parks scheme — establishing zones where software export companies could import equipment duty-free — created the institutional model for India’s IT export industry. Companies like Infosys and Wipro would build their early growth within STP registrations.

Sam Pitroda drove the roll-out of digital telephone exchanges and the PCO — the Public Call Office, or STD booth — which appeared across Indian towns and villages. For the first time, a farmer in rural Bihar could call his son in Delhi without booking a trunk call 24 hours in advance. The STD booth was not glamorous, but it was transformative: it gave ordinary Indians their first experience of communication infrastructure that actually worked, and planted the seeds of a connectivity revolution.

Reforms and Reversals

Rajiv’s partial economic liberalisation — modest reductions in licensing requirements and some easing of import duties — began unwinding the License Raj at its margins. Growth accelerated. But the Bofors scandal — allegations of kickbacks to Congress party figures for a Swedish howitzer contract — attached corruption’s stain to a leader elected on a platform of clean governance. The Shah Bano case reversal — overturning a Supreme Court judgment protecting a divorced Muslim woman’s maintenance rights under pressure from conservative Muslim opinion — revealed that modernisation had its limits when communal vote banks were at stake.

He was assassinated on May 21, 1991, during the election campaign following his 1989 defeat — killed by a female suicide bomber sent by Sri Lanka’s LTTE. The historical irony of his death is extraordinary: it was the chaos of his assassination, mid-election, that brought to power a man all but written off as a political irrelevance — who would change India’s economic history forever.

CHAPTER 5 — The Crisis and the Cure: 1991 and India’s Economic Revolution

The Night India Pawned Its Gold

In the spring of 1991, India’s economy was dying. The balance of payments crisis had been building through 1990 — partly from Gulf War disruption of worker remittances, partly from fiscal profligacy, partly from the structural fragility of an economy that had been growing faster than its foreign exchange earnings could sustain. By May 1991, India’s foreign exchange reserves stood at approximately $1.2 billion — enough for perhaps two weeks of imports.

What happened next is one of the defining moments in post-Independence history, and it happened largely in secret. The Indian government secretly airlifted 67.5 tonnes of gold from the Reserve Bank of India’s vaults to the Bank of England and the Bank of Japan, pledged as collateral for emergency foreign currency loans. Word leaked. The Economic Times ran the story. The symbolism was devastating: a civilization that had once accounted for a quarter of global GDP was secretly pawning its national gold reserves to pay its grocery bills.

A country that once had to pawn its gold in secret to pay its bills now receives $80 billion in foreign investment annually — and the investors are lining up. But we are not there yet. It was also the most useful crisis in Indian economic history.

P.V. Narasimha Rao: The Forgotten Liberator

History has treated P.V. Narasimha Rao (1921–2004) with peculiar ingratitude. He was one of the most consequential Prime Ministers India has ever had, and his name is on virtually no major institution or national programme.

Rao was a Telugu Brahmin scholar who read 17 languages, a political survivor who had been essentially retired — managing his farm and writing a novel — at the time of Rajiv Gandhi’s assassination. No one expected him to win the 1991 election. No one expected transformative governance from a 69-year-old recovering heart patient brought in as a caretaker.

What he did — with the full understanding of a lifelong politician who knew exactly how controversial it would be — was to use the economic crisis as cover for reforms he had believed in for years but which no Indian leader had dared attempt. He needed a Finance Minister of exceptional ability and credibility. He appointed Manmohan Singh.

Manmohan Singh and the Budget That Changed India

Manmohan Singh (born 1932) came from a small village in undivided Punjab — now in Pakistan. His father was a small trader. He walked miles to school. He read by kerosene lamp. He won scholarships to Cambridge and Oxford, completed his doctorate in trade economics, served as Reserve Bank Governor, and had been one of India’s most distinguished economic administrators for decades.

He was also, famously, the most mild-mannered senior politician in Indian public life — speaking softly, choosing words with the precision of someone who distrusted theatrics, the exact opposite of a demagogue.

On July 24, 1991, Manmohan Singh presented the Union Budget that was the most consequential in independent India’s history. Describing the moment India had reached, he quoted Victor Hugo: “No power on Earth can stop an idea whose time has come.” The idea whose time had come was the market economy.

The LPG Reforms — Liberalization, Privatization, Globalization:

Industrial licensing was abolished for all but a handful of sectors. For the first time since 1951, an Indian entrepreneur could start a factory without government permission to produce.

Import tariffs — which had reached 300% or more on some goods — were dramatically reduced, exposing Indian industry to international competition for the first time in four decades.

Foreign direct investment was opened in sectors previously closed, with automatic approval for equity positions up to 51%.

The rupee was devalued, making Indian exports cheaper and correcting a long-running overvaluation that had made Indian goods uncompetitive internationally.

Public sector enterprises would be partially privatised — the state would retain strategic control while allowing market capital to participate.

The IMF’s structural adjustment programme had demanded most of these reforms as conditions for the bailout. Rao and Singh would have implemented them anyway — the genius of the crisis was that it provided political cover for what was economically necessary but politically unpalatable.

The Question That Haunts 1991

India’s 1991 reforms are celebrated today almost universally — but they also prompt a question deserving honest examination: why did a country with India’s intellectual capital wait until a gun was at its head before reforming?

China reformed in 1978 from a position of relative stability. South Korea built its export-oriented industrial economy in the 1960s. Why did India wait 40 years?

The answer is a complex knot: the License Raj created industries that lobbied ferociously against reform. The Nehruvian ideological inheritance was genuine. Poverty was so widespread that any reform characterised as “pro-rich” was politically lethal in a universal-franchise democracy. The vested interests in the existing system — bureaucrats, politicians, protected industrialists — were more organised and immediate in opposition than diffuse future beneficiaries were in support.

This is the structural problem of reform in a democracy: the losers are specific, concentrated, and vocal; the winners are general, diffuse, and don’t yet know they will benefit. India’s democracy made the License Raj much harder to dismantle than authoritarianism would have — but it also meant that when reform came, it had to come through democratic process, making it slower, messier, and ultimately more durable.

CHAPTER 6 — The IT Revolution: How India Conquered the Digital World (1991–2010)

The Union of Unlikely Advantages

The Indian IT miracle was built on a confluence of factors so specific and historically contingent that it seems, in retrospect, almost designed — except that no one designed it consciously. It emerged from the intersection of Nehruvian educational investment (the IITs), colonial linguistic policy (English as the medium of Indian elite education), Rajiv Gandhi’s computer policy, and the accident of historical timing (Y2K).

The IITs admitted students through the brutally competitive IIT JEE, effectively identifying mathematical and engineering talent across all of India’s socioeconomic diversity. The product: a graduating class of world-class engineers trained at approximately $3,000 per year in government subsidy — training that would have cost $60,000–100,000 annually in the United States.

English was the critical connective tissue. Indian engineers could communicate with American clients, read American technical manuals, and work in American corporate cultures without language barriers — a capability that technically skilled engineers from China, Korea, Brazil and Eastern Europe initially lacked. This was Macaulay’s revenge: the language taught to create colonial clerks became the medium through which post-colonial India accessed the global knowledge economy.

The time zone advantage was discovered by accident: with a 9.5–12.5 hour difference from US time zones, Indian engineers could work on American clients’ problems while the American team slept, creating a 24-hour development cycle. The result was faster delivery, lower cost, and a structural integration of Indian technical talent into American technology production.

Y2K: The Accidental On-Ramp

The Year 2000 software problem required millions of lines of existing code to be read, analysed, and corrected — engineering literacy but not engineering creativity. American and European companies, facing enormous volumes of legacy code and a shortage of people willing to do the work at tolerable cost, turned to Indian IT firms. Y2K gave companies like Infosys, Wipro, and TCS their first major global contracts. It was the on-ramp to the global IT highway: once on the highway, Indian firms moved fast — from code fixes to enterprise application maintenance to business process outsourcing to software product development.

The Founding Generation

Narayana Murthy founded Infosys in 1981 with six colleagues and ₹10,000 (approximately $250) borrowed from his wife Sudha Murthy. His distinctiveness was not technological brilliance alone but ethical construction: he built Infosys with a public commitment to transparency, to paying taxes honestly, to never paying a bribe. In License Raj India, this was genuinely radical. It slowed Infosys in its early years. It made it a global brand when Indian IT went international, because global clients trusted its integrity.

Azim Premji inherited Western India Vegetable Products — a manufacturer of hydrogenated cooking oils — at age 21, having just dropped out of Stanford. He pivoted decade by decade: from vegetable oil to consumer products to information technology, each pivot executed with rigorous discipline. By the 2000s, Wipro was a global IT giant. Premji became its wealthiest individual — and then India’s most generous philanthropist, donating approximately $21 billion to educational causes, the largest philanthropic commitment in Indian history.

Ratan Tata (1937–2024) represented a different strand: the steward of India’s oldest conglomerate navigating liberalisation and taking it global. His acquisition of Jaguar Land Rover from Ford in 2008 — buying iconic British brands that Ford was desperate to shed — was the moment the world understood that Indian capital had arrived. The former colonial subject was now the employer of British workers and owner of British brands. Corus Steel, Tetley Tea, and the Pierre Hotel in New York completed the picture of an Indian conglomerate that had, in a decade, become a genuine multinational.

The Scale of Transformation

IT exports from India grew from approximately $565 million in 1991 to $10 billion by 2000, to $60 billion by 2010, to over $150 billion by 2022 — a 270-fold increase in thirty years. Bangalore transformed from a quiet “garden city” of government establishments into the Silicon Valley of Asia — host to the global development centres of Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Goldman Sachs, and virtually every major technology company on Earth.

The IT revolution created, for the first time in Indian history, a genuinely large aspirational urban middle class with global purchase power and global expectations — the people who bought motorcycles and then cars, who moved from rented flats to owned apartments, who funded the private school revolution, who populated the malls and multiplexes appearing in every major city.

The Honest Reckoning

The IT miracle had limits that need stating clearly. It was a services miracle, not a manufacturing miracle. India produced Infosys and Wipro; it did not produce Samsung or Hyundai. It built a global software industry employing highly educated professionals; it did not build the factory floor that absorbed semi-skilled rural workers by the tens of millions — the transformation that altered the economic geography of South Korea, China, and Taiwan.

At its peak, the Indian IT industry employed fewer than 5 million people directly. India’s population was 1.3 billion. The miracle was real, the prosperity it created was real — but its beneficiaries were a specific demographic: English-speaking, degree-educated, urban. The hundreds of millions working in agriculture, in informal urban economies, in construction and domestic service — for them, the Bangalore miracle was visible on television but tangible only in the remittances sent home by family members who had made it.

Why software and not steel? India’s labour laws made mass manufacturing employment politically difficult to manage. India’s infrastructure — power supply, road quality, port efficiency — was not adequate for competitive manufacturing. IT’s low capital requirements and high talent intensity suited a country with abundant educated labour and scarce investment capital. These are not permanent constraints. But they explain why India’s 21st-century challenge is unlike anything that came before it: having built a world-class knowledge services industry without first going through the manufacturing stage that transformed every other Asian economy, India must now bring the rest of its population into modernity — and the playbook for that has not yet been written.

The Golden Decade

When Manmohan Singh became Prime Minister in May 2004 — having been re-elected to Parliament from Assam after his transformative five years as Finance Minister — he brought to the highest office in the land the unusual combination of intellectual humility and moral authority. He knew exactly what India needed economically. He also knew, with equal precision, the limitations of what a coalition government in a democracy of 1.1 billion people could do about it.

The India he inherited was already accelerating. The IT revolution was in full flow. The liberalisation of 1991 had, after a decade’s latency, begun producing the second-order effects: foreign investment was building factories, real estate, and supply chains; the domestic private sector was expanding into sectors previously dominated by state enterprises; consumer demand was growing at rates that were beginning to look like a genuine mass-market transformation. Between 2004 and 2011, India’s GDP grew at an average of approximately 8–9% per year — the fastest sustained growth in Indian history.

The numbers were real and their human translation was vivid. A Korean child’s lifetime transformation from mud-floored farmhouse to Samsung television had its Indian parallel: an Indian family that had depended on a single government salary in 1991 now had two earning members, owned a two-wheeler, was paying a school fee for their children’s private English-medium education, and was saving — for the first time in the family’s history — in a mutual fund. This was not the entire country. This was 300–400 million people, and it was still the most dramatic improvement in material conditions that India had ever witnessed at scale.

The mobile phone revolution was the most visible face of this transformation. India went from near-zero mobile telephone penetration in 2000 to over 900 million subscribers by 2012 — the fastest telecommunications adoption in the history of any technology in any country. The mobile phone did things in India that landlines had never done: it reached the villages, it gave farmers access to market price information, it allowed migrant workers to transfer money home, it connected rural India to the information economy for the first time. Sunil Mittall’s Airtel, Mukesh Ambani’s Reliance (later), and the fierce competition between dozens of operators drove prices down to among the lowest in the world — at its nadir, Indian mobile calls cost a fraction of a cent per minute.

The Governance Innovations: NREGA, RTI, and Aadhaar

The UPA government, pulled leftward by its coalition support partners, enacted three governance innovations that were genuinely consequential — though their consequences pulled in different directions.

The Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (MGNREGA) of 2005 guaranteed 100 days of wage employment per year to rural households. It was the largest workfare programme in human history, eventually reaching over 200 million rural workers. It raised rural wages — significantly, to the frustration of urban employers who complained about labour cost increases, and to the unambiguous benefit of the rural poor who had previously worked for rates that couldn’t sustain a family. It was also imperfectly implemented, chronically prone to corruption in payment delivery, and provided employment rather than building the productive infrastructure and human capital that India needed. But it was real social protection at scale — a safety net for the most vulnerable.

The Right to Information Act (RTI) of 2005 was a more purely positive innovation: a law that gave every Indian citizen the right to request information from any government office and receive a response within 30 days. In a country whose bureaucracy had operated for centuries on the principle that information was power and power was hoarded, RTI was genuinely revolutionary. Ordinary citizens began using it to expose corruption, question land acquisition decisions, access welfare entitlements, and hold local government accountable. It was democracy at its capillary finest — functioning not through elections alone but through daily civic assertion.

Aadhaar — the biometric identity programme initiated under the UPA and executed brilliantly by Nandan Nilekani, the Infosys co-founder who returned from the private sector to public service — was arguably the most important governance innovation in Indian history since Independence. By 2016, Aadhaar had enrolled over 1 billion Indians with unique 12-digit identification numbers linked to fingerprints and iris scans. The implications were profound: for the first time, the Indian state could direct benefits — subsidies, pensions, scholarships — directly to identified individuals, bypassing the cascade of intermediaries (local politicians, middlemen, corrupt officials) who had siphoned an estimated 85 paise of every development rupee before it reached its intended recipient. Aadhaar-linked direct benefit transfer would, in the following decade, save an estimated $33 billion in subsidy leakages.

Nilekani understood that he was not building an identity card. He was building the identity infrastructure — the authentication layer on which a future digital economy could run. The UPI payments system, which would later revolutionise Indian finance, would not have been possible without Aadhaar’s identity backbone.

The Corruption Catastrophe

And then the scandals came. Not one, but a cascade — each one larger and more devastating than the last, each one arriving before the political damage from the previous one had been absorbed.

The 2G spectrum scam (2008) was, by the Comptroller and Auditor General’s calculation, a loss to the exchequer of approximately ₹1.76 lakh crore ($39 billion) — the result of telecom minister A. Raja allocating mobile spectrum licences at 2001 prices to favoured companies rather than auctioning them at market rates. The scale was staggering; the brazenness was worse.

The Commonwealth Games corruption (2010) turned India’s moment of international hosting — the Delhi Commonwealth Games were intended to showcase India’s arrival as a world-class nation — into a display of procurement fraud, construction delays, and money siphoned at every level. The opening ceremony, when it arrived, was genuinely impressive; the months of revelations before it were a tutorial in how Indian state infrastructure projects were routinely looted.

Coalgate (2012) — the CAG’s report that coal blocks had been allocated to private companies without competitive bidding, at a calculated loss of ₹1.86 lakh crore — completed the picture. By 2012, India’s political conversation was dominated not by development but by corruption.

Manmohan Singh — personally incorruptible, professorial, decent — presided over this as a diminished figure. The central paradox of his Prime Ministership remains genuinely painful to engage with: how does a man of complete personal integrity preside over India’s most corrupt government in decades?

The answer involves the structure of coalition democracy. Manmohan Singh led a government that depended for its parliamentary majority on regional parties whose leaders were sometimes under criminal investigation themselves. Those allies held cabinet positions. Those cabinet positions controlled ministries running the allocations that were being looted. Singh had to choose, repeatedly, between preserving the coalition (and with it the government’s ability to pass any legislation) and removing ministers who were running corrupt fiefdoms. He consistently chose the coalition. Whether this was political wisdom or moral failure is a judgment each Indian must make individually.

The Anna Hazare Moment

On April 5, 2011, a 73-year-old Gandhian activist named Anna Hazare sat down in Jantar Mantar in New Delhi to fast unto death, demanding a strong Lokpal (anti-corruption ombudsman) bill. By the third day, the protest had gone national. City squares across India filled with young people — IT workers, students, office professionals — wearing white caps inscribed “I am Anna.” The movement was energised by text messages, social media, and the 24-hour news cycle in ways that no previous Indian protest movement had been.

The Anna Hazare movement was the Indian middle class finally finding its political voice after 20 years of economic growth. A generation that had prospered through liberalisation — that had worked hard, studied hard, earned legitimately — was furious at the system’s assumption that corruption was simply the price of doing business. They had been to Singapore and noticed the difference. They had read about South Korea’s anti-corruption prosecutions. They wanted an India where rules were applied equally and public power could not be sold without consequence.

The movement produced no immediate legislation of consequence — the Lokpal Bill passed in 2014 was a diluted version that few found satisfying. But it produced something more important: a political opening that a state-level politician from Gujarat was expertly positioned to walk through.

CHAPTER 8 — The Modi Era: Digital India, UPI, and the Aspiration Economy (2014–Present)

The Man from Vadnagar

Narendra Modi was born in 1950 in Vadnagar, a small town in Gujarat, to a lower-caste family — his father sold tea at the local railway station. He sold tea alongside his father as a boy. He left home young to join the RSS (Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh), the Hindu nationalist organisation, became a full-time pracharak (worker), rose through the organisation’s hierarchy, and was eventually parachuted into the BJP’s Gujarat state machine. He became Chief Minister of Gujarat in 2001 and governed the state for 13 years — building a reputation, contested but significant, for administrative drive and infrastructure delivery.

His 2014 general election campaign was unlike anything Indian democracy had seen. It was personal, digital, aspirational, and relentlessly focused on a single promise: that a new India was waiting to be built by a strong, decisive leader unconstrained by the Congress party’s coalition arithmetic and dynastic politics. He won with the BJP’s first outright majority in 30 years. He was re-elected in 2019 with an even larger majority.

He is the most polarising Prime Minister since Indira Gandhi — adored by hundreds of millions who see in him the first leader from their background to reach the top, and viewed with deep anxiety by minorities and liberals who see his Hindu nationalist political base as a threat to India’s constitutional pluralism. Evaluating his economic record requires separating these questions, which is harder than it sounds.

The JAM Trinity and Financial Inclusion

Modi’s most significant structural economic achievement is a trinity of programmes so elegantly interconnected that they deserve a single name: JAM — Jan Dhan bank accounts, Aadhaar biometric identity, Mobile phones.

The Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana (PMJDY), launched in August 2014, aimed to give every Indian household a bank account. By 2023, over 500 million accounts had been opened, including for approximately 450 million previously unbanked Indians. These were not token accounts — they came with debit cards, overdraft facilities, and crucially, the ability to receive government transfers directly.

Combined with Aadhaar (which the BJP government initially opposed but eventually embraced and expanded) and mobile connectivity, JAM created the infrastructure for India’s direct benefit transfer revolution. LPG cooking gas subsidies, once distributed through a byzantine system of middlemen who consumed an enormous portion of the benefit, were now credited directly to Aadhaar-linked bank accounts. Scholarship payments, pension disbursements, COVID relief — all went directly to identified individuals. The documented subsidy savings ran to billions of dollars annually.

The JAM Trinity was not Modi’s invention — Aadhaar was UPA’s, Jan Dhan scaled out what earlier financial inclusion programmes had begun. But the BJP government executed the convergence with institutional energy that its predecessors had not matched, and the results at scale were transformative.

UPI: India’s Greatest 21st-Century Achievement

If one had to choose a single achievement of the Indian government in the 21st century — economic, technological, institutional — the most defensible choice would be the Unified Payments Interface (UPI): a real-time digital payments system that has made India the world’s largest digital payments market by transaction volume.

UPI was built by the National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI), a quasi-governmental institution, designed on open architecture principles that allowed any bank and any payments application to plug in and interoperate. When a person pays a vegetable vendor in a Mumbai market by scanning a QR code with their phone, the payment crosses instantaneously from their account to the vendor’s — regardless of which banks or apps they each use, without intermediary fees.

The numbers are almost incomprehensible in scale: UPI processed over 10 billion transactions per month by late 2023, representing over $2 trillion in annual payment value. India — a country that was 90% cash in 2016 — had, within seven years, built a digital payments infrastructure that was more advanced, more inclusive, and more used than anything in Europe, the United States, or China (which has WeChat Pay and Alipay, powerful but proprietary, not open-architecture). Singapore’s Prime Minister publicly called India’s UPI the world’s most sophisticated digital payments system. The G20 India presidency showcased UPI as an exportable public infrastructure model for developing nations.

The irony is rich: demonetisation — the decision that was supposed to drive digital payments adoption — was a short-term catastrophe. But the digital payments infrastructure that existed before demonetisation, and that was expanded aggressively in its aftermath, is a genuine success story unconnected to demonetisation’s stated rationale.

Demonetisation: The Most Controversial Decision in Modern Indian History

On November 8, 2016, at 8 PM, Narendra Modi appeared on national television and announced that ₹500 and ₹1,000 currency notes — constituting 86% of India’s currency in circulation — were demonetised effective midnight. Citizens had 50 days to deposit old notes in banks. New notes would be issued progressively.

The stated objectives were: eliminate black money stored as unaccounted cash, break the currency-based funding of terrorism, and push India toward a digital economy. The preparation was, by multiple accounts, minimal. The banking system was not ready. Enough new notes had not been printed. ATMs were not calibrated for the new note sizes.

What followed was one of the most remarkable social scenes in post-Independence Indian history: serpentine queues outside banks and ATMs across India, millions of people spending their working days waiting to exchange their own money, daily withdrawal limits that made it impossible for ordinary people to access their own savings. Small businesses — which ran almost entirely on cash — ground to a halt. The informal economy, which employed the majority of India’s workers, contracted sharply. The harvesting season was disrupted because farmers could not pay daily-wage labourers in cash.

The Reserve Bank of India’s eventual accounting showed that approximately 99.3% of the demonetised currency was deposited — meaning that the black money “destroyed” through demonetisation was negligible, because those who had black money found ways to deposit it through various mechanisms. The stated purpose was largely unachieved. The short-term economic pain — the informal economy contracted by estimates ranging from 1–3% of GDP for the final quarter of 2016 — was real and borne disproportionately by the rural poor and by small and medium enterprises.

Modi’s supporters argue that demonetisation’s real success was forcing informal economic transactions into the banking system and accelerating digital payments adoption. There is some truth to this — digital payments did surge post-demonetisation. But the digital payments ecosystem existed before demonetisation and would have grown regardless; the question is whether the shock therapy was necessary and whether those who bore its costs had a choice. The answer to both questions is politically contested and empirically murky.

The GST: India’s Most Ambitious Tax Reform

The Goods and Services Tax, implemented July 1, 2017, unified 29 states and 7 union territories — each with their own sales taxes, octroi duties, and commercial levies — into a single national market for the first time in post-Independence history. A truck carrying goods from Chennai to Delhi no longer stopped at every state border to pay different taxes and present different documentation. The administrative cost of moving goods across India fell significantly.

The GST was the right reform — and its rollout was chaotic. Multiple tax slabs (zero, 5%, 12%, 18%, 28%) created complexity that small businesses found overwhelming. The online compliance portal crashed repeatedly in its early months. Exporters faced working capital crises because refund claims for the GST paid on inputs took months to process. Small traders who had never filed a tax return in their lives were suddenly required to file three returns per month.

Over time, these implementation problems have largely been resolved. GST revenues — a measure of formalisation of the economy — have grown consistently, reaching record monthly collections of over ₹1.7 lakh crore by late 2023. The formalization of the economy and the expansion of the tax base are genuine achievements.

Startups, Unicorns, and the Innovation Economy

India went from near-zero technology startups of consequence in 2010 to over 100 unicorn companies (startups valued above $1 billion) by 2022 — second only to the United States and China. Flipkart (e-commerce, acquired by Walmart for $16 billion), Ola (ride-sharing), Zomato (food delivery), Razorpay (payments), CRED (fintech), Zepto (quick commerce), and dozens of others built businesses at scale within the Indian market and increasingly internationally.

The startup ecosystem grew for reasons that government policy partly enabled — the Startup India initiative, simplified company registration, digital identity infrastructure — and that the market partly created: 700 million smartphone users, UPI-enabled digital payments, a young English-speaking population hungry for digital services, and venture capital money seeking the last large untapped consumer market on Earth.

The ecosystem produced genuine innovations: Zepto’s 10-minute grocery delivery model became a global benchmark for quick commerce. PhonePe and Paytm built financial super-apps that rivalled anything in the world. BYJU’s — India’s edtech phenomenon — briefly became the world’s most valuable edtech company before a spectacular governance collapse reminded everyone that valuation and sustainable business are not the same thing.

CHAPTER 9 — The Human Story: What Growth Did and Did Not Do

The Achievements Are Real

Begin with what is true and important: India’s development record since 1991 is genuine, substantial, and life-altering.

415 million Indians were lifted out of extreme poverty between 1991 and 2021 — the largest reduction in absolute poverty in any country in that period, in absolute numbers exceeding China’s record. Life expectancy rose from 32 years at Independence to over 70 today — a 38-year addition to the average Indian life, representing the accumulated impact of nutrition improvement, disease control, vaccination, and access to medicine. Literacy rose from 16% at Independence to over 77% today. Infant mortality fell from about 180 per 1,000 live births in 1950 to under 30 by 2022. Maternal mortality has fallen dramatically. The spread of mobile telephony — 700 million smartphone users — has connectd more Indians to more information, more commerce, and more community than any previous technology.

The middle class that emerged from liberalization — estimated at 300–400 million people by various measures — is the most aspirational and educated mass consumer population in the world. Their children are more educated, more travelled, and more globally connected than any previous Indian generation. This is not nothing. This is the material of civilizational revival.

Agriculture: The Left-Behind Majority

Sixty percent of India’s population depends on agriculture or agriculture-related activities for its livelihood. Agriculture contributes approximately 17% of GDP. The arithmetic of this gap — 60% of the people generating 17% of the output — is the central structural challenge of India’s development story, and it has not been resolved.

Indian farmers face a compound crisis. Landholdings have fragmented through inheritance into parcels too small for efficient mechanised cultivation. Water tables in Punjab and Haryana — where the Green Revolution was most intense — are collapsing, the product of decades of subsidised electricity powering unsustainable groundwater extraction. Climate variability — irregular monsoons, heat stress during critical crop growth periods — is increasing yield volatility as climate change intensifies. Minimum Support Prices — the government’s crop-price floors — protect some politically powerful crops (wheat, rice) while leaving vegetables, pulses, and oilseeds to market volatility.

The farmer suicide crisis is India’s most morally urgent unresolved problem. Over 300,000 farmers have died by suicide since 1995 — typically indebted men whose crop failure has made their debt unpayable, who see no path forward. Each death is a specific, preventable tragedy, and the scale of them over three decades constitutes a systemic failure that has been observed, documented, studied, reported, and not resolved.

The three Farm Laws controversy of 2020–2021 — in which the Modi government passed legislation intended to liberalise agricultural markets, faced massive protests from Punjab and Haryana farmers (who feared losing MSP protections and being exposed to large corporate buyers), and then, in January 2022, withdrew the laws completely — illustrated both the urgency of agricultural reform and the political impossibility of delivering it without genuine farmer consent.

The Manufacturing Gap

India’s manufacturing sector contributes approximately 16–17% of GDP — roughly the same as two decades ago. In China, manufacturing’s share of GDP peaked at over 32%. In South Korea, over 30%. In India, it has not risen. This means that India has not had its factory moment — the mass employment of rural workers in labour-intensive manufacturing that was the transformative stage of every successful Asian development story, the stage that took people from fields to factory floors and created the consumer working class that becomes the domestic market for the next stage of development.

The reasons are structural and well-understood, if not yet resolved. India’s labour laws — particularly the Industrial Disputes Act, which made it legally near-impossible to fire workers in formal sector establishments above a certain size — created a powerful disincentive to formal manufacturing employment. Companies responded rationally: they kept operations below the size thresholds that triggered labour protections, subcontracted to informal operators, or invested in capital equipment rather than labour. The result was a formal manufacturing sector that was neither particularly large nor particularly labour-intensive.

India’s infrastructure constraints — reliable power supply, road quality, port turnaround time, logistics costs — raised manufacturing costs significantly above Chinese, Vietnamese, and Bangladeshi competitors. A factory in Shenzhen could produce and ship goods faster at lower total cost than a factory in Pune, even if Pune’s labour costs were similar.

The Production Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme — introduced from 2020, offering cash incentives to companies that increase manufacturing output in specified sectors — is the Modi government’s primary tool for addressing this gap. Early results in smartphones (Apple, Samsung, and their suppliers have expanded manufacturing in India significantly) and pharmaceuticals (where India was already strong) are encouraging. The crucial test is whether PLI success in electronics can catalyse the broader manufacturing ecosystem — supply chains, ancillary industries, skilled labour pools — that China built over three decades. That test is still running.

The Education Crisis Within the Education Expansion

India has achieved near-universal school enrolment — a genuine achievement given where it started. But enrolment without learning is attendance without education, and India’s learning crisis is severe.

The Annual Status of Education Report (ASER) — conducted by the NGO Pratham — has documented for two decades a reality that contradicts the enrolment numbers: approximately 50% of children in Class 5 cannot read a Class 2 text. Children are enrolled; they are not learning. The proximate causes are well-documented: teacher absenteeism (studies show 25–30% of government school teachers absent on any given day), inadequate school infrastructure, rote-learning pedagogy that tests memorisation rather than comprehension, and a curriculum poorly aligned with foundational skill-building.

This is not a minor problem. It is an existential challenge to India’s demographic dividend thesis. India will have the world’s largest youth population through 2040 — a potential labour force advantage that could power decades of growth. But a demographic dividend requires that the youth in question are educated and skilled enough to be productively employed. A generation that cannot read at grade level cannot be absorbed into a modern manufacturing economy, cannot work in digital services, and cannot repay the public investment in their schooling.

Gender: The Missing Half

India’s female labour force participation rate stands at approximately 22–24% — lower than Pakistan’s, lower than Bangladesh’s, lower than most countries at comparable income levels, and far below what economic models predict for a country at India’s stage of development.

The reasons are layered. Patriarchal social norms in many regions restrict women’s mobility and define paid employment outside the home as inappropriate or unsafe. Safety concerns — rates of sexual harassment and violence against women that are inadequately addressed by law enforcement — make working outside the home genuinely dangerous in many contexts. The withdrawal of women from the agricultural workforce (as rising family incomes make it socially prestigious for women to stop working) has not been replaced by entry into the formal economy.

The economic cost of this exclusion is estimated at 1.5–2 percentage points of annual GDP growth — meaning that India’s economy is growing several percentage points below its potential because half its population is excluded from full economic participation. More importantly, the human cost — of women who are educated but economically dependent, whose capabilities are unused, whose ambitions are constrained by gender boundaries — is incalculable and undiscussed.

Caste and Economic Growth

India’s caste system — the ancient social hierarchy that assigned hereditary status, occupation, and social standing from birth — intersects with economic growth in complex ways that resist simple summary.

At one level, economic growth and the spread of education have weakened caste’s occupational determinism. Graduates of IIT, irrespective of caste, find employment in a market that cares primarily about their technical skills. Urban, educated India is measurably less caste-determined in its daily social interactions than the India of fifty years ago.

But at the level of wealth and asset ownership, economic growth has in many cases reinforced rather than disrupted caste hierarchy. The upper-caste families who owned land, had access to education, and held political office under colonial and early post-colonial India were better positioned to capture the gains of liberalization than the Dalits and Adivasis whose inherited disadvantages accumulated over centuries. The IT industry, despite its meritocratic self-image, is disproportionately populated by upper-caste graduates — not because of deliberate discrimination but because higher-caste families had better access to the English-medium schools and coaching programmes that led to IIT admission.

Reservations — India’s affirmative action system — have created a Dalit and OBC professional and political class of consequence, and this is a genuine achievement of Indian democracy. But reservations in government employment and education, whatever their political importance, address a fraction of the economy. The private sector is largely unreserved. And it is in private sector enterprise — where most of India’s growth has been created since 1991 — that historical disadvantage continues to compound.

CHAPTER 10 — The Key Figures Gallery: Architects of Modern India

Jawaharlal Nehru (1889–1964)

The visionary who built the foundations and the prison. The IITs, the dams, the secular democratic Constitution, the Planning Commission — these are Nehru’s gifts, and they are real. The License Raj, the import-substitution walls, the centralised state ownership of industries that should have competed — these are his failures, equally real. He was the man who read the world deeply and his own country sometimes less so. History’s verdict: indispensable and insufficient.

Indira Gandhi (1917–1984)

India’s most powerful Prime Minister in domestic political terms. She won a war, nationalized banks, declared Emergency, reversed Emergency’s defeat, won again. She was capable of extraordinary vision (Green Revolution) and extraordinary abuse (Emergency). She died as she had governed: by violence, at the hands of proximate consequence of her own decisions. The communal pogrom after her death was the shadow that fell from her political choices. History’s verdict: magnificent and dangerous.

M.S. Swaminathan (1925–2023)

The agronomist who fed a billion Indians and then spent the rest of his life warning that the chemicals that saved India were poisoning its future. He was perhaps the most consistent in his moral reckoning of any figure in this report — the man who understood both what the Green Revolution achieved and what it was costing in soil health and groundwater. The “Father of India’s Green Revolution” who then called for an “Evergreen Revolution” of sustainable agriculture. History’s verdict: the most genuinely prescient development thinker in Indian history.

Dhirubhai Ambani (1932–2002)

The son of a village schoolteacher from Gujarat who became India’s greatest capitalist. Dhirubhai understood, with relentless clarity, that the License Raj was a system of rules that could be navigated as well as followed — and he navigated it with genius. He founded Reliance Industries on a borrowed capital basis, built it into India’s largest company, and gave the Indian middle class its first mass equity investment experience through the Reliance share offering of 1977. He was not uncorrupted by the system he navigated; his methods were often ethically complex. But he proved that Indian entrepreneurship could build at global scale, before liberalisation made it easier. History’s verdict: the capitalist who showed India capitalism was possible, even inside a socialist system.

P.V. Narasimha Rao (1921–2004)

The forgotten liberator. The Prime Minister who had the most consequential economic impact on India since Nehru, and has the least recognition to show for it. He used a crisis to accomplish what no crisis-free environment would have permitted. He was willing to be unpopular, to be underestimated, and to let Manmohan Singh take the credit publicly while he managed the political protection from behind. His legacy deserves rehabilitation. History’s verdict: India’s most underrated Prime Minister, perhaps its most pragmatic one.

Manmohan Singh (1932–2024)

The quiet revolutionary who changed India with a budget speech and a Victor Hugo quote. A man who spent his career as the most technically excellent economist in whichever room he was in, and who was — by the accident of a crisis and a PM’s trust — positioned to use that excellence at the decisive moment. His legacy in 1991 is secure. His legacy as Prime Minister is more contested: the architect of liberalisation presiding over coalition’s corruption. History’s verdict: the man who made modern India possible, and who was ultimately made smaller by the politics of governing it.

Narayana Murthy (born 1946)

The $250 startup founder who gave India its global software identity and its first visible model of transparent, ethical corporate governance. He demonstrated that you could build a world-class company in India without paying a bribe, without relatives in the government, without operating in the grey zones of regulatory ambiguity. That demonstration was as important as the company. History’s verdict: the man who proved that Indian ethical capitalism was possible.

Ratan Tata (1937–2024)

The gentleman industrialist who took India’s oldest conglomerate to the world stage. He was the face of what Indian business looked like when it behaved with personal integrity at the top: no scandal, no political proximity that compromised judgment, no exploitation of the system for personal enrichment. His great acquisitions — Jaguar Land Rover, Corus Steel, Tetley Tea — announced something important about where Indian confidence had arrived. His personally small lifestyle in a country where the rich often live large made him a figure of unusual moral authority. History’s verdict: India’s most admired industrialist because he made no concessions to admiration.

Nandan Nilekani (born 1955)

The Infosys co-founder who built Aadhaar and gave India its digital identity spine — arguable the most consequential single infrastructure project in India since the Green Revolution. He returned from private sector wealth to public service without salary or political ambition. He designed with an engineer’s precision and a systems thinker’s scope. The UPI ecosystem he helped architect has made India a global benchmark for financial infrastructure innovation. History’s verdict: the person who most successfully transferred private sector technology thinking into government infrastructure design.

Narendra Modi (born 1950)

The tea seller’s son who became India’s most powerful leader since Indira Gandhi, with a legacy still being written. His achievements — JAM Trinity, UPI, digital public infrastructure, PLI manufacturing push, COVID vaccination scale, India’s role in the G20 — are real and consequential. His failures and contradictions — demonetisation’s costs, the COVID-19 second wave’s devastation, the concerns about democratic backsliding and minority rights — are equally real and cannot be wished away. He is the first Prime Minister from a lower-caste background, and that matters symbolically in a country where social hierarchy has determined access to power for millennia. History’s verdict: too early for a verdict. Check back in 2040.

Sam Pitroda (born 1942)

The telecom visionary who planted the seed of India’s digital future in the 1980s, during a political moment that could easily have ignored him. An NRI engineer who returned to advise Rajiv Gandhi, built the Centre for Development of Telematics (C-DOT), rolled out digital telephone exchanges, launched the STD booth revolution, and conceptualised the National Knowledge Commission. He is the most underappreciated figure in India’s technology story. History’s verdict: the man who arrived 10 years before India was ready for him — and won anyway.

CHAPTER 11 — The Unfinished Republic: India’s Challenges and the Road to a $10 Trillion Economy

The Demographic Dividend and Its Deadline

India has the world’s largest youth population: approximately 600 million people under 25, adding roughly 10 million new workers to the labour force every year. This is, in theory, the greatest economic opportunity any country has ever had — a demographic gift that no other country will experience at this scale again. For the next 15–20 years, India’s working-age population will be growing while China’s, Japan’s, Korea’s, and Europe’s shrink.

The demographic dividend is not automatic. It is conditional. It becomes a dividend only if the young people entering the labour market are healthy, educated, and productively employed. The education quality crisis, discussed in Chapter 9, is the primary threat to this reality. A generation that cannot read at grade level, that lacks vocational skills, that enters a labour market with no formal jobs to absorb it — is not a demographic dividend. It is a demographic crisis waiting to ignite.

The window is real and it is closing. By 2040, India too will begin aging — the fertility rate has fallen below replacement level in most southern states and is falling nationally. What India does with its demographic advantage in the next fifteen years will determine its economic trajectory for the next half century. The pressure is enormous and the time is short.

The Manufacturing Imperative

India cannot afford to skip manufacturing. A services economy alone — no matter how sophisticated — cannot productively employ the tens of millions of low-skilled workers who annually leave agriculture. Software and finance employ the top 5% of the educational distribution. The remaining 95% need jobs in manufacturing, construction, and services like retail and hospitality — jobs that require some skill but not an IIT degree, jobs that pay enough better than agricultural labour to make the transition worthwhile.

The PLI scheme is a genuine attempt, and its early results in smartphones, pharmaceuticals, and specialty chemicals are real. But the deeper reforms that manufacturing requires — flexible labour laws that allow companies to hire and fire based on business needs, land acquisition processes that can deliver clear title quickly, reliable infrastructure, integrated logistics networks — are politically difficult and administratively complex in ways that check-writing schemes like PLI cannot substitute for.

India is not going to replicate China’s 30-year manufacturing build-out. But it does not need to. What it needs is enough manufacturing growth to absorb its surplus agricultural labour and create the broad-based income gains that have eluded the IT-era. The China+1 strategy — global companies diversifying supply chains away from China — creates a genuine opening that India is better positioned than any other country to capture: the scale, the English language competence, the democratic rule of law, the existing business ecosystem.

The question is whether India’s implementation machinery can execute at the speed that global business requires. That question is unanswered.

The Federalism Challenge

India’s growth story is wildly, almost embarrassingly uneven. Tamil Nadu’s per capita income is approximately $3,500 — approaching lower-middle income status by global measures. Karnataka hosts the world’s second-largest IT industry. Maharashtra’s economy, if independent, would be in the top 30 in the world. Gujarat’s manufacturing ecosystem has produced some of India’s most competitive export industries.

And then: Bihar, with a per capita income of approximately $750, among the lowest in the world. Uttar Pradesh — with 240 million people, the world’s most populous sub-national unit — remains deeply poor, with institutional capacity, infrastructure, and educational outcomes far below the national average. Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, and parts of Odisha have Adivasi populations living in conditions more comparable to sub-Saharan Africa than to Bengaluru’s gleaming tech parks.

India is not one country at one development stage. It is many countries at many stages, constitutionally required to act as one. The fiscal transfer system that moves money from richer states to poorer ones — the Finance Commission’s devolution formula — creates political resentment in prosperous Tamil Nadu and Karnataka, whose citizens argue they are subsidising states whose governance failures they did not cause. The politics of federalism and development equity will intensify as India’s richest states increasingly resemble developed-country economies while its poorest remain in conditions the rest of the country has left behind.

The Democracy-Development Tension

India’s democracy creates genuine constraints on development speed. Acquiring land for infrastructure projects requires consent processes, public hearings, and legal challenges that take years — the same project in China could be executed in months. Labour reforms that economists broadly agree would accelerate manufacturing employment are politically impossible when large trade unions can mobilise electoral consequences. Electricity pricing reform — needed to put state electricity distribution companies on sustainable financial footing — is impossible in states where subsidised electricity is a de facto transfer payment to the rural poor.

Is this a bug or a feature? The honest answer is: both, depending on which costs you’re willing to bear. India’s democratic constraints mean development is slower, messier, and more contested than China’s — but it also means that development decisions must ultimately command broader consent, that losers have recourse, and that growth does not come at the cost of the rule of law or political rights. The Mumbai infrastructure that requires consent processes is slower than the Beijing infrastructure that doesn’t — but it does not require displacement of people without legal recourse or compensation.

The democracy-development tension is real. It is also, for a country of India’s size and diversity, possibly the only sustainable path. An authoritarian India would face a different kind of instability: ethnic, linguistic, regional tensions that shared democratic institutions currently manage imperfectly but manage.

China’s Shadow

The world’s two most populous nations share one of the world’s most disputed borders and represent two fundamentally different models of development. China chose authoritarian market capitalism, delivered extraordinary manufacturing growth, built the world’s largest middle class within a single generation, and maintains political control through a system that tolerates no meaningful opposition. India chose democratic market capitalism, delivered slower and more uneven growth, preserved individual and political rights, and maintains political legitimacy through universal franchise elections that have changed governments multiple times.

The comparison is lived by India’s political class as a wound. India’s GDP — approximately $3.7 trillion as of 2024 — is one-fifth of China’s. India’s manufacturing sector is one-tenth China’s size. India’s infrastructure spending per year is a fraction of China’s. Every major development indicator shows China ahead — sometimes dramatically, sometimes only modestly.

And yet: China’s demographic challenge (the consequences of the One Child Policy are now accelerating population aging with alarming speed), its governance brittleness (no mechanism for peaceful political transition, all error-correction running through the same single-party apparatus that made the errors), its middle-income trap risk (manufacturing wages rising faster than productivity in many sectors), and its strategic isolation (the US and European decoupling accelerated by COVID-19 and Taiwan tensions) create vulnerabilities that India’s democratic, open model does not share.

The 21st century will test which model is more durable over a full civilisational time horizon. The 20th century’s verdict favoured democratic market economies over authoritarian command economies. China’s model is different from Soviet command economies. India’s model is different from the US or European models. The test is not yet complete.

The Soft Power Emergence

India’s cultural presence in the world is growing rapidly and in ways that extend well beyond Bollywood’s historic reach in the Indian diaspora and across the developing world.

Indian cinema — not just Hindi but Tamil, Telugu, Malayalam, and Kannada language films — has found global audiences with growing speed. RRR by S.S. Rajamouli won the Academy Award for Best Original Song in 2023, and its global streaming success on Netflix was followed by a recognition among international audiences that Indian cinema had depth and diversity they had not previously known. The Tamil film industry (Kollywood) creates films that out-perform Bollywood in some overseas markets. The Malayalam film industry produces some of the world’s most critically acclaimed intimate cinema.

The 35 million-strong Indian diaspora — the largest diaspora of any nation — has produced, in the past decade, a striking concentration of global leadership. CEOs of Google (Sundar Pichai), Microsoft (Satya Nadella), IBM, Pepsico, Adobe, Mastercard, FedEx — an Indian-origin person helms an extraordinary proportion of the world’s largest companies. US Vice President Kamala Harris is of Indian origin. UK Prime Minister Rishi Sunak is of Indian origin. Five Indian-origin billionaires appear in the 2024 Forbes global top 50.

This diaspora is not just evidence of individual achievement. It is evidence of the institutional quality — IITs, IIMs, the colonial-era legal and educational system, the competitive Indian educational environment — that India’s development story has produced, even imperfectly.

Yoga, Ayurveda, Indian cuisine, and Indian philosophy command growing global interest — not as curiosities but as substantive contributions to how people across the world think about health, food, and meaning. India’s soft power emergence is real, if less dramatically recognizable than Korea’s Hallyu Wave.

The Philosophical Reflection: India’s Unfinishable Story

End, as we must, with the question India’s story keeps asking: what kind of country is India becoming, and at what cost to the people who least chose to bear that cost?

India’s greatest achievement since 1947 is not economic — it is constitutional. The decision to build a democratic republic under a Constitution that guaranteed universal suffrage, fundamental rights, and rule of law, in 1950, for a country of 350 million people with 84% illiteracy and no democratic tradition, was a decision of audacious faith. It has not always been honoured. The Emergency happened. Communal violence has recurred. Democratic backsliding has been documented and contested. But the Constitution has survived, elections have remained meaningful and competitive, governments have changed, and courts have functioned with imperfect but real independence.

The economic story is that of a civilization slowly recovering its earlier proportion of global wealth — not by the same mechanisms that built colonial prosperity, but by the harder work of institutional construction and human capital investment. India’s share of global GDP, which fell from 24% in 1700 to 3% in 1991, has climbed back to approximately 7–8% by 2024 and continues to rise. The trajectory is clear. The pace is contested.

What makes India’s story ultimately different from Singapore’s, South Korea’s, and China’s is that it cannot be told as the story of a single decision, a single leader, a single strategy. It is a story of a civilization that is too large, too diverse, too democratic, and too ancient to be simplified. Every Indian who takes an auto-rickshaw past a gleaming tech park on a potholed road is living the complexity daily: the world-class and the broken, the aspiration and the frustration, in the same field of vision.

India’s miracle, when it fully arrives, will be entirely its own — messy, loud, contradictory, and extraordinary. It will not look like Singapore’s pristine efficiency or South Korea’s industrial intensity. It will look like India: 1.4 billion people arguing, creating, failing, reforming, and building — at full democratic volume, in 22 official languages, across seven time zones, simultaneously.

The question is not whether India will rise. The trajectory of the last 30 years, despite all its unevenness, makes the direction clear. The question is when, and at what cost to the farmers in debt distress and the first-generation learners in under-resourced schools and the women whose capabilities the economy has not yet found a way to include.

Those questions are ultimately answered not by Prime Ministers or Finance Ministers but by the daily decisions of 1.4 billion people who are, in their own ways, the authors of India’s unfinished story.
